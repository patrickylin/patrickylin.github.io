Our Foundation

What we believe, together with the Church across time and place.
A note on the name: "Reformed" names a theological tradition — God's sovereignty, covenant, and the convictions of the Reformation — not a church currently undergoing reform.
THE FOUNDATION

Scripture

The Old and New Testaments — God's Word, and the final authority for what we believe and how we live.

Reformed theology holds to organic inspiration: the Spirit worked through each author's own voice, style, and setting, so Scripture is fully God's Word and fully human writing at once. And Scripture is infallible — completely trustworthy for faith and life — even where precise historical or scientific detail was never its purpose.

SHARED WITH THE WHOLE CHURCH

The Ecumenical Creeds

Three creeds adopted by the worldwide church centuries ago, summarizing the most important truths of the Christian faith — held in common with Christians everywhere.

Apostles' Creed +
Background

Not written by the apostles themselves, but developed from the Church's ancient baptismal confessions, reaching its familiar form by around the 8th century. It summarizes the apostles' teaching in a form new believers could memorize and confess at their baptism.

The Creed
I believe in God, the Father almighty, creator of heaven and earth.
I believe in Jesus Christ, his only Son, our Lord, who was conceived by the Holy Spirit and born of the virgin Mary. He suffered under Pontius Pilate, was crucified, died, and was buried; he descended to hell. The third day he rose again from the dead. He ascended to heaven and is seated at the right hand of God the Father almighty. From there he will come to judge the living and the dead.
I believe in the Holy Spirit, the holy catholic* church, the communion of saints, the forgiveness of sins, the resurrection of the body, and the life everlasting. Amen.

*that is, the true Christian church of all times and all places

Nicene Creed +
Background

Formulated at the Council of Nicaea (325 AD) and completed at the Council of Constantinople (381 AD), in response to Arianism — the teaching that Jesus Christ was a created being, not truly and eternally God. Its central phrase, "of the same essence as the Father," was chosen specifically to rule that view out.

The Creed
We believe in one God, the Father almighty, maker of heaven and earth, of all things visible and invisible.
And in one Lord Jesus Christ, the only Son of God, begotten from the Father before all ages, God from God, Light from Light, true God from true God, begotten, not made; of the same essence as the Father. Through him all things were made. For us and for our salvation he came down from heaven; he became incarnate by the Holy Spirit and the virgin Mary, and was made human. He was crucified for us under Pontius Pilate; he suffered and was buried. The third day he rose again, according to the Scriptures. He ascended to heaven and is seated at the right hand of the Father. He will come again with glory to judge the living and the dead. His kingdom will never end.
And we believe in the Holy Spirit, the Lord, the giver of life. He proceeds from the Father and the Son, and with the Father and the Son is worshiped and glorified. He spoke through the prophets. We believe in one holy catholic and apostolic church. We affirm one baptism for the forgiveness of sins. We look forward to the resurrection of the dead, and to life in the world to come. Amen.
Athanasian Creed +
Background

Traditionally associated with Athanasius, the 4th-century bishop who defended Christ's full divinity against Arianism, though it was likely composed somewhat later. It is the most detailed of the three creeds, spelling out with technical precision both the doctrine of the Trinity and the two natures of Christ — written to close off several specific ways these truths were being misstated in the early church.

The Creed, Part One: The Trinity
Whoever desires to be saved should above all hold to the catholic faith. Anyone who does not keep it whole and unbroken will doubtless perish eternally. Now this is the catholic faith: that we worship one God in trinity and the trinity in unity, neither blending their persons nor dividing their essence. For the person of the Father is a distinct person, the person of the Son is another, and that of the Holy Spirit still another. But the divinity of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit is one, their glory equal, their majesty coeternal.
What quality the Father has, the Son has, and the Holy Spirit has: uncreated, immeasurable, eternal — and yet there are not three eternal beings, but one eternal being. So too the Father is almighty, the Son is almighty, the Holy Spirit is almighty — yet there are not three almighty beings, but one almighty being. Thus the Father is God, the Son is God, the Holy Spirit is God — yet there are not three gods, but one God. The Father was neither made nor created nor begotten from anyone. The Son was neither made nor created; he was begotten from the Father alone. The Holy Spirit was neither made nor created nor begotten; he proceeds from the Father and the Son. Nothing in this trinity is before or after, nothing is greater or smaller; the three persons are coeternal and coequal with each other.
The Creed, Part Two: Christ, God and Man
It is necessary for eternal salvation that one also believe faithfully in the incarnation of our Lord Jesus Christ. Now this is the true faith: that our Lord Jesus Christ, God's Son, is both God and human, equally. He is God from the essence of the Father, begotten before time; and he is human from the essence of his mother, born in time; completely God, completely human. Although he is God and human, Christ is not two, but one — not by his divinity being turned into flesh, but by God's taking humanity to himself; not by a blending of essence, but by the unity of his person.
He suffered for our salvation; he descended to hell; he arose from the dead; he ascended to heaven; he is seated at the Father's right hand; from there he will come to judge the living and the dead. At his coming all people will arise bodily and give an accounting of their own deeds. Those who have done good will enter eternal life, and those who have done evil will enter eternal fire. This is the catholic faith: one cannot be saved without believing it firmly and faithfully.
OUR REFORMED ACCENT

The Reformed Confessions

Historic confessions that articulate a Reformed understanding of that same biblical faith — not a different faith, but a particular, careful expression of it.

Belgic Confession +
Background

Written in 1561 by Guido de Brès, a Reformed pastor in the Netherlands, during a time of severe persecution — intended to explain the Reformed faith to both the government and the Roman Catholic Church.

Structure

37 articles covering the nature of God, the Trinity, creation, the fall, salvation, the church, and the sacraments.

Emphases

The continuity of Reformed belief with the ancient Christian creeds, the authority of Scripture, justification by faith, and the marks of a true church.

Heidelberg Catechism +
Background

Written in 1563 by Zacharias Ursinus and Caspar Olevianus in Heidelberg, Germany, as a teaching tool for instructing young people in the faith — commissioned by Elector Frederick III.

Structure

129 questions and answers in three parts — Guilt, Grace, and Gratitude — divided into 52 Lord's Days for a year of weekly teaching.

Emphases

A warm, personal tone; the comfort and assurance of belonging to Christ; and the practical, grateful shape of the Christian life.

Canons of Dort +
Background

Formulated by the Synod of Dort (1618–1619) in response to the Arminian controversy — a defense of the Reformed doctrines of grace against objections raised by followers of Jacobus Arminius.

Structure

Five main points, often remembered by the acronym TULIP — Total Depravity, Unconditional Election, Limited Atonement, Irresistible Grace, and Perseverance of the Saints.

Emphases

God's sovereignty in salvation from beginning to end, and the settled assurance believers can have that God preserves what he starts.

Together known as the Three Forms of Unity.
STEP TWO

Our Reformed Accent

Same faith, same family — a particular way of speaking it.

Most of what Reformed Christians believe, we hold in common with the whole Church. But every tradition speaks the same faith with its own emphasis — the way an English speaker from Toronto and one from Johannesburg share a language but sound different. Three words carry much of the Reformed emphasis: sovereignty, covenant, and kingdom.

GOD'S PLAN, GOD'S POWER

Sovereignty

Reformed Christians hold a high view of God's rule over everything — from the rise and fall of nations to the faith of one person. Because sin has touched even the human will, salvation is God's initiative from beginning to end; we are called into relationship with God, and respond in gratitude, rather than salvation depending on our own power to choose.

GOD KEEPS HIS PROMISES

Covenant

God is a promise-making, promise-keeping God, and his promises are not only to individuals but to families across generations — the same pattern seen with Israel and echoed at Pentecost, when Peter promised the Spirit "to you and your children." This is why Reformed churches baptize both believing adults and the infant children of believing parents: a sign that God's grace comes before we can earn or even understand it.

Acts 2:39
EVERY SQUARE INCH

Kingdom

God's kingdom is not a building or a strictly "religious" zone — it is God's reign extending over all of creation: work, art, family, and public life included. Reformed Christians resist splitting life into "sacred" and "secular," believing every calling can be carried out for God's glory.

“There is not a square inch in the whole domain of our human existence over which Christ, who is Sovereign over all, does not cry: 'Mine!'” — Abraham Kuyper, 1880
Adapted from the CRCNA's "Reformed Accent" essay.

STEP FIVE

The Sacraments

Two signs and seals of the covenant of grace.
💧
A SIGN FOR BELIEVERS AND THEIR CHILDREN

Baptism

Baptism marks a person's entry into God's covenant community, much as circumcision once marked God's covenant with Israel. This is why Reformed churches baptize believers who profess their faith, and also the infant children of believing parents — trusting God's promise that his covenant extends to "you and your children."

Gen. 17:10–12; Acts 2:39
🍞
MORE THAN A MEMORIAL

The Lord's Supper

The Lord's Supper is not simply an act of remembering — it is a means of grace, a true participation in Christ, and a renewal of his covenant with us each time it's shared. This is why Reformed churches celebrate communion often rather than occasionally: every table is a fresh proclamation of the gospel until Christ returns.

1 Cor. 10:16; 1 Cor. 11:26

Honest Questions

+

Questions elders are actually asked — answered plainly, without dodging the hard parts.

If God is sovereign, why do natural disasters and tragedies happen?
Reformed theology holds God's sovereignty and goodness together, even when suffering remains mysterious to us. Scripture doesn't promise an explanation for every tragedy — but it does promise that God can bring good out of suffering, and that in Christ, suffering itself will one day end.Rom. 8:28; Rev. 21:4
Does prayer actually change anything, if God's plan doesn't change?
God is unchanging, but he has ordained prayer itself as one of the means through which he carries out his plan. Praying doesn't move God from one plan to another — it's how he has chosen to involve his people in the plan he already has.James 5:16
Do Reformed churches pay enough attention to the Holy Spirit?
It's a fair challenge, and Reformed churches have sometimes earned it — a strong focus on doctrine can crowd out a lived sense of the Spirit's presence. But the Spirit is fully God, at work in every believer from regeneration through sanctification, and the Heidelberg Catechism itself teaches this at length. The goal isn't less doctrine — it's doctrine and daily dependence on the Spirit, held together.2 Thess. 2:13; Heidelberg Catechism Q&A 53
What about the rapture — will believers be taken up before a time of tribulation?
Reformed theology generally does not hold to a pre-tribulation rapture. Instead, it looks forward to a single, visible return of Christ, the resurrection of the dead, and the full renewal of creation — not an escape from the world, but its restoration.1 Thess. 4:16–17; Rev. 21:1–4
