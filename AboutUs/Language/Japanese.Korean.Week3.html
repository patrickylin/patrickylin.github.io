Week 3 · Katakana — your shortcut to hundreds of words
Big picture
Why katakana is the most fun script to learn

Katakana has the exact same 46 sounds as hiragana — you already know the phonetics. The only thing new is the visual shape of each character. And the payoff is huge: katakana is used to write foreign loanwords, which in Japan often come from English. Once you know katakana, you can read words like:

コーヒー
kōhī
Coffee
ホテル
hoteru
Hotel
タクシー
takushī
Taxi
レストラン
resutoran
Restaurant
The trick: Katakana is essentially a "squarer", more angular version of hiragana. If you know hiragana, you'll find many katakana characters feel visually related. Focus on the differences.

Common mix-ups to watch: ン (n) vs ソ (so) vs リ (ri) — and シ (shi) vs ツ (tsu). These pairs look deceptively similar. ン and ソ differ by stroke direction; シ and ツ differ by whether the small strokes are vertical or horizontal. Take extra time on these.
Lesson 3 · The trickiest lookalike pairs
4 pairs that fool everyone — study these carefully
シ vs ツ
シ = shi — small strokes on the LEFT side, nearly horizontal
ツ = tsu — small strokes on the TOP, more vertical
ン vs ソ
ン = n — starts with a small stroke pointing RIGHT, main stroke curves right
ソ = so — starts with a small stroke pointing DOWN, main stroke is sharper
ア vs マ
ア = a — the horizontal bar doesn't cross the vertical
マ = ma — the bar crosses through, with a hook at the bottom
ロ vs 口
ロ = ro — katakana, slightly rectangular
口 = mouth (kanji) — a kanji character meaning "mouth". Looks almost identical — context tells you which one!

Culture note
🏪
コンビニ (konbini) — Japan's convenience store culture
Japanese convenience stores (7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Lawson) are unlike anything in the West. They're open 24/7 and sell hot food, fresh sushi, ATMs, event tickets, and even pay your bills. As a visitor, the konbini is your best friend — and now you can read the sign in katakana!

Korean — 한국어
Week 3 · Final consonants (받침) + your first full sentences
Big picture
받침 (batchim) — the final consonant at the bottom of a block

So far all your Hangul blocks have been consonant + vowel (e.g. 가, 나, 도). But many Korean syllables have a third component — a final consonant sitting at the bottom of the block. This is called 받침 (batchim).

No batchim
가
ga
→
With batchim
강
gang (river)
ㄱ + ㅏ = 가 (ga)
Add ㅇ at the bottom:
ㄱ + ㅏ + ㅇ = 강 (gang)
Good news: You don't need to learn new letters for batchim — it uses the same consonants you already know. You're just placing them at the bottom of the block.
Lesson 1 · The 7 representative batchim sounds
All final consonants reduce to one of just 7 sounds

Even though many consonants can appear as batchim, they all reduce to 7 actual sounds when pronounced. This makes it manageable.

Consonant Batchim sound Example word Meaning
ㄱ -k (stop, unreleased) 국 guk Soup / broth
ㄴ -n 산 san Mountain
ㄷ -t (stop, unreleased) 낫 nat Sickle / better
ㄹ -l 말 mal Horse / word
ㅁ -m 밥 → 밤 bam Night / chestnut
ㅂ -p (stop, unreleased) 입 ip Mouth
ㅇ -ng 강 gang River
Unreleased stops: The -k, -t, and -p batchim sounds are "stopped" — your mouth forms the shape but doesn't release air. It's like the word just ends abruptly. Native speakers do this naturally; you'll hear it clearly once you're listening for it.
Lesson 2 · Korean sentence structure
The golden rule: Korean is Subject → Object → Verb

This is the biggest structural difference from English. In Korean, the verb always comes last. Particles (tiny markers) are attached to nouns to show their role in the sentence.

English vs Korean word order:
English
I   coffee   drink
Subject → Verb → Object
Korean
저는   커피를   마셔요
Subject → Object → Verb
Particles — your new best friends: 은/는 — topic marker (은 after consonant, 는 after vowel) — "as for..."
이/가 — subject marker (이 after consonant, 가 after vowel)
을/를 — object marker (을 after consonant, 를 after vowel)
에 — location / direction — "at", "to", "in" Don't panic — these feel weird at first but become automatic very quickly.
Lesson 3 · Your first full sentences
Build these 6 sentence patterns — they cover a huge range of situations
저는 (I) + [noun]이에요/예요 = I am [noun]
저는 학생이에요.
Jeoneun haksaengieyo.
I am a student.
이에요 after a consonant-ending noun, 예요 after a vowel-ending noun.
[noun]은/는 + [adjective]요 = [noun] is [adjective]
음식이 맛있어요.
Eumsigi masisseoyo.
The food is delicious.
음식 = food. You already know 맛있어요 from Week 2!
저는 (I) + [noun]을/를 좋아해요 = I like [noun]
저는 한국어를 좋아해요.
Jeoneun hangugeoreul joahaeyo.
I like Korean (language).
좋아해요 (joahaeyo) = like. 한국어 = Korean language.
[place]에 + 가요 / 있어요 = Going to / at [place]
식당에 가요.
Sikdange gayo.
I'm going to the restaurant.
식당 = restaurant. 가요 = going. 에 = direction particle.
[noun] + 주세요 = Please give me [noun]
물 주세요.
Mul juseyo.
Please give me water.
물 = water (Week 1!). 주세요 = please give me. Drop any particle for short, natural requests.
[noun] + 있어요 / 없어요 = There is / there isn't [noun]
시간이 없어요.
Sigani eopseoyo.
I don't have time / There's no time.
시간 = time. 없어요 = doesn't exist / don't have. Very natural in daily speech.
Culture note
🎤
노래방 (noraebang) — private karaoke rooms
Noraebang (literally "singing room") is a cornerstone of Korean social life. Unlike Western karaoke bars, you book a private room with your group — no audience, no embarrassment. It's used by all ages and is the go-to activity after a meal or work dinner. Even if your Korean is minimal, jumping into a 노래방 is one of the fastest ways to connect with locals.
