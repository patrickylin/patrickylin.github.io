Lesson 1 · Past continuous — was/were + verb-ing
The ongoing background of the past — the interrupted action pattern

The past continuous describes an action that was in progress at a specific past moment. It's formed with was/were + verb-ing — exactly like the present continuous but with past "to be". Its most important use in storytelling is the interrupted action pattern: something was happening when something else suddenly occurred.

Ongoing background (past continuous)
I was sleeping
was + sleeping
⚡
Trigger word
when
or "while"
→
Interrupting event (past simple)
the phone rang
past simple — snap!
The four main patterns:
🎥 + ⚡ I was reading when she called. — Ongoing action interrupted by sudden event.
🎥 + 🎥 While I was cooking, she was cleaning. — Two ongoing actions happening simultaneously.
🖼️ At 8pm last night, I was watching a film. — Activity in progress at a specific past time.
🎥 → It was raining, so we stayed inside. — Background → consequence.
Was vs were: I/he/she/it → was. You/we/they → were. Negative: wasn't / weren't. Question: Was she sleeping? Were they working? — exactly like present continuous but in the past. The same stative verb restriction applies: "I was knowing" ❌ — stative verbs don't use continuous forms in the past either.

Past simple vs past continuous — the classic contrast:

"She was walking home when it started to rain." — Walking was the background; the rain was the interruption.
"She walked home and it rained." — Two completed sequential events, no overlap implied.
"While they were talking, I finished my coffee." — Their talking was the backdrop to my finishing.
"What were you doing at 9pm last night?" — Asking about activity in progress at a specific time.
Lesson 2 · Modal verbs — the six you need most
Can · Could · Will · Would · Should · Must — the grammar of possibility and obligation

Modal verbs are auxiliary verbs that add meaning about ability, possibility, permission, obligation, and prediction. They have special grammar rules: no -s for he/she/it, no infinitive "to", always followed by the base form of the main verb, and no direct past form (they use workarounds).

Modal Past form Core meanings & examples

past: could

💪 Ability: I can speak Japanese.
🔓 Permission (informal): You can sit here.

should
past: should have
 should have
💡 Advice/recommendation: You should see a doctor.
📐 Expectation: The train should arrive at noon.
😬 Mild obligation: I should call my parents.
You shouldn't worry. · Should I bring anything? · She should be here soon.
must
past: had to
 had to
🔴 Strong obligation: You must wear a seatbelt.
🕵️ Strong deduction: She must be tired — she worked 12 hours.
⚠️ Must not = prohibition: You must not park here.
I must finish this today. · It must be cold outside. · You mustn't lie.
Must not vs Don't have to — the critical difference:
"You must not use your phone here." = Prohibition — it is forbidden.
"You don't have to use your phone here." = No obligation — you can if you want, but it isn't required.
This distinction is essential. Confusing them can cause serious misunderstanding — telling someone "you must not pay" when you mean "you don't have to pay" is the opposite of your intention!

Culture note
🎩
Modals as politeness markers — the British art of softening
In British English especially, modal verbs do enormous social work as politeness softeners. The progression "Can you help?" → "Could you help?" → "Would you be able to help?" → "I was wondering if you might possibly be able to help?" represents a scale of increasing deference and formality. Moving up the scale doesn't change the factual request — it signals more social distance, more respect, or higher stakes. Native British speakers navigate this scale instinctively, and learners who only know "can" and "must" often come across as blunter than intended. A key insight: in English, the more words you use to make a request, the more polite you are being — brevity can accidentally read as rudeness.

/ˈkomo ˈʎeɡo a/ · /ˈkomo se ˈβa a/

¿Cómo llego a la estación? = How do I get to the station? ¿Cómo se va al aeropuerto? = How do you get to the airport? (impersonal se — very natural).

Billete (Spain) · boleto (LatAm) = ticket. Ida = one way (lit. "going"). Ida y vuelta = return (lit. "going and return"). ¿En qué andén sale el tren? = Which platform does the train leave from?
Lesson 3 · The future tense — futuro simple
Attach the future endings directly to the infinitive — elegantly simple

The Spanish future simple is one of the most elegant tenses to learn: you simply add the future endings directly to the full infinitive (for ALL three verb families — no stem change needed for regular verbs). The endings are the same for -ar, -er, and -ir verbs.

PronounEndinghablar (speak)comer (eat)vivir (live)
yo -é hablaré comeré viviré
tú -ás hablarás comerás vivirás
él/ella -á hablará comerá vivirá
nosotros -emos hablaremos comeremos viviremos
vosotros -éis hablaréis comeréis viviréis
ellos/ellas -án hablarán comerán vivirán
Key irregular future stems — only 12 verbs break the pattern:
tener → tendr- (tendré) · salir → saldr- (saldré) · venir → vendr- (vendré)
poder → podr- (podré) · saber → sabr- (sabré) · haber → habr- (habrá)
querer → querr- (querré) · hacer → har- (haré) · decir → dir- (diré)
poner → pondr- (pondré) · caber → cabr- (cabré) · valer → valdr- (valdrá)
Once you know the irregular stems, the endings are always the same — so 12 stems to memorise vs 12 × 6 = 72 irregular forms. Memorise the stems!
Lesson 4 · Ir + a + infinitive — "going to" in Spanish
The everyday alternative to the future — plans, intentions, near future

Just like English "going to", Spanish uses ir + a + infinitive to express plans and intentions — and it's used far more frequently in everyday conversation than the future simple, which tends to sound more formal or distant.

Ir + a + infinitive (going to)
voy a · vas a · va a · vamos a · vais a · van a
📅 Near future / planned event
🎯 Personal intention
💬 Everyday casual speech
🔮 Prediction based on present evidence
Voy a comer paella esta noche.
(I'm going to eat paella tonight.)
Va a llover — mira las nubes.
(It's going to rain — look at the clouds.)
Future simple (futuro simple)
hablaré · comerás · vivirá...
🔮 More distant future
📜 Formal predictions / promises
❓ Wondering / speculation
📰 Written language / news
Comeré paella algún día en Valencia.
(I will eat paella in Valencia someday.)
¿Estará en casa? (I wonder if she's home.)
Vamos a — the double-duty expression: "Vamos a" literally means "We are going to" (future intention) but is also used as a direct command / suggestion: "¡Vamos a comer!" = Let's eat! / We're going to eat! Context clarifies which meaning applies. "¡Vamos!" alone = Let's go! / Come on! — one of the most energetic words in Spanish.

Modal expressions — power phrases with poder, deber, tener que:

💪
poder + infinitivo — can / to be able to
puedo · puedes · puede · podemos · podéis · pueden
Ability and permission
¿Puedes ayudarme? (Can you help me?) · No puedo dormir (I can't sleep) · ¿Se puede fumar aquí? (Can one smoke here? — impersonal). Poder is the Spanish equivalent of "can" and is used constantly.
⚖️
deber + infinitivo — should / ought to
debo · debes · debe · debemos · debéis · deben
Moral obligation / strong advice
Debes descansar más. (You should rest more.) · Debemos llegar a tiempo. (We ought to arrive on time.) Deber is stronger than English "should" — it implies a moral duty. For softer advice, use debería (conditional): Deberías llamarle.
🔴
tener que + infinitivo — have to / must
tengo que · tienes que · tiene que...
Obligation / necessity
Tengo que estudiar más. (I have to study more.) · Tienes que ver esa película. (You have to see that film.) This is the most common way to express obligation in conversational Spanish — stronger than deber but less harsh than must.

Culture note
🚄
La red de AVE — Spain's high-speed rail revolution
Spain has the longest high-speed rail network in Europe and the second longest in the world after China — over 3,700km of track. The AVE (Alta Velocidad Española — the acronym also means "bird" in Spanish, a deliberate wordplay) transformed how Spaniards travel. Madrid to Seville in under 2.5 hours. Madrid to Barcelona in 2h30. The trains are punctual, comfortable, and significantly cheaper than flying once you factor in airport time. Interestingly, Spain chose a unique track gauge for its AVE — the same as France's TGV standard — breaking from the wider "Iberian gauge" used by older Spanish trains. This decision was controversial but visionary: it integrated Spain into the European high-speed network. For Spanish learners, the AVE is a wonderful example of a word with multiple meanings — and proof that Spain takes its infrastructure as seriously as its language.

How modals work Verb-ending changes

The modality spectrum: Every language needs to express what is possible, obligatory, permitted, or desired — but the mechanisms differ radically. Japanese builds ability into the verb itself (potential form). Korean uses post-verbal auxiliary constructions. English uses invariant modal verbs (can never adds -s). Spanish uses fully conjugated verbs (puedo/puedes/puede...). Same communicative need — four completely different solutions. This is what makes comparative language study so illuminating.
