Japanese — 日本語
Week 1 · The writing systems & the 5 vowel sounds
Big picture
Japanese has 3 writing systems — here's the order to learn them
ScriptWhat it isWhen you'll learn it
ひらがな
Hiragana		46 phonetic characters. The core script for Japanese words. Every sound has a symbol.✅ Week 1–2 (start here)
カタカナ
Katakana		Another 46 phonetic characters. Used for foreign words & loanwords (e.g. coffee = コーヒー).Week 3
漢字
Kanji		Chinese-origin characters. Thousands exist — daily life needs ~2,000. Don't worry about these yet.Week 5+
Good news: Hiragana is phonetic — once you learn the 46 sounds, you can read anything written in it. Think of it like learning the alphabet, but each letter = a full syllable (e.g. か = "ka", み = "mi").

Culture note
🙇
Bowing (お辞儀 — ojigi)
In Japan, bowing replaces the handshake. A slight bow (~15°) means "thank you" or "hello". A deeper bow (~30–45°) shows respect or apology. As a foreigner, any sincere bow will be appreciated — locals won't expect perfection.

Korean — 한국어
Week 1 · Hangul — the world's most logical alphabet
Big picture
Korean uses ONE script — and it was scientifically designed

Unlike Japanese (3 scripts) or Chinese (thousands of characters), Korean uses Hangul (한글) — a single phonetic alphabet invented in 1443 by King Sejong specifically to be easy to learn. Most people can read Hangul in 1–2 weeks.

Key insight: Hangul characters aren't written left to right like English — they're stacked into syllable blocks. For example, the word 한 (han) is one block made of 3 parts: ㅎ (h) + ㅏ (a) + ㄴ (n).
Lesson 1 · The block system
How Hangul syllable blocks are built

Every Korean syllable is written as a compact block. The block always has at least a consonant + a vowel, and optionally a final consonant (called a 받침, batchim).

Culture note
🙏
Politeness levels (존댓말 — jondaemal)
Korean has formal and informal speech built into its grammar. As a beginner, always use formal speech (ending words in -요 or -습니다) with anyone older or in a service setting. Koreans deeply appreciate when foreigners try to use the polite form — it shows cultural awareness.
