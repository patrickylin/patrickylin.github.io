Lesson 1 · Japan's transport options
4 ways to get around — and when to use each
🚆
新幹線
shinkansen
Bullet train — city to city
The world's most punctual train. Tokyo→Osaka in 2h15m. Buy tickets at みどりの窓口 (midori no madoguchi = green window) or via a Japan Rail Pass if visiting multiple cities.
🚇
地下鉄
chikatetsu
Subway — within cities
Tokyo's subway is the world's busiest and most complex. Each line has a colour code and letter prefix (e.g. G = Ginza line). Maps are always in English too.
🚌
バス
basu
Bus — local routes
Pay when you board (urban) or when you exit (rural). IC cards work on most urban buses. Announce your stop with 次止まってください (tsugi tomatte kudasai = please stop at the next stop).
🚕
タクシー
takushī
Taxi — door to door
Doors open and close automatically — never touch them. Drivers wear white gloves. Expensive but reliable. Show the driver your destination written in Japanese or on a map app.
Lesson 2 · The IC card — your most important travel tool
Suica & Pasmo — one card for trains, buses, and shops

An IC card (交通系ICカード, kōtsū-kei IC kādo) is a rechargeable transit card that works on almost every train, subway, and bus in Japan. It also works as a contactless payment card at konbini, vending machines, and many shops.

Suica / PASMO
交通系ICカード — Japan IC Transit Card
🚆 All trains 🚇 Subway 🚌 Buses 🏪 Konbini 🍜 Some restaurants 🎰 Vending machines
🐧
Get one immediately on arrival. Buy a Suica at JR station machines or add it to Apple Wallet / Google Pay before you arrive. Top up at any チャージ (chāji = charge) machine. Starting balance ¥1,500 (¥500 is a deposit). This single card removes almost all transport friction during your trip.

Lesson 4 · Essential transport phrases
Everything you need to get from A to B in Japan
🎫
〜まで一枚ください
[destination]-made ichimai kudasai
One ticket to [destination], please
まで (made) = to/until. 一枚 (ichimai) = one (flat-thing counter). 二枚 (nimai) = two tickets.
🚉
〜行きは何番線ですか？
[destination]-yuki wa nan-ban-sen desu ka?
Which platform for [destination]?
〜行き (yuki) = bound for. 何番線 (nan-ban-sen) = which track number. Very useful when platforms are not in English.
⏱️
次の電車は何時ですか？
Tsugi no densha wa nan-ji desu ka?
What time is the next train?
次 (tsugi) = next. 電車 (densha) = train. Japanese trains run like clockwork — the answer will be exactly on time.
🔄
〜に乗り換えはどこですか？
[line name]-ni norikae wa doko desu ka?
Where do I transfer to [line]?
乗り換え (norikae) = transfer. One of the most useful phrases in complex stations like Shinjuku or Umeda.
🚕
〜へお願いします
[destination]-e onegaishimasu
To [destination], please (taxi)
The simplest taxi phrase. Show the driver a map if unsure. へ (e) = to/toward.
🗺️
〜はどうやって行きますか？
[place]-wa dou yatte ikimasu ka?
How do I get to [place]?
どうやって (dou yatte) = how / by what means. A slightly more advanced but very natural phrase.

Culture note
🚆
Train etiquette — Japan's unwritten rules
Japan's trains run on politeness as much as electricity. Silent carriages (especially the shinkansen's quiet car 静粛車, seishukusha) mean no phone calls. Priority seats (優先席) near the doors are for the elderly and pregnant — give them up without hesitation. Backpacks go on the rack overhead or held in front. Eating on local trains is considered rude, though shinkansen are fine (ekiben time!). And perhaps most importantly: the train will leave at exactly the announced second. Do not run — just catch the next one.

Lesson 1 · Korea's transport options
Getting around Korea — fast, affordable, and easy
🚇
지하철
jihacheol
Subway — Seoul's backbone
Seoul's subway has 23 lines and covers the entire metro area. It's cheap (from ₩1,400), extremely clean, and has English on all signs. One of the world's best urban transit systems.
🚄
KTX
KTX (케이티엑스)
Korea Train Express — bullet train
Seoul→Busan in 2h15m, Seoul→Gyeongju in under 2h. Book at korail.go.kr or the station. Dramatically more comfortable than flying.
🚌
버스
beoseu
Bus — colour-coded by type
Blue = long-distance city, Green = local neighbourhood, Red = express suburban, Yellow = downtown loop. T-money works on all. Board at the front, exit at the back.
🚕
택시
taeksi
Taxi — colour-coded by tier
Orange/silver = regular (cheap), Black = premium (고급택시). Use KakaoTaxi app for easy hailing with English support. Drivers often don't speak English — show destination on maps.
Lesson 2 · T-money — Korea's IC transit card
One card for all transport in Korea

T-money (티머니) is Korea's rechargeable transit card — the equivalent of Japan's Suica. It works on all Seoul subway lines, buses, KTX, and even some taxis and convenience stores.

T-money 티머니
교통카드 — Korea Transit Card
🚇 All subway lines 🚌 All city buses 🚕 Most taxis 🏪 GS25 / CU stores 🚄 KTX
💳
Buy at any convenience store (편의점) on arrival — GS25, CU, 7-Eleven, or Emart24. Cost: ₩2,500–4,000 for the card. Top up (충전, chung-jeon) at any subway station machine or convenience store. You get a transfer discount if you switch from bus to subway within 30 minutes — a big money-saver.

The 4 most useful lines for visitors in Seoul

Seoul has 23 subway lines but most visitor destinations are covered by just 4 core lines. Each line has a number, a colour, and stations are numbered — e.g. 강남역 is on Line 2 (green), station number 222.

Lesson 4 · Transport phrases in Korean
Getting around — station to taxi to asking directions
🎫
〜까지 얼마예요?
[destination]-kkaji eolmayeyo?
How much is it to [destination]?
까지 (kkaji) = until / to. Works for taxis and asking about ticket prices at the station.
🚇
몇 호선 타야 해요?
Myeot hoseon taya haeyo?
Which line do I need to take?
호선 (hoseon) = line number. 타다 (tada) = to ride/take. 몇 (myeot) = which number.
🔄
어디서 갈아타요?
Eodiseo garatayo?
Where do I transfer?
갈아타다 (garatada) = to transfer/change. 어디서 (eodiseo) = where (at). Very useful in multi-line journeys.
🚕
〜으로 가주세요
[destination]-euro gajuseyo
Please take me to [destination]
으로 (euro) = toward/to (after consonant). 가주세요 = please go. The standard taxi instruction.
⏹️
여기서 세워 주세요
Yeogiseo sewo juseyo
Please stop here
여기서 (yeogiseo) = here. 세우다 (seuda) = to stop (a vehicle). Essential for taxis — say it firmly when approaching your destination.
🗺️
〜에 어떻게 가요?
[place]-e eotteoke gayo?
How do I get to [place]?
어떻게 (eotteoke) = how. 가요 = go. One of the most useful street-level direction-asking phrases.

Lesson 6 · Bonus — Korean adjectives
Descriptive words that will transform your Korean

Korean adjectives work differently from English — they conjugate just like verbs! In their dictionary form they end in 다, and they're placed before a noun or at the end of a sentence as a predicate. Here are the 10 most useful ones for daily life.

크다
keuda
Big / large
커요 (keoyo) — it's big
큰 가방 (keun gabang) — big bag
작다
jakda
Small / little
작아요 (jagayo) — it's small
작은 방 (jageun bang) — small room
빠르다
ppareuda
Fast / quick
빨라요 (ppallayo) — it's fast
빠른 기차 (ppareun gicha) — fast train
느리다
neurida
Slow
느려요 (neuryeoyo) — it's slow
느린 버스 (neurin beoseu) — slow bus
비싸다
bissada
Expensive
비싸요 (bissayo) — it's expensive
비싼 택시 (bissan taeksi) — expensive taxi
싸다
ssada
Cheap / inexpensive
싸요 (ssayo) — it's cheap
싼 지하철 (ssan jihacheol) — cheap subway
가깝다
gakkapda
Close / near
가까워요 (gakkaweoyo) — it's near
가까운 역 (gakkaun yeok) — nearby station
복잡하다
bokjaphada
Complicated / crowded
복잡해요 (bokjaphaeyo) — it's crowded
복잡한 역 (bokjapan yeok) — busy station
Adjectives as predicates: In Korean, adjectives at the end of a sentence act exactly like verbs — 지하철이 빨라요 (jihacheori ppallayo) = "The subway is fast." You don't need 이다 (to be) — the adjective itself carries that meaning. This makes sentences feel very direct and natural.
Culture note
🚇
Seoul subway culture — unwritten rules
The Seoul subway has dedicated quiet zones and phone-free carriages on some lines. Priority seats (교통 약자석, gyotong yakjaseok) — marked in blue/pink — are strictly for the elderly, pregnant, and disabled. Unlike Japan, Koreans do eat and drink on the subway, though loud behaviour is frowned upon. One unique feature: subway stations double as massive underground shopping malls — Gangnam Station alone has over 200 shops beneath it. Arrive early and explore before your train.
