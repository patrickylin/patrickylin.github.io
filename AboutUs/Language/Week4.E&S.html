Lesson 1 · Numbers — from 1 to a billion
The English number system — regular, logical, and full of "and"

English numbers follow a consistent, predictable pattern from 1 upward. The main quirks are the irregular teens (eleven, twelve, thirteen...) and the British/American "and" rule for hundreds.

Large numbers:

100

British English: 101 = "one hundred and one". American English: "one hundred one" (no "and").

1,500 = "one thousand five hundred" or "fifteen hundred". Both are correct.

Always singular: "five million" (never "five millions" unless standalone).

Note: British "billion" historically meant a million million. Now both UK and US use the American billion (10⁹).
The world has eight billion people.
The "and" rule: British English inserts "and" before the last part of a number: 101 = "one hundred and one" · 1,256 = "one thousand two hundred and fifty-six". American English usually omits the "and". Both are understood globally — but knowing this prevents confusion when listening to British broadcasts or reading UK texts.

Lesson 3 · Telling the time
What time is it? — English has two distinct systems (and one very confusing one)

English has a formal digital system (3:15 = "three fifteen") and a traditional analogue system using "past", "to", "half", and "quarter". Native speakers use both interchangeably — learners must recognise both.

What time is it? / What's the time?
Both equally common in British English
Asking for the current time
American English prefers "What time is it?" Answer: "It's three o'clock" / "It's about half past two" / "It's nearly four." Adding "about" or "nearly" softens the precision.
⏰
At what time...? / When does...?
/æt wɒt taɪm/ · /wɛn dʌz/
Asking about scheduled times
"At what time does the train leave?" / "When does the museum open?" The answer uses "at": "It leaves at six thirty." / "It opens at nine o'clock."
🌅
AM and PM
ante meridiem / post meridiem
Before noon and after noon
AM = morning (midnight to noon). PM = afternoon/evening (noon to midnight). Common confusion: 12 AM = midnight · 12 PM = noon. Many native speakers find this unintuitive too — "12 noon" and "12 midnight" are unambiguous alternatives.

The date format — the source of enormous international confusion:

🇬🇧 British English
DD/MM/YYYY
Day first, then month, then year. The 4th of July = 04/07/2025. Spoken: "the fourth of July" or "July the fourth".
Written: 25/12/2025 = Christmas Day
Spoken: "the twenty-fifth of December"
🇺🇸 American English
MM/DD/YYYY
Month first — opposite to British! 04/07/2025 in American English = April 7th, not July 4th. This causes real-world confusion.
Written: 12/25/2025 = Christmas Day
Spoken: "December twenty-fifth"
The date format danger zone: 01/04/2025 means January 4th in the US but April 1st in the UK. When the day is above 12, the confusion disappears (25/12 can only be December 25th). But dates like 03/04 are genuinely ambiguous internationally. The ISO 8601 standard (YYYY-MM-DD) was invented precisely to eliminate this confusion — but everyday English ignores it completely.
📅
What's the date today? / What day is it?
Asking about the date or day
"It's Monday." / "It's the fifteenth of March." / "Today is March fifteenth." Note: ordinals are used when speaking dates — you SAY "the fifteenth" but you WRITE "March 15".
🗓️
On Monday · In March · In 2025 · At Christmas
Prepositions with time — on/in/at
ON = days and specific dates (on Monday · on 25th December). IN = months, years, seasons (in March · in 2025 · in summer). AT = times of day and holidays (at 3pm · at Christmas · at midnight). This on/in/at system must be memorised.
Culture note
🕰️
The 12-hour clock — and why the world is moving to 24-hour time
English-speaking countries (particularly the UK, US, and Australia) cling to the 12-hour AM/PM clock more than almost any other culture. Most of Europe, Japan, Korea, and Latin America primarily use the 24-hour clock (14:00 = 2 PM) in official contexts. This means that English learners from these backgrounds must learn a new system — and vice versa. An interesting linguistic note: the BBC, airports, railways, and the military all use 24-hour time even in English. The 12-hour clock is primarily a conversational convention, not an official one. When learning or teaching English time expressions, it's worth knowing both — "fifteen hundred hours" (15:00) alongside "three o'clock in the afternoon."

Lesson 2 · Telling the time — Las horas
¿Qué hora es? — Spanish time is elegant and logical

Spanish time uses the verb ser (to be) — because time is seen as a permanent/defining fact. Crucially: only 1 o'clock uses singular "Es la una" — all other hours use plural "Son las dos/tres/cuatro...".

De la mañana / de la tarde / de la noche: Instead of AM/PM, Spanish specifies: de la mañana (morning, ~midnight to noon) · de la tarde (afternoon, ~noon to sunset) · de la noche (evening/night, ~sunset to midnight). "Son las tres de la tarde" = it's 3 in the afternoon. "Son las diez de la noche" = it's 10 at night. More descriptive and less ambiguous than AM/PM!

Lesson 4 · Essential time & date phrases
Talking about when things happen — prepositions of time in Spanish
🕐
¿Qué hora es? / ¿A qué hora...?
/ke ˈoɾa es/ · /a ke ˈoɾa/
What time is it? / At what time...?
¿A qué hora llega el tren? = At what time does the train arrive? Answer: "A las tres y media" (At half past three). The preposition "a" = at (for times).
📅
¿Qué día es hoy? / ¿A cuántos estamos?
What day is today? / What's the date? (lit. "To how many are we?")
Asking the day and date
¿A cuántos estamos? is the uniquely Spanish way of asking the date — literally "at how many [of the month] are we?". Answer: "Estamos a quince de marzo" (It's the 15th of March).
🗓️
El lunes · En marzo · En 2025 · A las tres
Prepositions with time
El [day] = on [day] — no "en" for days! En [month/year] = in [month/year]. A las [time] = at [time]. "El lunes voy al médico" (On Monday I go to the doctor). "En marzo hace frío" (In March it's cold).
⏩
hoy · mañana · ayer · pasado mañana
today · tomorrow · yesterday · the day after tomorrow
Essential time reference words
Note: mañana means BOTH "tomorrow" AND "morning" — context clarifies. "Hasta mañana" = See you tomorrow. "Por la mañana" = in the morning. Also: la semana pasada (last week) · la semana que viene (next week).
Culture note
🎉
Fiestas nacionales — Spain's calendar of celebration
Spain has more public holidays than almost any other European country — 14 national holidays plus additional regional ones, meaning some regions celebrate up to 17 days per year. The most culturally significant: Semana Santa (Holy Week before Easter) — elaborate processions fill city streets for an entire week. El Día de la Hispanidad (October 12th) celebrates Spanish-speaking culture globally. La Nochevieja (New Year's Eve) brings a uniquely Spanish tradition — eating 12 grapes at midnight, one for each stroke of the clock, each grape representing good luck for a month of the coming year. And La Navidad (Christmas) in Spain centres not on December 25th but on January 6th — El Día de los Reyes Magos (Three Kings Day), when children receive their gifts. Knowing these calendar landmarks makes the language come alive with cultural context.
