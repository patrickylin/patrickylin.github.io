Lesson 1 · Japan's four seasons
四季 (shiki) — each season has deep cultural significance in Japan

Japan's four seasons are central to its culture, poetry, food, clothing, and festivals. Mentioning the season is the most natural way to start a weather conversation — and shows cultural fluency.

Lesson 3 · Weather conversation phrases
Talk about the weather — Japan's most universal small talk topic
☀️
今日はいい天気ですね
Kyō wa ii tenki desu ne
It's nice weather today, isn't it
天気 (tenki) = weather. The ね at the end invites agreement — one of the most natural conversation openers in Japanese. Works with any weather word.
🌧️
雨が降っています
Ame ga futte imasu
It is raining (currently)
降る (furu) = to fall (rain/snow). 降っています = is currently falling. This is the 〜ている form you're learning today — the rain is an ongoing state.
🌡️
今日は何度ですか？
Kyō wa nan-do desu ka?
What temperature is it today?
度 (do) = degrees. 〜度です = it's ~ degrees. Japan uses Celsius. 25度 = 25°C (comfortable). 35度以上 = 35°C+ (dangerous summer heat).
☂️
傘を持っていきますか？
Kasa wo motte ikimasu ka?
Are you taking an umbrella?
傘 (kasa) = umbrella. 持っていく (motte iku) = to take/bring along. Japanese people are meticulous about checking rain forecasts — umbrellas are a national obsession.
🌸
今年の春は暖かいですね
Kotoshi no haru wa atatakai desu ne
This year's spring is warm, isn't it
今年の (kotoshi no) = this year's. Seasonal comparisons are a very natural Japanese conversation topic — especially about cherry blossoms and autumn leaves.
🌀
台風が来ています
Taifū ga kite imasu
A typhoon is coming (approaching)
台風 (taifū) = typhoon. 来ている (kite iru) = is coming (ongoing approach). Japan has a typhoon season (September–October) — knowing this phrase is genuinely useful.

Lesson 4 · Grammar — 〜ている for ongoing states
Beyond "is doing" — 〜ている describes resulting states and habitual actions

You already know 〜ています means "is currently doing" (from Week 9's て-form lesson). Now we go deeper — 〜ている also describes ongoing states that result from a past action, and regular habitual activities. This distinction is crucial for natural Japanese.

Three uses of 〜ている: (1) Action in progress: 食べています = is eating right now. (2) Resulting state: 結婚しています = is married (resulted from getting married). (3) Habit/routine: 毎日走っています = runs every day (habitually). All use the same form — context tells you which meaning applies.
Use Verb + ている Natural meaning
Action in progress 雨が降っていますame ga futte imasu It is raining (right now)Look outside — the rain is actively falling.
Resulting state 窓が開いていますmado ga aite imasu The window is openSomeone opened it — now it's in the open state.
Resulting state 結婚していますkekkon shite imasu Is marriedThe wedding happened — married is the current state.
Habit / routine 毎日日本語を勉強していますmainichi Nihongo wo benkyō shite imasu Studies Japanese every dayAn ongoing regular activity — a true statement about you!
Resulting state 電気がついていますdenki ga tsuite imasu The light is onSomeone switched it on — the on-state is ongoing.
Occupation 東京に住んでいますTōkyō ni sunde imasu Lives in TokyoMoved there — living there is the ongoing state.

〜ている in weather and nature:

雪が積もっています。
Yuki ga tsumotte imasu.
Snow is piled up / accumulating.
積もる (tsumoru) = to pile up/accumulate. The snow has been falling and is now in a piled-up state.
桜が咲いています。
Sakura ga saite imasu.
The cherry blossoms are in bloom.
咲く (saku) = to bloom. The blossoms bloomed — and are now in the bloomed/blooming state. One of the most beautiful sentences in Japanese.
風が強く吹いています。
Kaze ga tsuyoku futte imasu.
The wind is blowing strongly.
強く (tsuyoku) = strongly. 吹く (fuku) = to blow. 吹いています = is blowing (ongoing).
Negative state — 〜ていません: The window is not open = 窓が開いていません (mado ga aite imasen). It's not raining = 雨が降っていません (ame ga futte imasen). Simply replace います with いません for any 〜ている state.
Culture note
🌸
季語 (kigo) — seasonal words in Japanese poetry
Japan's awareness of nature and seasons runs so deep that traditional haiku poetry (俳句) requires a 季語 (kigo = seasonal reference word) — a single word that places the poem in a specific season. 桜 (cherry blossom) = spring · 蛍 (firefly) = summer · 紅葉 (autumn leaves) = autumn · 雪 (snow) = winter. This poetic tradition reflects how deeply seasons are woven into Japanese identity. When a Japanese person mentions the weather, they're not just making small talk — they're connecting with something profound. The phrase 春らしくなりましたね (haru-rashiku narimashita ne = it's started to feel like spring, hasn't it) is one of the warmest things you can say to someone in late February or early March.

Lesson 1 · Korea's four seasons
사계절 (sagyejeol) — Korea's dramatic seasonal contrasts

Korea has sharper seasonal contrasts than Japan — winters are colder, summers are hotter, and the monsoon (장마, jangma) is more intense. Each season brings its own culture, food, and events.

🌸
봄
bom
Spring (March–May)
Cherry blossoms, yellow dust from China (황사 hwangsa), mild temperatures. Best season for outdoor festivals.
벚꽃 (beotkkot) · 황사 (hwangsa = yellow dust) · 따뜻하다 (ttatteuthada = warm)
☀️
여름
yeoreum
Summer (June–August)
장마 rainy season (June–July), then intense heat and humidity. Beach culture, watermelon, iced coffee obsession.
장마 (jangma = monsoon) · 덥다 (deopda = hot) · 습하다 (seupada = humid)
🍁
가을
gaeul
Autumn (September–November)
Korea's most beautiful season — vivid 단풍 (danpung = autumn foliage), crisp skies, harvest festivals, hiking season peak.
단풍 (danpung) · 시원하다 (siwonhada = cool/refreshing) · 추석 (chuseok)
❄️
겨울
gyeoul
Winter (December–February)
Bitterly cold with heavy snow in the north — but also ski resorts, 찜질방 culture, hot 삼겹살 dinners, and dazzling LED festivals.
눈 (nun = snow) · 춥다 (chupda = cold) · 패딩 (paeding = padded jacket — everyone wears one)

Lesson 3 · Weather conversation phrases
Natural Korean weather small talk
☀️
오늘 날씨가 정말 좋네요!
Oneul nalssiga jeongmal jone yo!
The weather today is really nice!
날씨 (nalssi) = weather. 좋네요 (joneyo) = it's nice (네요 adds a tone of pleasant discovery/shared observation). The most natural Korean weather opener.
🌧️
오늘 비가 올 것 같아요
Oneul biga ol geot gatayo
It looks like it'll rain today
비가 오다 → 비가 올 것 같아요. Uses -(으)ㄹ 것 같다 from Week 14! Weather prediction is a perfect use case for this grammar.
🌡️
오늘 몇 도예요?
Oneul myeot doyeyo?
What temperature is it today?
도 (do) = degrees Celsius. 영하 (yeongha) = below zero. e.g. 영하 10도 = minus 10°C — common in Korean winters!
☂️
우산 가져가요?
Usan gajyeogayo?
Are you bringing an umbrella?
우산 (usan) = umbrella. 가져가다 (gajyeogada) = to take/bring with you. Koreans check the 기상청 (weather app) religiously during 장마 season.
🍂
이제 제법 가을 날씨네요
Ije jepop gaeul nalssineyo
It's starting to feel properly autumnal now
이제 = now. 제법 = quite/properly. 가을 날씨 = autumn weather. 〜네요 = pleasant discovery/shared observation ending. A beautiful seasonal observation.

Lesson 4 · Grammar — -(으)ㄴ/는 noun modification
Turn verbs and adjectives into noun modifiers — the key to rich Korean sentences

In Korean, verbs and adjectives can directly modify nouns — like attaching "the ~ that ~" in English. This is called noun modification (관형사형, gwanhyeongsahyeong) and it's one of the most powerful and frequently used structures in Korean. Once you master it, your sentences become dramatically richer.

The three forms: Adjectives use (으)ㄴ before a noun: 예쁜 꽃 (pretty flower). Action verbs in present use 는: 먹는 사람 (the person who is eating). Action verbs in past use (으)ㄴ: 먹은 음식 (the food I ate). Future/guessing uses (으)ㄹ: 먹을 음식 (the food I will eat).

Adjective modifiers — (으)ㄴ:

춥다 (cold) + 날씨 (weather)
추운 날씨
chuun nalssi
Cold weather
맑다 (clear) + 하늘 (sky)
맑은 하늘
malgeun haneul
Clear sky
따뜻하다 (warm) + 봄날 (spring day)
따뜻한 봄날
ttatteutan bomnal
A warm spring day
아름답다 (beautiful) + 경치 (scenery)
아름다운 경치
areumdaun gyeongchi
Beautiful scenery

Action verb modifiers — 는 (present) and (으)ㄴ (past):

내리다 (to fall/descend) present
내리는 눈
naerineun nun
Snow that is falling
가다 (to go) present
가는 사람
ganeun saram
The person who is going
먹다 (to eat) past
먹은 음식
meogeun eumsik
The food (I) ate
보다 (to see) future
볼 영화
bol yeonghwa
The movie (I) will see

Full sentences using noun modification:

어제 먹은 음식이 맛있었어요.
Eoje meogeun eumsigi masisseoyo.
The food I ate yesterday was delicious.
먹은 (ate — past modifier) + 음식 (food) = "the food that I ate". Then the whole phrase becomes the subject.
내일 볼 영화 예매했어요?
Naeil bol yeonghwa yemaehasseoyo?
Did you book tickets for the movie you're seeing tomorrow?
볼 (will see — future modifier) + 영화 (movie) = "the movie we will see". 예매하다 = to pre-book.
창문 밖에 내리는 눈이 예뻐요.
Changmun bake naerineun nuni yeppeoyo.
The snow falling outside the window is pretty.
내리는 (falling — present modifier) + 눈 (snow) = "the snow that is falling". A naturally beautiful Korean sentence.
좋아하는 사람이 있어요?
Joahaneun sarami isseoyo?
Is there someone you like (romantically)?
좋아하는 (like — present modifier) + 사람 (person) = "a person that you like". One of the most K-drama lines ever.

Culture note
🌧️
장마 (jangma) — Korea's monsoon season and its culture
Korea's 장마 (monsoon rainy season, usually late June through mid-July) is one of the most distinctive weather events in East Asia. For three to four weeks, it rains almost daily — sometimes in torrential downpours. Korean culture has adapted beautifully: 비 오는 날 (rainy days) are considered perfect for 파전과 막걸리 (pajeon and makgeolli = Korean pancake and rice wine) — a beloved pairing said to have originated because the sound of sizzling pancakes resembles the sound of rain. 비 오는 날에는 파전이 생각나요 (on rainy days I think of pajeon) — you'll hear this constantly from Koreans. Rain in Korea isn't just weather; it's an invitation to slow down, cook, and connect.
