Lesson 1 · Relative clauses — joining sentences elegantly
Who, which, that, whose, where — the connectors that build complex sentences

A relative clause is a subordinate clause that gives more information about a noun. Instead of saying two short sentences ("I met a woman. She speaks five languages.") you can join them into one fluent sentence: "I met a woman who speaks five languages." Relative clauses are what separate beginner sentences from genuinely natural English.

Pronoun Used for Example
who People — as subject of the clause The teacher who taught me Japanese was from Kyoto. · The people who live next door are very kind.
whom People — as object (formal). Often replaced by "who" in speech. The colleague whom I respect most has retired. (Formal) · The colleague who I respect most... (Spoken)
which Things and animals The film which won the award was Spanish. · The car which broke down is being repaired.
that People or things — in defining clauses only (can replace who/which) The book that I recommended is excellent. · The person that called you is waiting.
whose Possession — for people and things The author whose book I loved is speaking tonight. · A company whose profits have tripled.
where Places The city where I grew up has changed a lot. · Is this the restaurant where we met?
when Times I remember the day when I first arrived in Japan. · There are moments when words aren't enough.
why Reasons (after "the reason") That's the reason why I left. · Do you know the reason why she's upset?
Can you drop "that"? In defining relative clauses, "that" can often be omitted when it is the OBJECT of the clause: "The book that I read was amazing." → "The book I read was amazing." ✅ But never drop it when it is the SUBJECT: "The person that called you..." → ~~"The person called you..."~~ ❌ (changes the meaning completely). This omission is called a "zero relative" and is extremely common in spoken English.
Lesson 2 · Defining vs non-defining clauses
The comma that changes everything — essential information vs bonus information

This distinction is one of the most important — and most overlooked — aspects of English relative clauses. The difference is not just punctuation; it changes the logical meaning of the sentence.

Defining (restrictive) — NO commas
The clause is ESSENTIAL to identify which noun is meant. Remove it and the sentence loses its specific meaning. "That" can be used.
The students who work hard will pass.
(Only those who work hard — not all students)

I want the book that is on the table.
(That specific book — not any book)
No commas. "That" allowed. Clause cannot be removed without changing meaning.
Non-defining (non-restrictive) — WITH commas
The clause adds EXTRA information about an already-identified noun. Remove it and the core meaning stays intact. "That" is NOT used — only who/which.
My sister, who lives in Madrid, is visiting next week.
(I have one sister — the clause just adds where she lives)

The Eiffel Tower, which was built in 1889, attracts millions.
(We already know which tower — built date is extra info)
Commas required. Never "that". Clause can be removed — sentence still makes sense.
The sister test: "My sister who lives in Madrid is visiting" (no commas) implies you have MORE THAN ONE sister, and you're specifying which one. "My sister, who lives in Madrid, is visiting" (commas) implies you have ONE sister, and you're adding where she lives as bonus information. The comma is not decoration — it signals whether the clause is identifying or merely commenting. In speech, this difference is marked by a pause.
Lesson 3 · The passive voice
Be + past participle — shifting the focus from doer to receiver

The passive voice moves the focus from who does the action (the agent) to what receives the action (the patient). It's formed with be + past participle in any tense. English uses the passive more than most languages — particularly in formal, academic, scientific, and journalistic writing.

Active — focus on the doer
Shakespeare wrote Hamlet.
Subject = Shakespeare (agent) · Verb = wrote · Object = Hamlet
→
Passive — focus on the work
Hamlet was written by Shakespeare.
Subject = Hamlet (patient) · Verb = was written · Agent = by Shakespeare (optional)

Passive in different tenses:

Present simple: English is spoken in over 50 countries.
Past simple: The Eiffel Tower was built in 1889.
Present perfect: Three people have been arrested.
Future: The results will be announced tomorrow.
Modal: This problem must be solved immediately.
Continuous: The bridge is being repaired at the moment.
When to use the passive — four good reasons:
1. Agent unknown: "My bike was stolen." (We don't know who took it.)
2. Agent obvious/unimportant: "He was arrested." (Obviously by police.)
3. Focus on the result: "The report has been completed." (Result matters, not who did it.)
4. Formal/impersonal style: "Mistakes were made." (Classic political evasion — passive hides the agent deliberately!)
Avoid overusing passive in conversation — it can sound distant and evasive. In academic and scientific writing, it's the standard.

Culture note
📰
The passive voice — power, evasion, and the art of non-apology
The passive voice does more than grammar in English — it is a tool of power and a shield of accountability. Politicians who say "mistakes were made" instead of "I made mistakes" use the passive to acknowledge a problem while erasing responsibility. Corporations issue statements saying "your data was compromised" rather than "we lost your data." Scientists write "the experiment was conducted" rather than "I conducted" to project objectivity. And the British passive is particularly beloved for extreme politeness: "It has been noted that..." (someone complained), "It would be appreciated if..." (please do this). Understanding the passive isn't just grammar — it's learning to read between the lines of English communication. When you hear the passive in public life, ask: who is the missing agent, and why are they hidden?

Lesson 1 · Subjunctive with time clauses
Cuando, hasta que, antes de que — time conjunctions that trigger the subjunctive

Spanish time conjunctions behave differently depending on whether they refer to something that has already happened (past/present habit — indicative) or something that has NOT yet happened (future/hypothetical — subjunctive). This distinction is one of the subtlest and most revealing aspects of the subjunctive.

Conjunction Meaning With indicative (past/habitual) With subjunctive (future/hypothetical)
cuando when Cuando llegué, ya había comido. (When I arrived — it already happened) Cuando llegues, te llamaré. (When you arrive — future, hasn't happened yet)
hasta que until Esperé hasta que llegó. (I waited until she arrived — completed) Espera hasta que llegue. (Wait until she arrives — future)
antes de que before — Llámame antes de que salgas. (Call me before you leave — ALWAYS subjunctive)
en cuanto as soon as En cuanto llegó, comimos. (As soon as she arrived — past) En cuanto llegues, comemos. (As soon as you arrive — future)
después de que after Después de que comió, se fue. (After she ate — past) Después de que comas, sal. (After you eat — future)
mientras while / as long as Mientras trabajaba, escuchaba música. (While working — habitual) Puedes quedarte mientras te portes bien. (As long as you behave — condition)
The golden rule: If the action in the time clause has NOT YET HAPPENED at the time of speaking — use the subjunctive. "Cuando llegues" (When you arrive — you haven't arrived yet) → subjunctive. "Cuando llegaste" (When you arrived — it already happened) → indicative past. The future is inherently uncertain and unrealised, so Spanish treats it as subjunctive territory. This rule applies to ALL time conjunctions except antes de que, which ALWAYS requires subjunctive regardless of tense.
📞
Llámame cuando llegues al aeropuerto.
Call me when you get to the airport.
Cuando + subjunctive (llegues) — the arrival hasn't happened yet. In English, "when you get" also uses present (not future) — both languages treat this future time clause as non-future in form!
⏳
Espera aquí hasta que vuelva.
Wait here until I come back.
Hasta que + subjunctive (vuelva) — the return is in the future. Contrast: "Esperé hasta que volvió" (I waited until she came back — past indicative because it already happened).
🚪
Avísame antes de que te vayas.
Let me know before you leave.
Antes de que ALWAYS takes subjunctive (vayas = subjunctive of irse). There is no indicative version of antes de que — it grammatically implies a future, unrealised event by definition.
✅
En cuanto termines, dímelo.
As soon as you finish, tell me.
En cuanto + subjunctive (termines) — the finishing is future. This is a very natural phrase in Spanish workplaces and everyday conversation. "En cuanto terminé, te llamé" (As soon as I finished, I called you) = past indicative.
Lesson 2 · Por vs Para — the most confusing pair in Spanish
Both translate as "for" — but they mean completely different things

Both por and para can translate as "for" in English — which is why they cause such persistent confusion. The key insight: por looks backward (cause, exchange, duration, movement through) while para looks forward (purpose, destination, deadline, recipient). Think of por as "because of / through / in exchange for" and para as "in order to / for the benefit of / by the time of".

POR
Cause · Exchange · Duration · Movement through · On behalf of
Because of / due to:
Lo hice por amor. (I did it because of love.)

In exchange for:
Te doy €10 por el libro. (I'll give you €10 for the book.)

Duration of time:
Estudié por tres horas. (I studied for three hours.)

Movement through / along:
Pasamos por Madrid. (We passed through Madrid.)

On behalf of / in place of:
Habló por todos nosotros. (He spoke for all of us.)

Means of communication:
Te llamo por teléfono. (I'll call you by phone.)

Passive agent:
Fue escrito por Cervantes. (Written by Cervantes.)
PARA
Purpose · Destination · Deadline · Recipient · Opinion
Purpose / in order to:
Estudio para aprender. (I study in order to learn.)

Recipient / intended for:
Este regalo es para ti. (This gift is for you.)

Destination (going toward):
Salgo para Madrid mañana. (I leave for Madrid tomorrow.)

Deadline (by a time):
Lo necesito para el lunes. (I need it by Monday.)

Opinion / perspective:
Para mí, es demasiado picante. (For me / in my opinion, it's too spicy.)

Employer / working for:
Trabajo para una empresa japonesa. (I work for a Japanese company.)

Unexpected contrast:
Para ser su primer intento, está muy bien. (For a first attempt, it's very good.)
The classic contrast pair:
"Compré flores por ti." = I bought flowers because of you / on account of you (you caused the action).
"Compré flores para ti." = I bought flowers for you (you are the intended recipient).
Same words, completely different meaning! Por looks at WHY something was done (cause). Para looks at WHO benefits or WHERE it goes (purpose/recipient). This distinction unlocks the whole system.
A memory shortcut:
POR = Past causes · On behalf of · Routes (through/along) · exchange · duration · means
PARA = Purpose · Addressed to (recipient) · Reaching a place (destination) · Arbitrary deadline
Neither is perfect, but the directional image helps: por = looking back at causes, para = looking forward to goals.

Culture note
📚
El Instituto Cervantes — the guardian of the Spanish language
Just as France has the Académie française and Japan has the Agency for Cultural Affairs overseeing language, Spain has the Real Academia Española (RAE) — founded in 1713 — whose motto is "Limpia, fija y da esplendor" (It cleans, fixes and gives splendour). The RAE publishes the authoritative dictionary of Spanish and issues rulings on grammar, spelling and new words. Unlike the Académie française (which famously resists foreign loanwords), the RAE tends to be more pragmatic — it accepted tuit (tweet) and selfi (selfie) into the dictionary. Alongside the RAE, the Instituto Cervantes (named after Miguel de Cervantes, author of Don Quijote) promotes Spanish language and culture worldwide — running schools in 90 countries and administering the DELE examinations. Together they represent the fascinating question of who "owns" a language spoken by 500 million people across 20 countries, each with their own vibrant regional variety.

🌏 Four-language comparison — relative clauses & passive voice
How all four languages join clauses and shift focus from doer to receiver

Relative clauses and passive constructions reveal fundamentally different strategies for linking information across the world's languages.

The relative clause direction: English and Spanish place relative clauses AFTER the noun they modify (post-nominal). Japanese and Korean place them BEFORE the noun (pre-nominal) — and don't need a relative pronoun at all, because the verb ending itself signals that the clause is modifying something. This reflects the SOV/SVO divide seen throughout this course. Spanish's universal relative pronoun que (which covers who/which/that) is actually simpler than English's multiple relative pronouns — one of Spanish's quiet elegances.
