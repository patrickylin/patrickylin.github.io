English
Week 1 · The alphabet · Vowel sounds · The great pronunciation challenge
Big picture
English — the world's most widely spoken second language

English is the global language of business, science, aviation, diplomacy, and pop culture. It's spoken by approximately 1.5 billion people — but only 400 million as a first language. That means most English speakers in the world learned it as a second language, just like you're helping someone do now.

English belongs to the Germanic branch of the Indo-European family — but it's been shaped by Latin, French, Old Norse, and dozens of other languages. This messy history is exactly why English pronunciation is so irregular. A language built by invaders and immigrants never got to standardise itself neatly.

The great irony of English: English is considered one of the easier languages to start learning — basic sentences come quickly. But it's one of the hardest to master — its spelling system, irregular verbs, idioms, and subtle social registers take years to fully command. This makes it uniquely rewarding to teach well from the very beginning.

The English spelling problem: The letters A, E, I, O, U each represent multiple different sounds. The letter "A" alone makes at least 7 different sounds: cat, cake, car, call, care, about, many. This is the single biggest challenge for English learners — and why phonics (the study of sound-letter relationships) is taught as a subject in English schools.
Lesson 2 · The English vowel sounds
English has 20 vowel sounds — but only 5 vowel letters to represent them

This mismatch is the heart of English pronunciation difficulty. Compare: Spanish has 5 vowel letters and 5 vowel sounds — perfect correspondence. English has 5 vowel letters and 20 vowel sounds. The result: learners must memorise pronunciation word by word.

The most common sound in English! Unstressed syllables
The schwa /ə/ — the most important sound in English: The schwa is a short, neutral "uh" sound that appears in almost every multi-syllable English word. "banana" = bə-NAH-nə. "about" = ə-BOUT. "doctor" = DOC-tə. Learning where schwas appear is the key to natural English rhythm. Japanese and Korean learners often give every syllable equal weight — English requires stressed syllables to be longer and louder, with unstressed syllables reduced to schwas.
Lesson 3 · Key English pronunciation rules
The patterns that bring order to English spelling chaos
1. Silent E rule
A final silent "e" makes the preceding vowel say its name (long sound).
kit → kite · hop → hope · mad → made
The e is silent but changes the whole word!
2. Vowel team rule
When two vowels go walking, the first one does the talking (usually).
rain · meat · coat · read
But: "ei" in "weird", "ie" in "friend" break this rule!
3. Word stress
English words have one stressed syllable — louder, longer, clearer than the rest.
PH-o-to · pho-TO-gra-phy · PHO-to-graph
Same root word — stress shifts completely!
4. "TH" sounds
English has two TH sounds that don't exist in Japanese, Korean, or Spanish.
voiced /ð/: this · the · that · mother
voiceless /θ/: think · three · thank · tooth
5. R vs L distinction
English /r/ and /l/ are completely different sounds. Japanese and Korean learners often struggle with this distinction.
rice vs lice · right vs light · read vs lead
In Japanese, ら/り/る/れ/ろ sits between R and L
6. Contractions
English commonly shortens words in speech — learners must recognise both forms.
I am → I'm · do not → don't · they are → they're
Contractions are essential for natural spoken English

Culture note
🌍
Which English? British vs American vs World Englishes
English is not one language — it's a family of closely related dialects. British English (UK) and American English differ in spelling (colour vs color), pronunciation (bath /bɑːθ/ vs /bæθ/), vocabulary (lift vs elevator, biscuit vs cookie), and even grammar (I've just eaten vs I just ate). Then there are Australian, Canadian, Indian, Singaporean, Nigerian, and hundreds of other World English varieties — each legitimate and vibrant. When teaching English, it's worth deciding which standard to use as a model while acknowledging that all varieties are valid. The most globally understood varieties are General American and Received Pronunciation (BBC English).

Spanish — Español
Week 1 · The alphabet · 5 perfect vowels · Pronunciation consistency
Big picture
Spanish — the world's second most spoken language by native speakers

Spanish has approximately 500 million native speakers across Spain, Latin America, parts of Africa, and the Philippines. It's the official language of 20 countries — more than any other language. In the United States alone, over 41 million people speak Spanish as their first language.

Spanish belongs to the Romance language family — descended directly from Latin, alongside French, Italian, Portuguese, and Romanian. This shared heritage means that once you know Spanish, other Romance languages become dramatically easier to learn.

The great news for pronunciation: Spanish is one of the most phonetically consistent languages in the world. Unlike English, where you must memorise how to pronounce every word individually, Spanish has a nearly perfect relationship between spelling and sound — once you know the rules, you can read any Spanish word aloud correctly. This is Spanish's greatest gift to the learner.
Lesson 1 · The Spanish alphabet
El alfabeto — 27 letters, one extra that English doesn't have

The Spanish alphabet has 27 letters — all 26 English letters plus Ñ (eñe), which represents a unique sound. Until 2010, CH and LL were also treated as separate letters — so older Spanish dictionaries order words differently!

Tap any letter above to explore its Spanish sound
Key difference from English: In Spanish, letters are consistent. The letter B always sounds like /b/. The letter A always sounds like /a/. Once you learn the alphabet sounds, you can read any Spanish word correctly — something English can't offer!

This is one of the reasons Spanish pronunciation feels accessible to Japanese speakers — and why learning both languages simultaneously makes surprising sense.

The rolled R (RR): The double-R trill is the sound most learners find hardest. The tongue tip vibrates rapidly against the roof of the mouth. Practice by saying "butter" very quickly — the T flapping in American English is close to a single Spanish R (como · pero). The double R needs several rapid flaps. Don't worry if it takes weeks — it always does!
Lesson 4 · Spanish pronunciation rules
The rules that make Spanish so learnable — and a few surprises
1. Accent marks (tildes)
An accent mark (´) shows which syllable to stress when it falls outside the normal pattern.
café · mamá · está · fútbol
Also distinguishes words: sí (yes) vs si (if)
2. Default stress rules
Words ending in a vowel, N, or S: stress the second-to-last syllable. Words ending in other consonants: stress the last syllable.
ha-BLO · CA-sa · ciu-DAD
No accent mark needed if it follows the default
3. H is always silent
Unlike English, Spanish H is never pronounced. It's a completely silent letter in all positions.
hola = /ola/ · hotel = /oˈtel/
English: H is silent only in "hour", "honest" etc.
4. B and V sound the same
In Spanish, B and V are pronounced identically — both as a soft /b/ sound. The difference is purely spelling.
boca (mouth) · vaca (cow) — same /b/ sound!
English strictly separates /b/ and /v/
5. No silent letters (mostly)
Every letter in Spanish is pronounced — except H, and U in QUE/QUI combinations.
que = /ke/ · quiero = /ˈkjeɾo/
Compare English: knight, lamb, psychology...
6. Spanish R vs English R
Single Spanish R is a quick tap of the tongue — lighter than English R. English R is never trilled.
pero (but) = one tap · perro (dog) = trill
The distinction between pero/perro changes meaning!

Culture note
🌎
Which Spanish? Spain vs Latin America
Just as English varies between British and American, Spanish varies significantly between Spain and the 19 Latin American countries where it's spoken. The most notable difference: in Spain, C before E/I and Z are pronounced /θ/ (like English "th") — so "gracias" sounds like "gra-thyas". In Latin America, these are pronounced /s/ — "gra-syas". This is called ceceo (Spain) vs seseo (Latin America). Vocabulary also differs: in Spain a car is "coche", in Mexico "carro", in Argentina "auto". Neither is more "correct" — they're simply different regional standards, both beautiful and valid.
