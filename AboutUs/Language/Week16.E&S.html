📍 Situation: You're in a job interview for a marketing position at a London company.
Interviewer
Thank you for coming in today. Could you tell me a little about yourself?
You
Of course. I've been working in digital marketing for five years, and I've recently been focusing on social media strategy. I'm particularly interested in this role because I admire how your company has been growing internationally.
Interviewer
Excellent. What would you say is your greatest strength?
You
I'd say it's my ability to analyse data and turn it into clear, actionable insights. In my current role, I set up a new reporting system that reduced our decision-making time by 30%.
Interviewer
Very impressive. Do you have any questions for us?
You
Yes — I was wondering if you could tell me more about the team I'd be working with, and what success looks like in this role after the first six months.
Grammar used: Present perfect (I've been working · I've recently been focusing) · Passive (has been growing) · Modal (would · I'd say) · Conditional (I'd be working) · Reported speech structure (I was wondering if...)
The 15 most powerful English phrases — your forever toolkit
🤝
I was wondering if you could...
The politest way to make any request
Past continuous + conditional = maximum politeness. Works in every situation from asking directions to negotiating a pay rise.
💬
I'm looking forward to...
Anticipating something with pleasure — ends every professional email
Always + -ing or noun. "I'm looking forward to hearing from you." The single most useful email closing line in English.
🎯
I'd rather... / I'd prefer...
Expressing preference politely
I'd rather + base form. I'd prefer + to-infinitive or noun. More elegant than "I want".
🙏
Would you mind...?
Ultra-polite request — always + -ing
"Would you mind opening the window?" The most deferential request in English. "Not at all" = yes, I'll do it.
🤔
As far as I'm concerned...
Introducing a personal opinion
Signals this is your view, not fact. More sophisticated than "I think" or "In my opinion".
😊
To be honest / Frankly speaking...
Softening a candid statement
Signals you're about to say something direct. Paradoxically makes honesty more palatable by announcing it.
⚖️
On the one hand... on the other hand...
Presenting two sides of an argument
Essential for balanced discussion. Shows nuance and critical thinking — valued in academic and professional English.
✅
That's a fair point. / Fair enough.
Conceding a point gracefully
Acknowledges the other person's argument without fully agreeing. Very British — part of the polite disagreement toolkit.
🗣️
Could you say that again? / Could you speak more slowly?
Asking for repetition or clarity
Never be afraid to use these. Native speakers appreciate them — and speak more clearly when asked. "I beg your pardon?" is the very formal British version.
📞
I'll get back to you on that.
Buying time to find an answer
Essential in professional contexts. Signals you won't guess — you'll find out and respond. Better than silence or a wrong answer.

📍 Situación: You're at a restaurant in Seville. You want a table, you have a dietary restriction, and you'd like to pay.
Camarero
¡Buenas tardes! ¿Cuántas personas son?
Good afternoon! How many people?
Tú
Somos dos. ¿Tendría una mesa cerca de la ventana, por favor?
There are two of us. Would you have a table near the window, please?
Camarero
Por supuesto. ¿Tienen alguna alergia o intolerancia?
Of course. Do you have any allergies or intolerances?
Tú
Sí — soy alérgico al marisco. ¿Me puede decir qué platos no llevan marisco? Y quisiera probar algo típico de Sevilla.
Yes — I'm allergic to seafood. Could you tell me which dishes don't contain seafood? And I'd like to try something typical of Seville.
Camarero
¡Claro! Le recomiendo el salmorejo y el rabo de toro. ¡Están riquísimos!
Of course! I recommend the salmorejo and oxtail. They're absolutely delicious!
Tú (después)
Ha sido todo delicioso. La cuenta, por favor. ¿Se puede pagar con tarjeta?
Everything has been delicious. The bill, please. Can one pay by card?
Grammar used: Condicional (tendría) · Ser alérgico · Quisiera (polite conditional) · Impersonal se (¿se puede?) · Present perfect (ha sido) · Superlative (-ísimos)
Las 15 frases más poderosas — your forever Spanish toolkit
🙏
Quisiera... / Me gustaría...
I would like... — the polite way to ask for anything
Conditional of querer/gustar. More elegant than "quiero". Works in shops, restaurants, hotels — everywhere.
❓
¿Podría ayudarme? / ¿Me podría decir...?
Could you help me? — conditional for polite requests
Podría (conditional of poder) = the Spanish equivalent of "could you". Essential for formal and polite situations.
🤔
No lo sé con seguridad, pero creo que...
I don't know for certain, but I think...
Hedging your answer — valued in all cultures. Sé (saber, present) · creo (creer, present). Shows intellectual honesty.
😊
¡Qué + adj/noun! — ¡Qué bueno! / ¡Qué lástima!
How...! / What a...! — Spanish's most versatile exclamation
Add any adjective or noun. ¡Qué suerte! ¡Qué asco! ¡Qué maravilla! Instant emotional expressiveness.
🗣️
¿Puede repetir / hablar más despacio?
Can you repeat / speak more slowly?
Never hesitate to use this. Spanish speakers will be delighted you're trying and will always slow down.
📍
¿Cómo se va a...? / ¿Dónde está...?
How do you get to...? / Where is...?
Impersonal se in the direction question. ¿Cómo se va al centro? = How does one get to the centre?
🌡️
Espero que + subjuntivo
I hope that... — desire + subjunctive
Espero que tengas un buen día · Espero que todo salga bien. Natural, warm, and grammatically impeccable.
🤝
Mucho gusto. / Encantado/a.
Nice to meet you. — gender-matched
Encantado if you're male, encantada if female. Your first use of gender agreement in a real social situation!
💪
¡Ojalá! / ¡Menos mal! / ¡Venga!
Hopefully! / Thank goodness! / Come on!
Three exclamations that make you sound truly natural. Ojalá = subjunctive always. Menos mal = relief. Venga = Spain's most versatile word.
🌟
Si tuviera más tiempo, aprendería...
If I had more time, I would learn... — hypothetical
Imperfect subjunctive + conditional. The signature pattern of hypothetical thinking in Spanish. Beautiful and grammatically complete.

The four-language insight: Japanese and Korean share SOV word order and lack articles. English and Spanish share SVO order. Japanese and Spanish share 5 pure vowels. Spanish and Korean are the most grammatically complex of the four. English is the most globally spread. Each language is a complete, perfect system — none is harder or easier, only differently organised. Studying four of them simultaneously gives you a rare cross-linguistic perspective that most people never develop. That perspective — seeing language as a system, not just words to memorise — is perhaps the greatest gift of this entire 16-week journey.
