4, 7, and 9 have two readings each. The readings し (shi), しち (shichi), and く (ku) sound like the words for "death", "bad luck direction", and "suffering" — so in daily speech, yon, nana, and kyuu are preferred. Stick to the safer versions as a beginner.
Lesson 2 · Big numbers
Tens, hundreds, thousands — the pattern is beautifully simple

Japanese numbers are additive. Once you know 1–10, you can build any number by stacking. No "twenty" or "thirty" — just "two-ten", "three-ten" etc.

十 juu (10) 二十 ni-juu (20) 三十 san-juu (30) 百 hyaku (100)
千 sen (1,000) 万 man (10,000) 百万 hyaku-man (1,000,000)

How to build any number:

三+十+五 = 三十五 (san-juu-go) = 35
二+百+五十+八 = 二百五十八 (ni-hyaku-go-juu-hachi) = 258
一万+五千 = 一万五千 (ichi-man-go-sen) = 15,000
Japan uses 万 (man = 10,000) as a base unit — not thousands like English. So 100,000 is "10 man" (十万), not "one hundred thousand". This trips up English speakers most around large prices and population figures.
Lesson 3 · Telling time
〜時 (ji) = o'clock · 〜分 (fun/pun) = minutes

Time in Japanese is straightforward — just attach the number to the right suffix.

🕒
三時です
San-ji desu
It's 3 o'clock
時 (ji) = o'clock. Number + 時 for any hour.
🕠
三時半です
San-ji han desu
It's 3:30
半 (han) = half past. Very commonly used.
🕔
四時十五分です
Yo-ji juu-go-fun desu
It's 4:15
分 (fun/pun) = minutes. Note: 4 o'clock uses よ (yo) not し (shi).
🕐
何時ですか？
Nan-ji desu ka?
What time is it?
何 (nan) = what/how many. 何時 = "what hour". Memorise this question.

Lesson 5 · Counting words (助数詞)
Japanese uses different counter words depending on what you're counting

This is one of the most distinctive features of Japanese. You don't just say "three cats" — you say "three small-animal-units of cat". The counter changes based on the type of object.

〜枚 (mai)
flat thin things
paper, plates, tickets, T-shirts
二枚 = 2 sheets/tickets
〜本 (hon)
long thin things
bottles, pens, umbrellas, rivers
三本 = 3 bottles
〜冊 (satsu)
bound books
books, magazines, notebooks
一冊 = 1 book
〜杯 (hai)
cups / glasses
coffee, tea, soup, glasses of water
コーヒー一杯 = 1 coffee
〜匹 (hiki)
small animals
cats, dogs, fish, insects
猫二匹 = 2 cats
〜人 (nin / ri)
people
1 person = 一人 (hitori), 2 = 二人 (futari), 3+ uses nin
三人 = 3 people
Don't panic about counters. Even Japanese children take years to master all of them. As a beginner, just learn 〜個 (ko) — a generic counter for small objects — and 〜人 (nin) for people. You can use these for almost anything and be understood.

Culture note
💴
Cash is still king in Japan
Despite being a tech powerhouse, Japan remains heavily cash-based — especially at smaller restaurants, temples, and rural shops. ATMs at 7-Eleven and Japan Post are your most reliable options for foreign cards. Always carry yen in small denominations. Coins matter: a 500-yen coin is worth about $3.50 USD. Prices are usually displayed clearly in Arabic numerals, so you won't need to read kanji numbers at checkout — but you will need to count out cash!

Korean — 한국어
Week 4 · Two number systems — when to use each one
Big picture
Korean has two completely separate number systems — and they're not interchangeable

This is the most surprising thing about Korean numbers for English speakers. There are native Korean numbers (고유어 수사) and Sino-Korean numbers (한자어 수사) — borrowed from Chinese, just like Japanese. Each system is used for completely different purposes.

Native Korean — used for:
Counting objects (1–99)
Telling someone's age
Counting hours (time)
Counting people
Sino-Korean — used for:
Money (prices, won)
Minutes & seconds (time)
Dates (months, days)
Phone numbers, addresses
Numbers above 99
Don't let this overwhelm you. The good news: for most tourist/beginner situations, prices use Sino-Korean (which is simpler to learn) and you can always just show fingers for small counts. You'll get the feel for which system to use naturally over time.

Lesson 3 · The two systems in real life
Side by side — see when each is used

Telling time — hours use Native, minutes use Sino:

🕒
세 시 삼십 분
se si samsip bun
3:30 — "three (native) hours, thirty (sino) minutes"
시 = hour marker, 분 = minute marker
🕐
몇 시예요?
Myeot siyeyo?
What time is it?
몇 (myeot) = how many/what number. Use this to ask the time.

Age — always uses Native Korean numbers + 살 (sal):

🎂
저는 스물다섯 살이에요
Jeoneun seumul-daseot sal-ieyo
I am 25 years old
스물 = 20 (native), 다섯 = 5 (native), 살 = age counter

Prices — always Sino-Korean + 원 (won):

💵
오천 원이에요
O-cheon won-ieyo
It's 5,000 won (about $3.70 USD)
Typical price for a street food tteokbokki (rice cake).
🧾
얼마예요?
Eolmayeyo?
How much is it?
From Week 2 — you already know this one!
Culture note
🎂
Korean age — you may be older than you think!
Korea has traditionally used a unique age system where everyone is born at age 1 and everyone gains a year on New Year's Day — not on their birthday. This means you could be 1–2 years older in Korean age than your international age. As of 2023, Korea officially switched to the international system for legal purposes, but you may still encounter the traditional system in casual conversation. If someone asks your Korean age, add 1 (or 2 if your birthday hasn't passed this year).
