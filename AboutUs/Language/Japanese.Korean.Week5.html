Lesson 3 · Saying the full date
Japanese date order: Year → Month → Day

Japanese writes dates in the opposite order to English — biggest unit first. This is the same as ISO format (2025-03-15) but spoken aloud.

Full date format
二〇二五年三月十五日
Ni-zero-ni-go-nen san-gatsu juu-go-nichi
March 15, 2025
年 (nen) = year · 月 (gatsu) = month · 日 (nichi/ka) = day
Days of the month are irregular! The first 10 days have special readings — 1st = 一日 (tsuitachi), 2nd = 二日 (futsuka), 3rd = 三日 (mikka), 4th = 四日 (yokka), 10th = 十日 (tooka). From the 11th onward, most follow the pattern [number]-nichi — except 14th (juuyokka), 20th (hatsuka), and 24th (nijuuyokka).

Lesson 4 · Making appointments
Essential phrases for scheduling — shops, restaurants, clinics
📅
〜に予約したいです
[date/time]-ni yoyaku shitai desu
I'd like to make a reservation for [date/time]
予約 (yoyaku) = reservation. Works for restaurants, hotels, clinics.
📆
今日は何日ですか？
Kyou wa nan-nichi desu ka?
What's today's date?
今日 (kyou) = today. 何日 (nan-nichi) = what day of the month.
🗓️
来週の月曜日はどうですか？
Raishuu no getsuyoubi wa dou desu ka?
How about next Monday?
来週 (raishuu) = next week. 今週 (konshuu) = this week. 先週 (senshuu) = last week.
✅
〜は空いていますか？
[time]-wa aite imasu ka?
Is [time] available?
空いている (aite iru) = available / open. Very useful for checking availability.
🏥
何時から何時まで開いていますか？
Nan-ji kara nan-ji made aite imasu ka?
What are your opening hours?
から (kara) = from · まで (made) = until. Very useful for checking shop hours.

Culture note
🌸
Japan's own calendar era system — 和暦 (wareki)
Japan uses its own era-based calendar alongside the Western one. Each new emperor's reign starts a new era — the current era is 令和 (Reiwa), which began in May 2019 when Emperor Naruhito ascended the throne. So 2025 is 令和7年 (Reiwa 7-nen). You'll see this on official documents, receipts, and government forms in Japan. Don't be surprised — it's not a separate calendar, just a different way to label the year.

Lesson 4 · Korean verbs — the big unlock
How Korean verb conjugation works — it's more logical than you think

All Korean verbs in the dictionary end in 다 (da). To conjugate, you remove 다 and add an ending. The most important endings for beginners are the polite present, polite past, and question form.

The golden rule: Korean verbs change based on (1) whether the verb stem's last vowel is 아/오 (bright vowels) or anything else (dark vowels), and (2) the formality level. For now, we focus on the polite informal level (해요체 — haeyoche) which ends in 아요/어요. This is what you'll use 90% of the time in everyday speech.

The 3 essential verb endings:

Tense / type Ending Example with 먹다 (to eat) Meaning
Present (polite) 아요 / 어요 먹어요meogeoyo (I/you/they) eat
Past (polite) 았어요 / 었어요 먹었어요meogeosseoyo ate
Question (polite) 아요? / 어요? 먹어요?meogeoyo? Do (you) eat? / Are you eating?
Negative (polite) 안 + verb 안 먹어요an meogeoyo Don't eat / Not eating
Bright vs dark vowels (모음 조화): If the verb stem's last vowel is ㅏ or ㅗ (bright), use 아요. For all other vowels (dark), use 어요. Example: 가다 (to go) → 가요 (bright ㅏ). 먹다 (to eat) → 먹어요 (dark ㅓ). With practice, this becomes instinctive — Korean speakers naturally "feel" which sounds right.

Lesson 6 · Full sentences using verbs + time words
Put it all together — these are real, natural Korean sentences
🍜
어제 한국 음식을 먹었어요.
Eoje hanguk eumsigul meogeosseoyo.
I ate Korean food yesterday.
어제 = yesterday · 한국 음식 = Korean food · 먹었어요 = ate (past)
🚇
내일 서울에 가요.
Naeil Seoul-e gayo.
I'm going to Seoul tomorrow.
내일 = tomorrow · 서울에 = to Seoul · 가요 = going (present)
☕
오늘 커피를 마셔요?
Oneul keopireul masyeoyo?
Are you drinking coffee today?
오늘 = today · 커피를 = coffee (object) · 마셔요? = drinking? (question)
🛍️
이번 주에 뭐 해요?
Ibeon ju-e mwo haeyo?
What are you doing this week?
이번 주 = this week · 뭐 = what · 해요 = doing (present)
Culture note
🌺
공휴일 — Korea's colorful public holiday calendar
Korea has some fascinating national holidays. Chuseok (추석) — the autumn harvest festival in September/October — is the most important family holiday, like Thanksgiving and Christmas combined. Seollal (설날) is Lunar New Year, when the whole country travels home. On these holidays, cities empty out dramatically and transport is packed days in advance. If you're visiting during these periods, book everything well ahead — and expect to see beautiful traditional clothing (한복, hanbok) everywhere.
