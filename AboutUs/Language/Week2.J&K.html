Lesson 2 · Key pronunciation rules
3 rules that will make you sound much more natural
1. Long vowels
おかあさん
Double vowels (aa, oo, uu) are held for twice as long. okāsan (mother) — the "aa" is a long "a". Getting this wrong changes the meaning!
2. Silent vowels
す · で·す
The vowels い and う are often whispered or silent between voiceless consonants. desu sounds more like "des". Very common at the end of sentences.
3. Double consonants
きって
A tiny っ (small tsu) before a consonant means pause briefly before it — like a tiny stop. kitte (stamp) has a beat of silence before the "t".
4. Flat pitch
ありがとう
Japanese doesn't have the heavy stress English does. Each syllable gets roughly equal time and energy — like a steady drumbeat. a-ri-ga-to-u, not "a-ri-GA-to".
Lesson 3 · Survival phrases
10 phrases to use from the moment you arrive in Japan
👋
すみません
Sumimasen
Excuse me / Sorry / Pardon me
The single most useful word in Japan. Use it to get someone's attention, to apologise mildly, or to squeeze past someone.
🙏
ありがとうございます
Arigatou gozaimasu
Thank you very much (formal)
Use this in shops, restaurants, and with strangers. Casual version: ありがとう (arigatou).
🍽️
これをください
Kore wo kudasai
This one, please
Point at a menu item or product and say this. Works everywhere. こ·れ·を·く·だ·さ·い — all from characters you know!
💴
いくらですか
Ikura desu ka?
How much is it?
Essential for shopping. The か at the end turns any statement into a question — remember this pattern.
🚻
トイレはどこですか
Toire wa doko desu ka?
Where is the toilet?
トイレ is a katakana loanword from English "toilet" — you'll learn katakana in Week 3.
🤔
わかりません
Wakarimasen
I don't understand
Honest and polite. Locals will appreciate it and often try to help differently.
🗣️
もう一度お願いします
Mou ichido onegaishimasu
Please say that again
Very useful in conversations. Pair it with わかりません if needed.
🏨
〜はありますか
[thing] wa arimasu ka?
Do you have [thing]?
Slot any word into 〜. E.g. "みず (mizu) はありますか" = "Do you have water?"
✅
はい / いいえ
Hai / Iie
Yes / No
はい (hai) is also used like "I see" or "understood" — you'll hear it constantly in conversation.
🔍
〜はどこですか
[place] wa doko desu ka?
Where is [place]?
E.g. "えき (eki = station) はどこですか" = "Where is the station?" Your most-used direction phrase.
Culture note
🍱
いただきます & ごちそうさま (Itadakimasu & Gochisousama)
Before eating, Japanese people say いただきます ("I humbly receive") and after eating ごちそうさまでした ("It was a feast"). These aren't prayers — they're expressions of gratitude to everyone involved in making the meal. Using them in a restaurant or someone's home earns enormous respect.

Lesson 1 · New consonants
5 more essential consonants — including the aspirated sounds

Korean has a unique feature: many consonants come in three versions — basic, aspirated (extra breath), and tense (more forceful). This week we add the aspirated versions.

ㅇ
silent / ng
silent at start, "ng" at end
ㅈ
j
like "j" in "jump"
ㅎ
h
like "h" in "house"
ㅋ
k (aspirated)
ㄱ + extra breath
ㅌ
t (aspirated)
ㄷ + extra breath
Key insight — ㅇ is a placeholder: Every syllable block needs a consonant. When a syllable starts with a pure vowel sound, ㅇ fills the consonant slot silently. For example: 아 = "a", 이 = "i". At the end of a block, it becomes an "ng" sound — e.g. 강 (gang).
Lesson 2 · Compound vowels
5 compound vowels made by combining basic ones

These look complex but they're just two vowels blended together. They appear in very common words, so recognising them early helps a lot.

ㅐ
ae
like "e" in "bed"
ㅔ
e
similar to ㅐ — nearly same sound today
ㅘ
wa
ㅗ + ㅏ blended
ㅢ
ui / eui
tricky — practice carefully
ㅒ
yae
like "yeh"
ㅐ and ㅔ sound almost identical in modern Korean. Native speakers sometimes mix up their spelling too — don't stress over the difference. Just recognise both.
Lesson 3 · Survival phrases
10 phrases to use from the moment you arrive in Korea
👋
안녕하세요
Annyeonghaseyo
Hello (formal)
The standard greeting for anyone older or in a service setting. Your most-used phrase in Korea. Slight bow included.
🙏
감사합니다
Gamsahamnida
Thank you (formal)
From Week 1 — you already know this one! Casual version: 고마워요 (gomawoyo).
🍽️
이거 주세요
Igeo juseyo
This one, please
Point and say this anywhere — menus, shops, street food stalls. 이거 = "this thing", 주세요 = "please give me".
💴
얼마예요?
Eolmayeyo?
How much is it?
Essential for markets and street food. The answer will often be in numbers — we cover those in Week 4.
🚻
화장실이 어디예요?
Hwajangsili eodiyeyo?
Where is the bathroom?
화장실 = toilet/bathroom. 어디예요 = "where is it?" — memorise this pair and use it anywhere.
🤔
모르겠어요
Moreugeseoyo
I don't know / I'm not sure
Polite and honest. Koreans respond well to humility — this phrase shows self-awareness.
🗣️
다시 말씀해 주세요
Dasi malsseum-hae juseyo
Please say that again
Very polite phrasing — 말씀 is the honorific form of "words/speech". Use freely without embarrassment.
🚇
〜이/가 어디예요?
[place]-i/ga eodiyeyo?
Where is [place]?
Use 이 after a consonant-ending word, 가 after a vowel-ending word. E.g. "지하철역이 어디예요?" = "Where is the subway station?"
🍜
맛있어요!
Masisseoyo!
It's delicious!
Say this after eating and watch the reaction — Koreans are immensely proud of their food. Instant connection builder.
📸
사진 찍어도 돼요?
Sajin jjigeodo dwaeyo?
May I take a photo?
Always ask before photographing people or in temples/markets. 사진 = photo, 찍어도 돼요 = "is it okay to take?"
Culture note
🍺
Pouring drinks for others (술 문화 — sul munhwa)
In Korean culture, you never pour your own drink when dining with others — you pour for everyone else, and someone pours for you. It's a sign of care and respect. The same applies to water and tea. If you notice someone's glass is empty, fill it — you'll immediately earn goodwill at the table.
