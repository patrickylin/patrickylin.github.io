Lesson 1 · Where to shop in Japan
6 types of shops — every visitor should know these
🏬
デパート
depāto
Department store
Multi-floor shopping palaces. Food hall (デパ地下, depachika) in the basement is a must. Brands floor by floor — cosmetics and food at basement level, fashion upstairs.
💴
百円ショップ
hyaku-en shoppu
100-yen shop (Daiso, Seria)
Everything for ¥100–300. Incredible quality for the price — stationery, kitchenware, snacks, travel accessories. Daiso is the most famous. Don't leave Japan without visiting one.
🎮
電気街
denkigai
Electronics district (Akihabara)
Tokyo's Akihabara is the world's densest concentration of electronics, anime goods, and gaming. Osaka has Den-Den Town. Prices on electronics can be excellent especially for cameras.
🏪
ドラッグストア
doraggu sutoa
Drug store (Matsumoto Kiyoshi etc.)
Japanese drug stores sell cosmetics, medicines, snacks and household goods at great prices. Popular with tourists for buying Japanese skincare and beauty products.
👘
着物屋
kimono-ya
Kimono / traditional clothing shop
Many shops in Kyoto and Asakusa offer kimono rental (着物レンタル, kimono rentaru) by the day — a beautiful way to experience traditional Japan.
🛒
スーパー
sūpā
Supermarket
Hit the supermarket (especially after 7pm) for dramatically discounted 弁当 (bento) and 刺身 (sashimi). Stickers say 半額 (hangaku = half price) or 30%引き (30% off).

Lesson 4 · Shopping phrases
From browsing to buying — everything you need
👀
見ているだけです
Mite iru dake desu
I'm just looking
Staff will often greet you immediately — this phrase lets you browse peacefully without feeling obligated to buy.
🔍
〜を探しています
[item]-wo sagashite imasu
I'm looking for [item]
探す (sagasu) = to look for/search. 〜を探しています is natural and polite. Staff will immediately try to help.
👕
〜色の〜はありますか？
[colour]-iro no [item]-wa arimasu ka?
Do you have this in [colour]?
e.g. 青色のシャツはありますか？(Do you have a blue shirt?) 色 (iro) = colour. Works for size too: Mサイズはありますか？
📐
試着してもいいですか？
Shichaku shite mo ii desu ka?
May I try this on?
Always ask before entering a changing room. Staff will direct you to 試着室 (shichakushitsu = fitting room).
💳
カードで払えますか？
Kādo de haraemasu ka?
Can I pay by card?
払う (harau) = to pay. カード (kādo) = card. 現金 (genkin) = cash. Smaller shops may be cash-only.
🎁
プレゼント用に包んでもらえますか？
Purezento-yō ni tsunde moraemasu ka?
Could you gift-wrap this?
Japanese gift wrapping (包装, hōsō) is an art form — done meticulously and usually free. Just ask!
🔖
割引はありますか？
Waribiki wa arimasu ka?
Is there a discount?
割引 (waribiki) = discount. セール (sēru) = sale. 半額 (hangaku) = half price. Note: haggling is not a Japanese custom — only ask in market or outlet contexts.
🧾
免税はできますか？
Menzei wa dekimasu ka?
Can I get tax-free shopping?
Tourists can claim consumption tax (消費税, shōhizei — currently 10%) back on purchases over ¥5,000 at participating shops. Look for the 免税 (tax-free) logo.
Lesson 5 · Tax-free shopping in Japan
Save 10% on almost everything — if you know how
🧾
消費税免除 (shōhizei menjō) — Consumption tax exemption Foreign visitors can claim back Japan's 10% consumption tax on purchases of ¥5,000 or more (before tax) at registered shops. Show your passport, fill in a short form, and the tax is either deducted immediately or refunded at the airport. Look for the 免税 (tax-free) or "Japan Tax-Free Shop" sticker at the entrance. Department stores, electronics shops, and drug stores almost always participate.
Tax-free vocabulary: 消費税 (shōhizei) = consumption tax · 免税 (menzei) = tax-free · パスポートを見せてください (pasupōto wo misete kudasai) = Please show your passport · 免税カウンター (menzei kauntā) = tax-free counter.
Culture note
🛍️
いらっしゃいませ (irasshaimase) — the sound of Japanese shopping
The moment you step into any Japanese shop, you'll hear いらっしゃいませ — a formal welcome meaning "welcome, honoured customer." You don't need to reply — just a nod or smile is fine. Staff may follow you attentively which can feel different from Western shopping culture, but this is simply dedicated service (おもてなし, omotenashi). Another quirk: cashiers will count your change back to you out loud, double-bagging every purchase and placing it on the counter rather than handing it to you directly — a sign of hygiene-conscious respect.

Lesson 1 · Where to shop in Korea
6 essential shopping destinations
🏙️
명동
Myeongdong
Myeongdong — Seoul's shopping heart
The most famous shopping district in Korea. K-beauty shops, street food, international brands, and endless stalls. Prices are tourist-facing but selection is unbeatable.
💄
올리브영
Ollibeu-yeong
Olive Young — Korea's K-beauty chain
Think of it as Korea's Sephora + drug store combined. Every K-beauty brand you've ever heard of is here. Best prices in the country. App available in English. Locations everywhere.
🏬
백화점
baekwajeom
Department store (Lotte, Shinsegae)
Lotte and Shinsegae department stores rival Tokyo's finest. High-end fashion, gourmet food halls, and dedicated tourist service desks. Tax-refund counters on site.
🧺
시장
sijang
Traditional market
Gwangjang Market (광장시장) for street food and textiles, Namdaemun (남대문) for wholesale goods. Real prices, real Korea. Bargaining (흥정, heungjeong) is acceptable here.
🛍️
면세점
myeonsejeom
Duty-free shop
Korea's duty-free shops (at airports and downtown) are among the world's largest. Popular for luxury goods, cosmetics, and Korean spirits like 한정판 (limited edition) soju.
📱
용산 전자상가
Yongsan jeonjasangga
Yongsan Electronics Market
Seoul's answer to Akihabara — floors of electronics, computer parts, gaming gear, and phone accessories. Wholesale prices if you know how to negotiate.

Lesson 3 · Shopping phrases
Browse, ask, and buy with confidence
👀
그냥 구경하는 거예요
Geunyang gugyeonghaneun geoyeyo
I'm just browsing
그냥 = just. 구경하다 = to browse/look around. Staff in Korean shops can be very attentive — this phrase gives you space to explore.
🔍
〜 있어요?
[item] isseoyo?
Do you have [item]?
The simplest way to ask for something. e.g. 빨간 원피스 있어요? (Do you have a red dress?) 있어요 = do you have / is there.
👕
다른 색상 있어요?
Dareun saeksang isseoyo?
Do you have this in another colour?
다른 = different/another. 색상 = colour. You can also say 다른 사이즈 있어요? (Do you have another size?)
📐
입어 봐도 돼요?
Ibeo bwado dwaeyo?
May I try this on?
입다 (ipda) = to wear/put on. 봐도 돼요 = is it okay to try. 피팅룸 (pitting-rum) = fitting room.
💰
깎아 주실 수 있어요?
Kkakka jusil su isseoyo?
Can you give me a discount?
깎다 (kkakda) = to reduce/cut (price). Only use this at traditional markets (시장) — not in regular shops or malls. Very effective at Namdaemun and Gwangjang.
💳
카드 돼요?
Kadeu dwaeyo?
Do you take card?
Korea is one of the world's most cashless societies — almost everywhere accepts card. But it's worth checking at small market stalls.
🧾
영수증 주세요
Yeongsujeung juseyo
Please give me a receipt
영수증 (yeongsujeung) = receipt. Keep all receipts if you plan to claim a tax refund at the airport.

Lesson 4 · Comparison grammar
A보다 B가 더 〜 — "B is more ~ than A"

Korean comparisons use a fixed structure: put the thing you're comparing against first (A보다), then what you're saying is better/bigger/cheaper (B가 더). The word order feels reversed from English — but once you see the pattern, it's very logical.

A보다 + B가 + 더 [adjective] = B is more [adjective] than A
이것보다 저것이 더 싸요.
Igeotboda jeogeosi deo ssayo.
That one is cheaper than this one.
이것 = this thing · 저것 = that thing · 더 싸요 = cheaper
A보다 + B가 + 더 [adjective]
한국이 일본보다 더 가까워요.
Hanguki Ilbonboda deo gakkaweoyo.
Korea is closer than Japan.
한국 = Korea · 일본보다 = than Japan · 더 가까워요 = is closer

Superlatives — "the most ~" using 제일 or 가장

제일 / 가장 + [adjective] = the most [adjective]
이게 제일 싸요.
Ige jeil ssayo.
This one is the cheapest.
제일 and 가장 are interchangeable. 제일 is slightly more casual, 가장 slightly more formal.
어느 게 제일 좋아요?
Eoneu ge jeil joayo?
Which one is the best?
어느 게 = which one. 좋다 (jota) = good. An incredibly useful shopping phrase.
Shopping comparisons in action: 이 립스틱보다 저 립스틱이 더 예뻐요 (this lipstick is prettier than that lipstick). 어느 색이 제일 인기 있어요? (Which colour is most popular?) — 인기 있다 (ingi itda) = to be popular, a great phrase to ask staff for recommendations.
Culture note
💄
K-beauty (케이뷰티) — Korea's global skincare revolution
Korea's 10-step skincare routine transformed the global beauty industry. The philosophy centres on skin health first — layers of hydration (toner → essence → serum → moisturiser) rather than covering imperfections. Key concepts to know in shops: 미백 (mibaek = brightening), 보습 (boseum = moisturising), 수분 (subun = hydration), 민감성 피부 (mingamseon pibu = sensitive skin), 지성 (jiseong = oily), 건성 (geonseong = dry). Staff at Olive Young and 미샤 (Missha) speak enough English to help — but knowing these words opens up the entire product range to you.
