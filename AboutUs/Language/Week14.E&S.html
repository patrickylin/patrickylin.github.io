Lesson 1 · Collocations — words that belong together
Native speakers choose word partners automatically — learners must learn them deliberately

A collocation is a pair (or group) of words that naturally go together in a language. While the grammar may be technically correct with other combinations, native speakers would find them unnatural. "Do homework" ✅ — "make homework" ❌. Both are logical, but only one sounds right to a native ear. Collocations cannot be translated word for word — they must be learned as units.

Make vs do — the eternal collocation battle: The make/do distinction is one of the most persistent challenges in English. A rough guide: make = creating, producing, causing something to exist (make a cake, make noise, make a mistake — the mistake now exists). do = performing an activity or task (do exercise, do your homework, do research — the activity is being carried out). But many common collocations break this logic — "do damage", "make friends", "take an exam" — these simply must be memorised.

Lesson 3 · Formal vs informal register
The same idea expressed three ways — choosing the right level for the context

Register is the level of formality appropriate to a situation. English has a wide range — from highly formal academic writing to very casual spoken slang. Native speakers switch between registers automatically; learners must develop this same flexibility. Using the wrong register is often more jarring than a grammar mistake.

The Latin-Germanic register divide: English has a peculiar dual vocabulary — Latin/French-origin words tend to be formal (commence, obtain, sufficient, inquire), while Old English/Germanic words tend to be informal (start, get, enough, ask). This happened because French-speaking Normans became England's ruling class after 1066 — their French vocabulary became the language of law, administration, and prestige. Understanding this explains why "help" (Germanic) feels warmer than "assist" (Latin), and why "buy" (Germanic) sounds more casual than "purchase" (Latin/French).

Culture note
🎭
Idioms as cultural windows — what English metaphors reveal about British culture
Every language's idioms reveal what that culture values and fears. English is rich with sporting metaphors — "hit the ball out of the park", "move the goalposts", "a level playing field", "it's not cricket" (British: it's unfair) — reflecting the deep cultural role of sport. Naval and maritime idioms reflect Britain's seafaring history: "plain sailing", "taken aback", "cut and run", "know the ropes", "above board". Weather idioms reflect the British obsession with climate: "under the weather", "right as rain", "steal someone's thunder", "every cloud has a silver lining". Learning a language's idiom clusters gives you a map of its history and values — and makes you sound native in a way that no grammar lesson alone can achieve.

Lesson 1 · Falsos amigos — false friends
Words that look the same in English and Spanish but mean something completely different

False friends (falsos amigos) are words that look or sound similar across two languages but have different meanings. For English speakers learning Spanish, they are a constant source of embarrassing — and sometimes very funny — errors. The closer the words look, the more dangerous they are.

sensible sensible = sensitive (emotionally) "Es muy sensible" = She is very sensitive (cries easily) sensato/a = sensible / reasonable · razonable = reasonable

The most dangerous false friend of all — embarazada: Saying "Estoy embarazada" when you mean "I am embarrassed" is a legendary learner error. If you are a woman, it means you have announced a pregnancy. The correct word for embarrassed is avergonzado/a (from vergüenza = shame/embarrassment). The humour of this error has made it the most famous false friend in Spanish teaching — and a powerful reminder that looking similar is no guide to meaning.
Lesson 2 · Diminutivos y aumentativos
The suffixes that make Spanish feel warm, vivid and emotionally alive

Spanish speakers add suffixes to nouns, adjectives, and even adverbs to express size, affection, intensity, or irony. Diminutives (-ito/-ita, -illo/-illa) make things smaller or more affectionate. Augmentatives (-ón/-ona, -azo/-aza) make things bigger or more intense. These suffixes are not just about size — they carry enormous emotional weight.

café ☕
cafecito
a nice little coffee
Affection, not necessarily small. "¿Un cafecito?" is a warm invitation.
momento ⏱️
momentito
just a moment (softer)
"Un momentito" is politer and softer than "un momento". Widely used in Latin America.
poco 🤏
poquito
a tiny little bit
"Un poquito de paciencia" = just a tiny bit of patience. Very common!
perro 🐕
perrito
perrazo
little dog / huge dog
Perrito = cute little dog. Perrazo = a massive dog (augmentative with awe/intensity).
coche 🚗
cochecito
cochazo
small/cute car / amazing car
Cochecito = baby carriage too! Cochazo = a fantastic expensive car (augmentative of admiration).
golpe 💥
golpecito
golpazo
light tap / massive blow
Golpecito = a gentle knock. Golpazo = a huge crash. The suffix reverses the intensity entirely.
libro 📖
librito
librazo / librote
little booklet / great big book
Librazo can mean an impressive/excellent book — augmentatives often imply admiration.
bueno ✅
buenísimo
buenazo
excellent / very good-natured
-ísimo = superlative of intensity (added to adjectives). Buenazo = a really good-natured person. Es un buenazo = He's a really good soul.
tarde ⏰
tardecita
tardísimo
late afternoon / extremely late
Tardecita = the pleasant late afternoon (used affectionately in Latin America). Tardísimo = incredibly late. Diminutives apply even to time words!
Regional variation in diminutives: The use of diminutives varies significantly across Spanish-speaking regions. Latin America — particularly Mexico, Colombia, and Central America — uses diminutives far more extensively and affectionately than Spain. In Mexico, ahorita (diminutive of ahora = now) is famously ambiguous — it can mean immediately, soon, in a while, or never, depending on tone. ¿Me traes un vasito de agua? (Could you bring me a little glass of water?) is softer and more polite than asking without the diminutive. Mastering diminutives is one of the fastest ways to sound natural and warm in spoken Spanish.
Lesson 3 · Conectores y marcadores del discurso
The linking expressions that make Spanish flow naturally — beyond simple "y" and "pero"

Discourse markers are the connective tissue of language — the words and phrases that link ideas, signal contrast, introduce examples, draw conclusions, and organise speech. Moving beyond y (and) and pero (but) into richer connectors is one of the clearest signs of growing proficiency.

ConnectorFunctionExample
además Moreover / furthermore / also — adding information El hotel está bien situado; además, tiene piscina. (The hotel is well located; moreover, it has a pool.)
sin embargo However / nevertheless — introducing contrast (stronger than pero) Me gusta el plan; sin embargo, creo que es demasiado caro. (I like the plan; however, I think it's too expensive.)
por lo tanto Therefore / consequently — logical conclusion No tenemos suficiente presupuesto; por lo tanto, debemos esperar. (We don't have enough budget; therefore, we must wait.)
de hecho In fact / actually — emphasising or reinforcing No es difícil; de hecho, es bastante sencillo. (It's not difficult; in fact, it's quite simple.)
es decir That is / in other words — clarifying or reformulating Habla tres idiomas; es decir, es trilingüe. (She speaks three languages; that is, she is trilingual.)
a pesar de (que) Despite / in spite of — concession A pesar del frío, salimos a caminar. (Despite the cold, we went for a walk.)
por otro lado On the other hand — presenting another perspective El trabajo es bien pagado; por otro lado, hay mucho estrés. (The job is well paid; on the other hand, there's a lot of stress.)
en cuanto a As for / regarding — changing topic or focus En cuanto al precio, podemos negociar. (As for the price, we can negotiate.)
o sea I mean / that is — casual clarification (very spoken) Es rarísimo, o sea, no lo entiendo. (It's very weird, I mean, I don't understand it.) — very colloquial
así que So / and so — informal conclusion or result No había taxi, así que cogimos el metro. (There was no taxi, so we took the metro.)
O sea — the Spanish "I mean": O sea is one of the most-used spoken discourse markers in everyday Spanish — the equivalent of English "I mean" or "like". It signals a reformulation, a clarification, or simply a conversational filler. Young Spanish speakers especially sprinkle it throughout speech: "O sea, es que no sé..." (I mean, it's just that I don't know...). Recognising it in fast native speech is as important as learning formal connectors for written language.

Culture note
❤️
El lenguaje del cariño — diminutives as emotional language in Spanish culture
In Spanish-speaking cultures, especially in Latin America, diminutives go far beyond grammar — they are the language of warmth, intimacy, and affection. A mother calls her child hijito/hijita (little child/son/daughter). A grandmother offers un cafecito as an act of love, not just a beverage. Ahorita in Mexico doesn't just mean "right now" — it carries an entire attitude toward time, relationships, and the present moment. Even favor (favour) becomes favorcito when making a small, intimate request from someone you're close to. The English equivalent would be adding "little" or "dear" to everything — which sounds strange in English but feels completely natural in Spanish. This difference reveals a cultural truth: Spanish-speaking cultures tend to express emotional closeness through language modification itself, while English cultures express it more through tone and indirect signals. Learning to use diminutives is learning a new emotional vocabulary.

The idiom universality insight: Many idioms share the same underlying image across languages — "hit the nail on the head" in English = "dar en el clavo" in Spanish = "図星をさす" (hit the bull's eye) in Japanese = "핵심을 찌르다" (pierce the core) in Korean. Different images, same concept. This reveals that human thought patterns are often universal — we all reach for metaphors of precision, accuracy, and hitting a target. Where languages diverge most vividly is in their diminutive systems: Spanish has a rich, productive suffix system that English must approximate with separate words ("little", "dear"), while Japanese and Korean use different mechanisms entirely. The diminutive is where Spanish most beautifully differs from English — it encodes warmth into the word itself.
