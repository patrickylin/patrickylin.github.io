Lesson 1 · Adjectives & adverbs
Words that describe — and the crucial difference between them

Adjectives describe nouns (things, people, places). Adverbs describe verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. English keeps them strictly separate — a distinction that Spanish, Japanese, and Korean handle very differently.

Adjective — describes a noun
Adjective + Noun / Noun + be + Adjective
Comes before the noun it modifies, or after a linking verb (be, seem, look, feel, taste, smell).
No change for gender or number — one form fits all!
a fast car · a beautiful city
The soup is hot. · She seems tired.
three interesting books (no plural -s on adj!)
Adverb — describes a verb or adjective
Most adjectives + -ly → adverb
Modifies how, when, where, or to what degree an action happens. Position is flexible — but usually after the verb or at the sentence end.
She drives quickly. (how)
He speaks very clearly. (degree + how)
I completely agree. (degree)
It's incredibly hot today. (degree + adj)
The -ly rule and its exceptions: Most adverbs add -ly to the adjective: slow→slowly · careful→carefully · happy→happily (y→ily). But several common adverbs are identical to their adjectives — no -ly: fast (adj & adv) · hard · early · late · high · low. And "good" (adj) → "well" (adv): "She is a good singer" vs "She sings well." Never say "She sings good" — one of the most frequent errors in learner English.
Lesson 2 · Comparatives & superlatives
Bigger, the biggest — comparing things in English

English forms comparatives and superlatives in two ways: with -er / -est endings (for short adjectives) or with more / most (for longer adjectives). The rule of thumb: one syllable uses -er/-est; three or more syllables use more/most; two syllables can go either way.

Base adjectiveComparative (-er / more)Superlative (-est / most)
fast faster the fastestTokyo is the fastest city in the world.
big bigger the biggestChina is the biggest country by population.
happy happier the happiestShe was the happiest person in the room.
expensive more expensive the most expensiveThis is the most expensive restaurant in town.
interesting more interesting the most interestingThat was the most interesting lecture I've heard.
good better the bestThis is the best sushi I've ever eaten.
bad worse the worstThat was the worst film I've ever seen.
far farther / further the farthest / furthestWhich station is further from the centre?
Key comparative structures:
A is -er / more ~ than B: "Tokyo is bigger than Osaka."
A is as ~ as B: "Korean food is as spicy as I expected."
A is not as ~ as B: "This hotel is not as expensive as the other one."
The -er, the -er: "The harder you work, the better the results."
Lesson 3 · Countable & uncountable nouns
A hidden grammar system that determines articles and quantity words

English nouns divide into two categories that affect the words used with them. This system doesn't exist in Japanese or Korean, and works differently in Spanish — making it a genuinely novel concept for East Asian language speakers.

Countable — can be counted
Use a/an, a number, or many/few. Can be singular or plural.
a book · three books · many books · a few books
an apple · five apples · several apples
a chair · how many chairs?

✅ Can say: one book, two books, a book
Uncountable — cannot be directly counted
No a/an, no plural -s. Use much/little. Needs a "measure word" to count portions.
water · rice · music · information
advice · furniture · luggage · weather
much water · a little milk

❌ Cannot say: a water, two rices, an information
✅ Instead say: a glass of water, two bowls of rice, a piece of information
Common uncountable traps for learners: advice (NOT "an advice") · news (NOT "a news") · information (NOT "an information") · luggage/baggage (NOT "a luggage") · furniture (NOT "a furniture") · progress (NOT "a progress") · research (NOT "a research") · work when meaning employment (NOT "a work" — but "a piece of work" ✅). These are among the most frequent errors in learner English because the corresponding words in other languages ARE countable.
Lesson 4 · Shopping phrases
Everything from browsing to buying in English
🛍️
I'm just looking, thanks.
Polite response when a shop assistant approaches
The most-used phrase in any English-speaking shop. Prevents an unwanted sales pitch while being completely polite. Equivalent: "Just browsing, thanks."
💰
How much is this / are these?
Asking the price (singular / plural)
Note the singular/plural switch: "How much is this?" (one item) vs "How much are these?" (multiple items). Also: "What's the price of...?" or "How much does it cost?"
👗
Do you have this in a different size / colour?
Asking for alternatives
Size vocabulary: extra small (XS) · small (S) · medium (M) · large (L) · extra large (XL). British English: colour. American English: color. Both understood globally.
🔄
Can I try this on? / Where are the fitting rooms?
Trying on clothing
Fitting rooms (UK) = changing rooms (UK/AUS) = dressing rooms (US). "Try on" = phrasal verb meaning to put on clothing to see if it fits. "I'll take it" = I'll buy it.
💳
I'll take it. / Could I pay by card?
Deciding to buy / payment method
Contactless (tap to pay) is now universal in the UK. "Do you accept card?" / "Is contactless OK?" Cash is declining rapidly — many UK shops are card-only since 2020.
↩️
I'd like to return / exchange this, please.
Returns and exchanges
Return = give it back for a refund. Exchange = swap it for something else. "I have the receipt." / "It doesn't fit." / "It's faulty." — reasons for returning. Most shops have a 28–30 day return policy.

Culture note
🏬
British shopping culture — queuing, politeness, and the art of the receipt
Shopping in Britain comes with unwritten social rules as firm as any grammar rule. The queue (line) is sacred — jumping it is one of the most serious social offences in British culture. Shop assistants are addressed politely and thanked even for the smallest interaction. "Cheers" (casual thank you) is ubiquitous. Bargaining over prices is not done in mainstream shops — but is perfectly acceptable in markets, car boot sales, and independent traders. The phrase "I'm just looking" is so deeply embedded that shop assistants will immediately back away upon hearing it. And the receipt (always kept!) is essential — British consumer law gives strong rights to returns and refunds, making the paper trail genuinely valuable. Unlike Japan (where wrapping and presentation are paramount), British shopping culture prizes efficiency, practicality, and value for money above all.

Lesson 1 · Gustar — the verb that works backwards
The most surprising grammar pattern in Spanish — and one of the most beautiful

Gustar means "to please" or "to be pleasing to" — not "to like". The subject of gustar is the thing being liked, not the person doing the liking. This reversal trips up English speakers because English puts the liker first, but Spanish puts the liked thing first.

How gustar works — the structure reversed:
me
+
gusta
+
el café
→
Me gusta el café.
I like coffee. (lit. "Coffee pleases me.")
me
+
gustan
+
los gatos
→
Me gustan los gatos.
I like cats. (lit. "Cats please me.") — plural noun → gustan!
The two forms of gustar: gusta — when the subject (the liked thing) is singular or an infinitive: "Me gusta el chocolate" · "Me gusta nadar". gustan — when the subject is plural: "Me gustan las películas de acción" · "Me gustan los idiomas." The person doing the liking is NEVER the subject — they appear as an indirect object pronoun (me, te, le, nos, os, les).
PersonIndirect object pronounExampleEnglish
ImeMe gusta el jazz.I like jazz.
You (informal)te¿Te gustan las tapas?Do you like tapas?

WenosNos gustan los idiomas.We like languages.
You all (Spain)os¿Os gusta la paella?Do you all like paella?
They / You all (formal)lesLes encanta el flamenco.They love flamenco.

Other verbs that work like gustar — the "backwards" verb family:

encantar — to love (stronger than gustar): Me encanta el sushi. (I love sushi.)
molestar — to bother / to annoy: Me molesta el ruido. (Noise bothers me.)
interesar — to interest: ¿Te interesa la historia? (Are you interested in history?)
doler — to hurt / ache: Me duele la cabeza. (My head hurts.)
quedar — to fit / suit: ¿Te queda bien? (Does it fit you well?)
faltar — to be missing / to lack: Me faltan cinco euros. (I'm five euros short.)
Lesson 2 · Adjective agreement — concuerda con el sustantivo
Every Spanish adjective must match its noun in gender AND number

This is one of the most fundamental — and most practised — aspects of Spanish. Every adjective must agree with the noun it modifies in both gender (masculine/feminine) and number (singular/plural). English adjectives never change — "a red car", "red cars", "a red house" all use "red". Spanish changes the adjective every time.

Masc. Singular
rojo
el coche rojo
Fem. Singular
roja
la casa roja
Masc. Plural
rojos
los coches rojos
Fem. Plural
rojas
las casas rojas
Adjectives ending in -e or a consonant — no feminine change: Adjectives ending in -e have only singular/plural forms: grande→grandes (no gender change). Adjectives ending in a consonant also don't change for gender: azul→azules · joven→jóvenes · feliz→felices. Only adjectives ending in -o change to -a for feminine. Nationalities ending in a consonant DO add -a: español→española · francés→francesa · inglés→inglesa.

Position matters — before or after the noun changes meaning!

After the noun (most adjectives — distinguishing/classifying):
un hombre pobre = a poor man (financially poor)
una ciudad grande = a large city

Before the noun (emphatic / emotional / certain adjectives):
un pobre hombre = a poor man (pitiable — changes meaning!)
una gran ciudad = a great city (grande→gran before noun = great, not large)

Always before the noun: numbers · possessives · demonstratives · bueno/malo (can precede — bueno→buen / malo→mal)

Lesson 4 · De compras — shopping in Spanish
From browsing to bargaining — everything you need in a Spanish shop
👀
Solo estoy mirando, gracias.
/ˈsolo esˈtoi miˈɾando ˈɡɾasjas/
I'm just looking, thank you.
The essential phrase to buy yourself peace in any Spanish shop. Uses estar (estoy mirando — present continuous) because it's a temporary ongoing action. Utterly universal.
💰
¿Cuánto cuesta esto? / ¿Cuánto vale?
/ˈkwanto ˈkwesta/ · /ˈkwanto ˈβale/
How much does this cost?
Costar and valer both mean "to cost". ¿Cuánto cuesta? = How much does it cost? ¿Cuánto cuesta todo? = How much is everything? ¿Tiene algo más barato? = Do you have something cheaper?
👗
¿Lo tiene en otra talla / en otro color?
/lo ˈtjene en ˈotɾa ˈtaʎa/
Do you have it in another size / colour?
La talla = clothing size (S, M, L) · el número = shoe size. ¿Me lo puedo probar? = Can I try it on? El probador = fitting room. ¿Me queda bien? = Does it suit/fit me?
🏷️
¿Está en oferta? / ¿Tiene descuento?
/esˈta en oˈfeɾta/ · /ˈtjene desˈkwento/
Is it on offer? / Do you have a discount?
Las rebajas = the sales (January and July in Spain — huge shopping events). La oferta = offer/deal. El descuento = discount. ¿Acepta tarjeta? = Do you accept card?
🛒
Me lo llevo. / ¿Me puede dar una bolsa?
/me lo ˈʎeβo/ · /me ˈpweðe ˈðaɾ ˈuna ˈbolsa/
I'll take it. / Could you give me a bag?
Me lo llevo = I'll take it (lit. "I'll take it with me"). Bags (bolsas) are charged in Spain — usually €0.05–0.10 each, like much of Europe. Bring your own reusable bag (bolsa reutilizable)!
↩️
Quisiera devolver esto. Tengo el ticket.
/kiˈsjeɾa ðeβolˈβeɾ ˈesto/
I'd like to return this. I have the receipt.
Quisiera (I would like — conditional of querer) is beautifully polite for requests. Devolver = to return (something). El ticket / el recibo / la factura = receipt. Consumer rights in Spain are strong — keep your receipt!

Culture note
🛍️
Las rebajas — Spain's legendary January and July sales
Spain's rebajas (sales) are a national institution. They begin officially on January 7th (the day after Three Kings Day — Reyes) and in July (the day after the summer school term ends). Unlike rolling promotions common in the UK or US, Spain's rebajas are concentrated events — and Spaniards take them seriously. Major department stores (El Corte Inglés, Zara, Mango) see queues before opening. Discounts can reach 50–70% on genuine stock. The January rebajas are particularly beloved — a tradition of treating yourself after the Christmas gifting season, when tú mereces algo (you deserve something). Learning to navigate a Spanish sale — "¿Hay rebajas?" (Are there sales on?), "¿Cuánto es con el descuento?" (How much is it with the discount?), "¿Está incluido en las rebajas?" (Is it included in the sale?) — is as culturally important as any grammar lesson.

The adjective agreement divide: Japanese, Korean, and English all have zero adjective agreement — the adjective never changes regardless of the noun. Spanish stands apart — every adjective must match in gender and number. This is one of the clearest structural differences between East Asian languages + English on one side, and the Romance languages on the other. Interestingly, Japanese 好き (suki = liked) works similarly to gustar — the liking is expressed as an adjective applied to the liked object, not as a verb with the liker as subject!
