Lesson 1 · Family vocabulary — the in-group / out-group distinction
Japanese has TWO sets of family words — one for your family, one for others'

This is one of the most fascinating quirks of Japanese. When talking about your own family you use humble words; when talking about someone else's family you use honorific words. This reflects the core Japanese value of humbling yourself while elevating others.

Lesson 3 · Relationship & love phrases
Expressions of care, affection, and connection
💕
好きです
Suki desu
I like you / I love you (mild)
The standard way to express romantic interest in Japanese. Less intense than 愛している — 好き is used for everything from confessing a crush to saying you enjoy something.
❤️
愛しています
Aishite imasu
I love you (deep, profound)
Much stronger and rarer than 好き. Reserved for committed, serious relationships. Japanese couples rarely say this casually — it carries enormous weight. Often expressed through actions, not words.
😊
あなたといると楽しいです
Anata to iru to tanoshii desu
I have fun when I'm with you
A natural, warm expression of enjoying someone's company — often more meaningful than a direct "I love you" in Japanese culture.
🤗
いつもありがとう
Itsumo arigatō
Thank you, as always
いつも (itsumo) = always. A deeply warm expression said to someone who is consistently there for you — family, partner, or close friend.
💌
付き合ってください
Tsukiatte kudasai
Please go out with me / Will you date me?
The formal way to ask someone to be your girlfriend/boyfriend in Japan. 付き合う (tsukiau) = to go out with. This is considered a serious, meaningful confession (告白, kokuhaku).
🌸
あなたのことが心配です
Anata no koto ga shinpai desu
I'm worried about you
〜のことが心配 = worried about ~. Expressing concern is a key form of affection in Japanese culture — showing you're thinking about someone.
💪
頑張ってね
Ganbatte ne
Do your best / Hang in there
One of the most-used encouraging phrases in Japanese — said to anyone facing a challenge. 頑張る (ganbaru) = to do one's best/persevere. ね softens it to a warm, caring tone.
Lesson 4 · Grammar — 〜たい (tai-form)
"I want to ~" — expressing desires and wishes

The たい-form attaches to the verb stem to express what you want to do. It conjugates like an い-adjective — so you can make it past tense (たかった = wanted to), negative (たくない = don't want to), and more.

Formation: Remove ます from the polite verb form, add たい. e.g. 食べます → 食べたい (want to eat) · 行きます → 行きたい (want to go) · 会います → 会いたい (want to meet). In polite speech: add です → 食べたいです.
Dictionary 〜たい form Meaning & example sentence
行くiku (go) 行きたいikitai Want to go日本に行きたいです。= I want to go to Japan.
食べるtaberu (eat) 食べたいtabetai Want to eatラーメンが食べたいです。= I want to eat ramen.
会うau (meet) 会いたいaitai Want to meet / Miss (someone)あなたに会いたいです。= I want to see you / I miss you.
話すhanasu (speak) 話したいhanashitai Want to speakもっと話したいです。= I want to talk more.
知るshiru (know) 知りたいshiritai Want to knowもっとあなたのことを知りたいです。= I want to know more about you.
帰るkaeru (return home) 帰りたいkaeritai Want to go home早く帰りたいです。= I want to go home soon.

たい-form tenses — it conjugates like an adjective:

食べたい→ 食べたいです (present want) → 食べたかったです (wanted to) → 食べたくないです (don't want to)
あなたに会いたかったです。
Anata ni aitakatta desu.
I wanted to see you. / I've missed you.
One of the most heartfelt sentences in Japanese — past tense たかった conveys longing. Perfect for reunions.

Culture note
💝
愛の表現 — How love is expressed in Japan
Japanese people express love differently from many Western cultures — less through words, more through actions. The concept of 気遣い (kizukai = thoughtful consideration for others) means that quietly making someone's coffee, remembering their preferences, or showing up when they need you carries far more emotional weight than saying "I love you." The phrase 大切にします (taisetsu ni shimasu = I will cherish you) is considered deeply romantic — far more so than a casual 愛している. When a Japanese person says 一緒にいると安心する (issho ni iru to anshin suru = I feel at ease when I'm with you), that is perhaps the highest form of romantic expression in the culture.

Lesson 3 · Love & relationship phrases in Korean
Expressions of affection — from casual to heartfelt
사랑해요 💕
saranghaeyo
I love you (polite)
The classic expression of love — 사랑 (sarang) means love. Casual: 사랑해 (saranghae). Very commonly said between couples, and also between close friends and family in Korean culture — less weighted than the English equivalent.
💙
좋아해요
joahaeyo
I like you (romantic interest)
좋아하다 = to like. The step before 사랑해요 — used to confess feelings. 좋아해요 is warm and genuine without the intensity of 사랑해요.
🥺
보고 싶어요
bogo sipeoyo
I miss you / I want to see you
보다 (to see) + 고 싶다 (want to). One of the most used and most heartfelt phrases in Korean — said to partners, family, and close friends equally.
😊
같이 있으면 행복해요
gachi isseumyeon haengbokhaeyo
I'm happy when I'm with you
같이 (gachi) = together + -(으)면 (if/when) + 행복해요 (I'm happy). Uses grammar from Week 9! A beautifully natural expression of happiness in someone's company.
💪
힘내요!
himnaeyo!
Cheer up! / Hang in there!
힘 (him) = strength + 내다 (to produce). The Korean equivalent of 頑張って — said to anyone facing difficulty. Shows deep care and solidarity.
💍
사귀어요
saguieoyo
We are dating / going out
사귀다 (saguida) = to date/go out. 사귀자 (saguja) = "let's date" — the casual proposal to start a relationship. 사귄 지 얼마나 됐어요? = How long have you been dating?
🤗
항상 고마워요
hangsang gomawoyo
Thank you, always
항상 (hangsang) = always. 고마워요 (gomawoyo) = thank you (casual-warm). Deeply touching when said to someone consistently in your life.
Lesson 4 · Grammar — -(으)ㄹ 것 같다
"It seems like ~ / I think it will ~" — guesses, predictions, and impressions

-(으)ㄹ 것 같다 expresses a guess, prediction, or subjective impression. It's one of the most natural-sounding patterns in Korean — native speakers use it constantly to soften statements, share opinions, and express uncertainty politely.

Formation: Verb/adjective stem + (으)ㄹ 것 같다 for future/present guesses (after consonant: 을 것 같다, after vowel/ㄹ: ㄹ 것 같다). For past guesses: verb + (은/ㄴ) 것 같다. 것 같다 conjugates like 하다: 것 같아요 (polite), 것 같아 (casual).

Future / present guesses:

Verb stem+ (으)ㄹ 것 같아요= "I think it will ~" / "It seems like ~"
비가 올 것 같아요.
Biga ol geot gatayo.
It looks like it's going to rain.
오다 (to come/rain) → 올 것 같아요. A future prediction based on evidence (dark clouds).
이 영화 재미있을 것 같아요.
I yeonghwa jaemiisseul geot gatayo.
This movie seems like it'll be interesting.
재미있다 → 재미있을 것 같아요. An impression before experiencing something.
그 사람 좋아할 것 같아요.
Geu saram joahal geot gatayo.
I think you'll like that person.
좋아하다 → 좋아할 것 같아요. Predicting someone else's reaction.

Past guesses — "it seems like ~ happened":

Verb stem+ (은/ㄴ) 것 같아요= "It seems like ~ happened"
그 사람 많이 힘든 것 같아요.
Geu saram mani himdeun geot gatayo.
That person seems to be having a hard time.
힘들다 (to be difficult/hard) → 힘든 것 같아요. A compassionate observation — very natural when showing empathy.
피곤한 것 같아요. 좀 쉬세요.
Pigonhan geot gatayo. Jom swiseyo.
You seem tired. Please rest a bit.
피곤하다 → 피곤한 것 같아요. Softening an observation with 것 같아요 makes it caring rather than blunt.
Why it sounds natural: Using 것 같아요 softens statements — it's the difference between "You're tired" (blunt) and "You seem tired" (caring). Koreans use it constantly to express opinions without being imposing: 맛있을 것 같아요 (it seems like it'll be delicious) rather than 맛있어요 (it's delicious) before tasting. It's one of the key patterns that makes your Korean sound fluent and socially aware.

-(으)ㄹ 것 같다 in relationships and emotions:

잘 될 것 같아요
jal doel geot gatayo
I think it'll work out well
잘 되다 = to go well. Reassuring someone about their situation.
좋아하는 것 같아요
joahaneun geot gatayo

The classic K-drama friend observation!
많이 그리울 것 같아요
mani geuriul geot gatayo
I think I'll miss you a lot
그립다 (to miss) → 그리울 것 같아요. Said when saying goodbye.
괜찮을 것 같아요
gwaenchaneul geot gatayo
I think it'll be okay
괜찮다 (to be okay) → 괜찮을 것 같아요. Comforting and reassuring.
Culture note
💝
빼빼로 데이와 발렌타인 데이 — Korea's gift-giving love holidays
Korea has multiple "couple holidays" throughout the year — one for every month on the 14th, in fact. The most famous: 발렌타인 데이 (Valentine's Day, Feb 14) where women give men chocolate, and 화이트 데이 (White Day, March 14) where men return the gesture with candy. But the most uniquely Korean is 빼빼로 데이 (Pepero Day, November 11) — 11/11 looks like four Pepero sticks — where friends, couples, and even colleagues exchange these long chocolate biscuit sticks. The date 11/11 is also the most popular wedding date in Korea. Beyond romantic love, Korean culture expresses deep care through 밥은 먹었어? (bap-eun meogeosseo? = "Have you eaten?") — asking whether someone has eaten is one of the most common ways Koreans show they care.
