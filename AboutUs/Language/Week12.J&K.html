Lesson 1 · Essential sightseeing vocabulary
8 must-know sites and what makes each unique
⛩️
神社
jinja
Shinto shrine
Dedicated to Shinto deities (kami). Marked by a torii gate (鳥居). Visitors purify hands at the temizuya (手水舎), bow twice, clap twice, pray, bow once. Over 80,000 across Japan.
🛕
お寺
otera
Buddhist temple
Buddhist worship sites with a main hall (本堂, hondō). Often have incense burners — waft the smoke toward yourself for good health. Many have stunning garden landscapes and pagodas (五重塔, gojūnotō).
🏯
城
shiro
Castle
Himeji (姫路城), Matsumoto (松本城), and Osaka Castle (大阪城) are the most impressive. Many have original keeps (天守閣, tenshukaku) dating back 400+ years. Stunning in cherry blossom season.
🌸
公園
kōen
Park
Japanese parks are immaculately maintained. Ueno, Shinjuku Gyoen, and Maruyama Park are legendary — especially for cherry blossom (花見, hanami) season from late March through April.
🗻
山
yama
Mountain
富士山 (Fujisan = Mt. Fuji) is Japan's highest and most iconic. Climbing season is July–August. Other highlights: Japanese Alps, Nikko's mountains, and Yakushima's ancient cedar forests.
🌊
海岸
kaigan
Coastline / Beach
Japan has 29,751km of coastline. Okinawa has tropical-grade beaches; the Izu Peninsula offers rugged Pacific cliffs; the Japan Sea coast turns dramatic in winter with crashing waves and snow.
🎋
庭園
teien
Traditional garden
The three great gardens of Japan: Kenroku-en (Kanazawa), Kōraku-en (Okayama), and Kairaku-en (Mito). Japanese garden design (造園, zōen) is a centuries-old art form.
🏟️
博物館 / 美術館
hakubutsukan / bijutsukan
Museum / Art museum
TeamLab (チームラボ) digital art spaces, the Tokyo National Museum, and Naoshima's contemporary art island are world-class. Many offer audio guides in English.

Lesson 3 · Expressing wonder & awe
How to react when Japan takes your breath away

Japanese has a rich set of exclamations and reactive expressions. Using these spontaneously — not just reciting memorised phrases — signals to locals that you're genuinely engaged with their culture.

きれい！
kirei!
Beautiful! / Pretty!
For scenery, flowers, views
すごい！
sugoi!
Amazing! / Wow!
The most versatile exclamation
感動しました
kandō shimashita
I was deeply moved
For temples, ceremonies, art
壮大ですね
sōdai desu ne
It's magnificent / Grand
For mountains, castles, vistas
最高ですね！
saikō desu ne!
It's the best!
Enthusiastic approval of anything
雰囲気がいいですね
fun'iki ga ii desu ne
The atmosphere is great
For temple grounds, market streets
写真を撮ってもいいですか？
shashin wo totte mo ii desu ka?
May I take a photo?
Always ask at temples and of people
もっとゆっくり見たいです
motto yukkuri mitai desu
I want to look more slowly
When a tour moves too fast
Lesson 4 · Getting & giving recommendations
Ask locals where to go — and share what you've found
🗺️
おすすめの場所はありますか？
Osusume no basho wa arimasu ka?
Do you have any recommended places?
おすすめ (osusume) = recommendation. 場所 (basho) = place. Swap for: レストラン (restaurant), お土産 (souvenir), 観光スポット (sightseeing spot).
⏱️
どのくらい時間がかかりますか？
Dono kurai jikan ga kakarimasu ka?
How long does it take?
どのくらい (dono kurai) = how much/how long. 時間がかかる (jikan ga kakaru) = to take time. Essential for planning sightseeing schedules.
🎟️
入場料はいくらですか？
Nyūjōryō wa ikura desu ka?
What is the admission fee?
入場料 (nyūjōryō) = admission/entrance fee. 無料 (muryō) = free. Many temples and shrines have small fees (¥500–1,000). National parks are usually free.
🕐
何時まで開いていますか？
Nan-ji made aite imasu ka?
Until what time is it open?
まで (made) = until. 開いている (aite iru) = is open. Many temples close at 5pm — always check before making the journey.
💬
ここは本当によかったです！
Koko wa hontō ni yokatta desu!
This place was really wonderful!
よかった (yokatta) = was good (past tense of いい). 本当に (hontō ni) = truly/really. Say this to a local guide or host — it means the world to them.

Lesson 5 · お土産 (omiyage) — Japan's gift-giving tradition
The art of bringing back regional souvenirs

Omiyage (お土産) is far more than souvenir shopping — it's a deeply ingrained social obligation. When you visit a new place in Japan, you're expected to bring back regional food gifts for your colleagues, family, or hosts. Knowing what's special to each region is a cultural badge of honour.

🍪
八つ橋
yatsuhashi
📍 Kyoto
Cinnamon-flavoured rice cakes — Kyoto's most iconic souvenir. Available in raw (生, nama) or baked form with various fillings.
🍫
白い恋人
Shiroi Koibito
📍 Hokkaido (Sapporo)
White chocolate cookies sandwiched between langue de chat wafers. Japan's most famous regional confection — always sells out.
🥮
もみじ饅頭
momiji manjū
📍 Hiroshima (Miyajima)
Maple leaf-shaped cakes filled with red bean paste. Hiroshima's signature sweet — named after the maple trees at Miyajima.
🍢
東京ばな奈
Tokyo Banana
📍 Tokyo
Banana-flavoured cream-filled sponge cakes in the shape of a banana. The quintessential Tokyo station souvenir — bought by millions annually.
🦐
えびせんべい
ebi senbei
📍 Nagoya (Ise)
Shrimp-flavoured rice crackers. A savoury classic with origins along the Pacific coast. Beautifully packaged and beloved across Japan.
🍬
ちんすこう
chinsukō
📍 Okinawa
Traditional Ryukyuan shortbread cookies — lard-based, crumbly, and subtly sweet. Okinawa's most beloved confection, centuries old.
🎁
これはお土産です。どうぞ。
Kore wa omiyage desu. Dōzo.
This is a souvenir for you. Please accept it.
どうぞ (dōzo) = please (go ahead). Use both hands when presenting a gift — a sign of respect.
🗾
〜の名物です
[place] no meibutsu desu
This is a famous specialty from [place]
名物 (meibutsu) = famous local specialty. e.g. 京都の名物です = This is a Kyoto specialty. Always explain where your omiyage is from.
Omiyage etiquette: Never give one item per person — buy enough for the whole group. Present gifts with both hands and a small bow. The recipient typically doesn't open the gift immediately in front of you — this is considered polite. The packaging (包装, hōsō) matters as much as the contents in Japan.
Culture note
🌸
花見 (hanami) — cherry blossom viewing
Hanami (花見, "flower viewing") is one of Japan's most joyful cultural traditions. Every spring, when cherry blossoms bloom for just 1–2 weeks, entire parks fill with people sitting under the trees eating, drinking, and celebrating life's fleeting beauty. The concept of mono no aware (物の哀れ) — the poignant appreciation of impermanence — is at the heart of why the Japanese attach such meaning to a flower that blooms and falls in days. Blue tarps go down weeks in advance to reserve the best spots in popular parks. Joining a hanami picnic with locals, sharing food and drinks under falling petals, is one of the most memorable experiences Japan offers.

Lesson 1 · Essential Korean sightseeing
8 must-know sites and cultural landmarks
🏯
경복궁
Gyeongbokgung
Gyeongbok Palace — Seoul's grandest
Built in 1395, Korea's most majestic Joseon-dynasty palace. The changing of the guard (수문장 교대식) ceremony happens daily. Wear hanbok for free entry. Stunning in autumn and winter snow.
🏙️
북촌 한옥마을
Bukchon Hanok Village
Traditional hanok village — Seoul
A hillside neighbourhood of 900+ preserved traditional Korean houses. One of Seoul's most photographed areas. Best visited early morning before crowds arrive. Quiet neighbourhood — be respectful.
🗻
설악산
Seoraksan
Seorak Mountain National Park
Korea's most dramatic mountain landscape — granite peaks, Buddhist temples, and cable cars. Spectacular in autumn when foliage turns crimson and gold. Near Sokcho on the east coast.
🌋
한라산
Hallasan
Halla Mountain — Jeju Island
Korea's highest peak (1,947m) at the centre of Jeju Island. A UNESCO World Heritage site. Several hiking trails — the Seongpanak trail is the most popular full-day route to the summit crater lake.
🌊
해운대
Haeundae
Haeundae Beach — Busan
Korea's most famous beach — long white sand, modern hotels, and a lively promenade. Busan's beach culture peaks in summer. The Haeundae sea festival draws millions.
🛕
불국사
Bulguksa
Bulguk Temple — Gyeongju
UNESCO-listed Buddhist temple from 528 AD. One of Korea's finest examples of Silla-era architecture. Nearby Seokguram Grotto houses a breathtaking granite Buddha. Don't miss the lantern festival in spring.
🎭
인사동
Insadong
Insadong — Seoul's cultural street
Traditional arts, galleries, teahouses, and craft shops. A wonderful contrast to Myeongdong's commercialism. Try bungeo-ppang (fish-shaped pastry) from street stalls while browsing galleries.
🏝️
제주도
Jejudo
Jeju Island — Korea's Hawaii
Volcanic island with tangerine orchards, lava tube caves, dramatic coastlines, and haenyeo (해녀) female divers. A UNESCO triple crown site. 1-hour flight from Seoul.

Lesson 3 · 등산 (deungsan) — Korea's hiking culture
Hiking is a national passion — here's the vocabulary you need

Koreans are among the world's most passionate hikers. On weekends, mountains like Bukhansan (just north of Seoul) draw tens of thousands of climbers. The hiking scene has its own fashion, etiquette, and vocabulary.

🥾
등산 (deungsan) — mountain climbing / hiking
deungsan = ascending a mountain
The general word for hiking in Korea. 등산객 (deungsangaek) = hiker. 등산복 (deungsanbok) = hiking gear/clothes — Koreans take their hiking fashion seriously (often head-to-toe branded gear).
등산 좋아해요? — Do you like hiking?
🗺️
등산로 (deungsanno) — hiking trail
deungsanno = mountain path
Trail difficulty markers: 쉬운 코스 (swiun koseu = easy trail), 중간 코스 (junggan koseu = medium), 어려운 코스 (eoryeoun koseu = difficult). Most national parks have excellent trail maps in English.
이 등산로는 어려워요? — Is this trail difficult?
⛰️
정상 (jeongsang) — summit
jeongsang = mountaintop
Reaching the summit is a huge moment in Korean hiking culture. Hikers photograph themselves at the summit marker stone (정상석). Sharing summit photos is a beloved social media tradition.
정상까지 얼마나 걸려요? — How long to the summit?
🍜
산 위의 라면 (san wie ramen) — summit ramen
literally: ramen above the mountain
A beloved Korean hiking ritual — eating instant ramen at the summit or at a mountain hut (산장, sanjang). The altitude, fresh air, and achievement make it taste like the best meal of your life.
정상에서 라면 먹어요! — We eat ramen at the summit!
Hiking phrases: 조심하세요 (josimhaseyo = be careful) · 천천히 가세요 (cheoncheonhi gaseyo = go slowly) · 다 왔어요! (da wasseoyo = we're almost there!) · 경치가 정말 아름다워요 (gyeongchiga jeongmal areumdaweoyo = the view is truly beautiful).

Lesson 5 · Grammar — -(으)려고 하다
"I'm planning to / I intend to ~" — expressing intentions naturally

-(으)려고 하다 expresses your intention or plan to do something. It's one of the most natural ways to talk about future plans in Korean — more nuanced than simply using the future tense, because it implies you've thought about it and decided.

The rule: Verb stem + 으려고 하다 (after a consonant) or + 려고 하다 (after a vowel). e.g. 먹다 → 먹으려고 해요 (I'm planning to eat), 가다 → 가려고 해요 (I'm planning to go), 보다 → 보려고 해요 (I'm planning to see). The 하다 conjugates normally for tense.
Verb stem+ (으)려고 하다= I'm planning to / intending to ~
경복궁에 가려고 해요.
Gyeongbokgunge garyeogo haeyo.
I'm planning to go to Gyeongbok Palace.
가다 → 가려고 해요. After a vowel-ending stem — no 으 needed.
한라산을 등산하려고 해요.
Halrasaneul deungsanharyeogo haeyo.
I'm planning to hike Hallasan.
등산하다 → 등산하려고 해요. 하다 verbs: just add 려고 after 하.
사진을 많이 찍으려고 해요.
Sajineul mani jjigeoryeogo haeyo.
I'm planning to take a lot of photos.
찍다 → 찍으려고 해요. After a consonant — add 으려고.
내일 인사동에서 기념품을 사려고 해요.
Naeil Insadongeseo ginyeompumeul saryeogo haeyo.
Tomorrow I'm planning to buy souvenirs at Insadong.
사다 → 사려고 해요. A full practical sentence — notice how naturally it uses everything from previous weeks: 내일 (tomorrow), 에서 (location particle), 을 (object marker).

10 natural -(으)려고 해요 sentences for travellers:

한복을 입으려고 해요
hanboguel ibeuryeogo haeyo
I'm planning to wear hanbok
Free entry to palaces in hanbok!
야경을 보려고 해요
yagyeongeul boryeogo haeyo
I'm planning to see the night view
야경 = night scenery
찜질방에 가려고 해요
jjimjilbange garyeogo haeyo
I'm planning to go to a jjimjilbang
From Week 11 vocabulary!
정상에 올라가려고 해요
jeongsange ollagaryeogo haeyo
I'm planning to climb to the summit
올라가다 = to go up / climb
케이블카를 타려고 해요
keibeulkareul taryeogo haeyo
I'm planning to take the cable car
타다 = to ride/take
일출을 보려고 해요
ilchureul boryeogo haeyo
I'm planning to watch the sunrise
일출 = sunrise — popular at Jeju!
-(으)려고 하다 vs -(을)ㄹ 수 있다 vs -(아/어)서 — keeping your grammar toolkit straight: 가려고 해요 = "I'm planning to go" (intention). 갈 수 있어요 = "I can go" (ability). 가서 해요 = "I go and then do" (sequence). Three different meanings — the right one changes the whole sentence!

Culture note
👘
한복 (hanbok) — Korea's traditional clothing
Hanbok (한복) is Korea's traditional clothing, characterised by vibrant colours, graceful silhouettes, and natural fabrics. Women's hanbok features a short jacket (저고리, jeogori) and a full skirt (치마, chima); men's typically has baggy trousers (바지, baji) and a jacket. Today, wearing hanbok at palaces like Gyeongbokgung is hugely popular — many rental shops (한복 대여, hanbok daeyeo) surround the palace gates and offer day rentals for ₩15,000–30,000. Wearing hanbok grants free entry to most royal palaces in Seoul and creates instantly memorable photos. It's one of the best immersive cultural experiences Korea offers visitors of any age.
