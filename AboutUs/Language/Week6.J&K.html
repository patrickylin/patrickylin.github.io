Lesson 1 · Essential Japanese dishes
12 dishes every visitor needs to know — and how to order them
MUST TRY
🍣
寿司 / すし
sushi
Sushi — vinegared rice with toppings
At a conveyor belt (回転寿司 kaiten-zushi), just grab plates. At a proper sushi bar, say the fish name + ひとつ (hitotsu = one please).
MUST TRY
🍜
ラーメン
rāmen
Ramen — noodle soup
4 main styles: 醤油 (shoyu/soy), 塩 (shio/salt), 味噌 (miso), 豚骨 (tonkotsu/pork bone). Each region has its specialty.
🍱
弁当 / べんとう
bentō
Bento — boxed meal
From konbini or train stations. Usually rice + protein + vegetables. A complete, affordable meal.
🍙
おにぎり
onigiri
Rice ball — stuffed with filling
Fillings include 鮭 (sake/salmon), ツナ (tsuna/tuna), 梅 (ume/pickled plum). Konbini staple.
MUST TRY
🍛
カレーライス
karē raisu
Japanese curry rice
Milder and thicker than Indian curry. Japan's most-eaten dish — every family has their version. Deep comfort food.
🍢
おでん
oden
Simmered hot pot — winter classic
Found at konbini in winter — point at what you want and the staff ladles it out. Tofu, daikon, fish cakes, eggs.
🥟
餃子 / ぎょうざ
gyōza
Pan-fried dumplings
Japanese gyoza are thinner-skinned and crispier than Chinese ones. Often served with ramen or as a side dish.
MUST TRY
🍡
たこ焼き
takoyaki
Octopus balls — Osaka street food
Batter balls with octopus inside, topped with sauce, mayo, and bonito flakes. Warning: they're extremely hot inside!
🫕
しゃぶしゃぶ
shabu-shabu
Hotpot — swish thin-sliced meat in broth
Named after the swishing sound the meat makes. A communal, interactive dining experience.
🍤
天ぷら
tempura
Light battered and deep-fried seafood & veg
The batter is deliberately thin and airy — nothing like heavy Western batter. Dip in tentsuyu (dashi dipping sauce).
🥩
焼肉 / やきにく
yakiniku
BBQ — grill your own meat at the table
You cook it yourself over a tabletop grill. Order different cuts and dip in tare (sauce). Social and very popular.
🍮
茶碗蒸し
chawanmushi
Steamed savoury egg custard
Silky smooth, served in a lidded cup. A classic side dish at traditional Japanese restaurants.

Lesson 2 · Restaurant phrases
Everything you need from entering to paying
🚪
一人です / 二人です
Hitori desu / Futari desu
Table for one / two
Staff will ask 何名様ですか (nan-mei-sama desu ka = how many people?). Just hold up fingers or say the number + 人 (nin/ri).
📖
メニューをください
Menyū wo kudasai
Please give me the menu
Many restaurants have picture menus or plastic food displays outside — point and use これをください if needed.
🙋
すみません！
Sumimasen!
Excuse me! (to call a waiter)
In Japan, calling out すみません to get a server's attention is completely normal and expected — not rude at all.
✏️
〜をひとつ、お願いします
[dish]-wo hitotsu, onegaishimasu
One [dish], please
ひとつ (one) / ふたつ (two) / みっつ (three) — these are native Japanese counting words for general objects.
🌶️
辛くしないでください
Karakunishinai de kudasai
Please don't make it spicy
辛い (karai) = spicy. 辛くして (karakushite) = make it spicy. 甘い (amai) = sweet. 少し (sukoshi) = a little.
💳
お会計をお願いします
Okaikei wo onegaishimasu
Bill / check, please
In Japan, you don't split bills at the table — pay at the register on your way out. Never tip; it can be considered rude.
🥡
持ち帰りできますか？
Mochikaeri dekimasu ka?
Can I take this away / get takeout?
持ち帰り (mochikaeri) = takeaway/takeout. テイクアウト (teiku-auto) is also widely understood.
Lesson 3 · Dietary restrictions
Essential phrases for allergies and dietary needs
アレルギーがあります
arerugī ga arimasu
I have an allergy
〜アレルギーがあります
[allergen]-arerugī ga arimasu
I am allergic to [allergen]
ベジタリアンです
bejitarian desu
I am vegetarian
肉は食べません
niku wa tabemasen
I don't eat meat
〜は入っていますか？
[ingredient]-wa haitte imasu ka?
Does this contain [ingredient]?
小麦粉 = flour/wheat
卵 = egg · 乳 = dairy
海産物 = seafood
〜なしでお願いします
[ingredient]-nashi de onegaishimasu
Without [ingredient], please
なし (nashi) = without
e.g. ネギなし = no green onion
グルテンなし = gluten-free
Note on vegetarianism in Japan: Traditional Japanese cooking uses dashi (出汁) — a stock often made from fish (bonito flakes) or seafood. Many dishes that appear vegetarian contain dashi. Always ask 魚は入っていますか？(sakana wa haitte imasu ka? = Does it contain fish?) to be safe.
Lesson 4 · Japanese dining etiquette
6 rules that will make you look like a seasoned Japan visitor
🍽️
いただきます & ごちそうさま Say いただきます (itadakimasu) before eating and ごちそうさまでした (gochisousama deshita) after. Already covered in Week 2 — this is the most appreciated thing a foreigner can do.
🥢
Chopstick rules Never stick chopsticks upright in rice (resembles funeral offerings) and never pass food chopstick-to-chopstick (a funeral ritual). Rest them on the chopstick holder (箸置き, hashioki) when not in use.
🍶
Pour for others, not yourself When drinking with a group, pour for those around you before filling your own glass. It's a sign of care and attentiveness.
💴
No tipping — ever Tipping is not part of Japanese culture and can cause confusion or even offence. The service price is included. Leave the table clean — that's the real appreciation.
🍵
お茶 (ocha) is usually free Hot green tea is complimentary at most Japanese restaurants. It's refilled automatically. Asking for water (お水 o-mizu) is fine too — it's always free.
🔔
Call buttons & shouting すみません Many restaurants have a call button (呼び出しボタン) at the table. If not, calling out すみません loudly is totally acceptable — staff will come promptly.
Culture note
🍱
駅弁 (ekiben) — train station bento culture
Japan has elevated the humble packed lunch into an art form. Ekiben (station bento boxes) are sold at bullet train (新幹線, shinkansen) platforms across the country, each region featuring its own local specialties — Hokkaido crab, Kyoto pickled vegetables, Kobe beef. Buying an ekiben before your shinkansen journey and eating it while watching the countryside go by is one of the most quintessentially Japanese experiences. Look for them in the basement food halls of department stores (デパ地下, depachika) too.

Lesson 1 · Essential Korean dishes
12 dishes every visitor needs to know
MUST TRY
🌶️
김치찌개
kimchi-jjigae
Kimchi stew — Korea's ultimate comfort food
Fermented kimchi simmered with pork (or tofu) and tofu. Deeply flavourful, spicy, and eaten year-round.
MUST TRY
🥩
삼겹살
samgyeopsal
Grilled pork belly — Korea's most social meal
Grilled at the table, wrapped in lettuce with garlic and ssamjang paste. The centrepiece of Korean social dining.
MUST TRY
🍲
비빔밥
bibimbap
Mixed rice bowl with vegetables & egg
Rice topped with seasoned vegetables, a fried egg, and gochujang paste. Mix everything together before eating.
🍜
냉면
naengmyeon
Cold buckwheat noodles — summer essential
Served in an icy broth or dry with sauce. The scissor-cut noodles tradition means staff will offer to cut them for you.
🥘
된장찌개
doenjang-jjigae
Fermented soybean paste stew
Korea's answer to miso soup — but much heartier. Made with tofu, zucchini, and fermented soybean paste.
MUST TRY
🌶️
떡볶이
tteokbokki
Spicy rice cakes — king of Korean street food
Chewy cylindrical rice cakes in a sweet-spicy gochujang sauce. Found everywhere on the street. Addictive.
🍗
치킨
chikin
Korean fried chicken — double-fried, crispy
Double-fried for maximum crispiness — a technique that changed the world. Often delivered late at night with beer (치맥, chimaek).
🧆
순두부찌개
sundubu-jjigae
Soft tofu stew — silky and spicy
Served in a stone pot, still bubbling. Crack a raw egg in at the table and it cooks in the broth. A breakfast and lunch favourite.
🧇
파전
pajeon
Green onion pancake
Crispy savoury pancake with green onions, often with seafood (해물파전, haemul pajeon). A classic rainy day food.
🍣
김밥
gimbap
Rice rolls — Korea's answer to sushi
Rice and fillings wrapped in seaweed. Not raw fish — usually ham, egg, vegetables. A picnic and lunch staple.
🥗
잡채
japchae
Glass noodles with vegetables & meat
Sweet potato starch noodles stir-fried with sesame oil, vegetables, and beef. Served at celebrations and special occasions.
🍖
갈비탕
galbi-tang
Short rib soup — rich and clear-broth
Slowly simmered beef short ribs in a milky-white bone broth. A restorative, deeply satisfying bowl.
Lesson 2 · Spice levels — know before you order
맵기 (maepgi) — Korean food ranges from mild to brutal

Korea is famous for its bold spice. Knowing how to ask about spice levels can save you a lot of pain — and help you find your perfect dish.

Spice level guide — 맵기 단계 (maepgi dangye)
안 매워요 (not spicy)
된장찌개, 갈비탕
doenjang-jjigae, galbi-tang
조금 매워요 (a little spicy)
비빔밥, 잡채
bibimbap, japchae
매워요 (spicy)
김치찌개, 순두부찌개
kimchi-jjigae, sundubu
많이 매워요 (very spicy)
떡볶이, 불닭볶음면
tteokbokki, buldak
아주 매워요 (extremely spicy)
핵불닭, 마라탕
haek-buldak, maratang
🌶️
덜 맵게 해주세요
deol maepge haejuseyo
Please make it less spicy
덜 = less · 맵게 = spicy · 해주세요 = please make it. One of the most useful phrases in Korea.
🔥
더 맵게 해주세요
deo maepge haejuseyo
Please make it spicier
더 = more. Koreans will genuinely light up if you ask for more spice — it shows cultural confidence!

Lesson 3 · 반찬 — the side dish system
The most unique thing about eating in Korea

When you sit down at a Korean restaurant, several small side dishes called 반찬 (banchan) arrive at your table automatically — and they're free. These might include kimchi, seasoned spinach, fish cake, pickled radish, and more. You can ask for refills at no extra cost.

🥬
반찬 더 주세요 (banchan deo juseyo) "More banchan, please." Use this freely — restaurants expect and welcome it. Never hesitate to ask for a refill of any side dish you enjoy.
🍚
공기밥 하나 더 주세요 (gonggi-bap hana deo juseyo) "One more bowl of rice, please." 공기밥 (gonggi-bap) is a bowl of plain steamed rice — often free or very cheap. Request it any time.
✂️
Scissors are a dining tool In Korean restaurants, scissors (가위, gawi) are used to cut meat on the grill, cut long noodles, and even cut pancakes. Don't be surprised when the server pulls out a pair mid-meal.
Lesson 4 · Restaurant phrases
From entering to paying — Korean restaurant essentials
🚪
몇 명이세요?
Myeot myeong-iseyo?
How many people? (what staff will ask)
Answer: 두 명이요 (du myeong-iyo = two people) or just hold up fingers.
📖
메뉴판 주세요
Menyupan juseyo
Please bring the menu
Many Korean restaurants have picture menus or tablets — point and say 이거 주세요 (igeo juseyo = this one, please).
🙋
저기요!
Jeogiyo!
Excuse me! (to call a waiter)
저기요 literally means "over there" — the standard way to get a server's attention in Korea. Some restaurants have buzzers on the table (벨, bel).
🍽️
잘 먹겠습니다
Jal meokgesseumnida
I will eat well (said before eating)
The Korean equivalent of いただきます — a polite expression of gratitude before a meal. Very appreciated if you use it.
💳
계산해 주세요
Gyesan-hae juseyo
Bill / check, please
계산 (gyesan) = calculation/payment. Unlike Japan, many Korean restaurants accept card everywhere. Tipping is not expected.
🥡
포장해 주세요
Pojang-hae juseyo
Please pack this to go
포장 (pojang) = packaging/to go. Very common — Koreans frequently take leftovers home.
Culture note
🍺
치맥 (chimaek) — fried chicken + beer culture
치맥 (chimaek) combines 치킨 (chikin = fried chicken) and 맥주 (maekju = beer) — Korea's favourite pairing. It became a global phenomenon after featuring in the drama My Love from the Star in 2013. Korean fried chicken is distinct from Western styles — double-fried for an incredibly thin, glass-like crust and usually available in flavours like soy garlic, honey butter, or spicy. Late-night delivery of chimaek is a cornerstone of Korean social culture. Try ordering from a 치킨집 (chicken restaurant) at least once.
