Lesson 4 · At the clinic & pharmacy
Essential phrases for getting medical help
🚨
救急車を呼んでください！
Kyūkyūsha wo yonde kudasai!
Please call an ambulance!
The most critical emergency phrase. Say it clearly and loudly — passers-by will act immediately in Japan.
🏥
近くの病院はどこですか？
Chikaku no byōin wa doko desu ka?
Where is the nearest hospital?
病院 (byōin) = hospital. 診療所 (shinryōjo) = clinic. 内科 (naika) = internal medicine. 外科 (geka) = surgery.
📋
保険証を持っていません
Hokenshō wo motte imasen
I don't have a health insurance card
As a tourist you'll pay full price — keep receipts for travel insurance claims. 旅行保険 (ryokō hoken) = travel insurance.
💊
この薬はありますか？
Kono kusuri wa arimasu ka?
Do you have this medicine?
Show the packaging or the name written down. 薬局 (yakkyoku) = pharmacy. ドラッグストア (doraggu sutoa) = drug store (also sells OTC medicine).
🌡️
〜にアレルギーがあります
[substance]-ni arerugī ga arimasu
I am allergic to [substance]
ペニシリン (penishirin) = penicillin · 花粉 (kafun) = pollen · ナッツ (nattsu) = nuts · 乳製品 (nyūseihin) = dairy
📝
英語を話せる医者はいますか？
Eigo wo hanaseru isha wa imasu ka?
Is there a doctor who speaks English?
Large city hospitals often have international wards (国際外来, kokusai gairai). Your hotel concierge can also help find English-speaking doctors.

Lesson 5 · Grammar — the て-form (te-form)
The most important verb form in Japanese — gateway to complex sentences

The て-form (te-form) is one of the most essential grammar tools in Japanese. It connects verbs together, forms requests, expresses ongoing actions, and much more. Mastering it unlocks a huge range of sentences.

3 verb groups, 3 te-form rules. Japanese verbs fall into 3 groups — and each has its own pattern for forming the て-form. The good news: Group 2 and Group 3 are dead simple. Group 1 has some variation but follows a predictable sound-change pattern.
Group Dictionary form て-form Rule
Group 1
(u-verbs)
書くkaku (write)
飲むnomu (drink)
話すhanasu (speak)
待つmatsu (wait)
書いてkaite
飲んでnonde
話してhanashite
待ってmatte
 く→いて · ぐ→いで
む/ぬ/ぶ→んで
す→して
つ/る/う→って
Group 2
(ru-verbs)
食べるtaberu (eat)
見るmiru (see)
食べてtabete
見てmite
 Drop る, add て
Simple!
Group 3
(irregular)
するsuru (do)
来るkuru (come)
してshite
来てkite
 Memorise these two — just 2 irregular verbs!

4 ways to use the て-form:

て-form+ください= Please do [verb]
ここに座ってください。
Koko ni suwatte kudasai.
Please sit here.
て + ください = the standard polite request form. Used constantly in daily life.
て-form+います= Currently doing [verb]
薬を飲んでいます。
Kusuri wo nonde imasu.
I am taking medicine (currently).
て + います = the present progressive (like English "-ing"). Extremely common.
て-form+もいいですか= May I do [verb]?
ここに座ってもいいですか？
Koko ni suwatte mo ii desu ka?
May I sit here?
て + もいいですか = asking permission. You've already seen this pattern — 試着してもいいですか from Week 8!
[verb1 て-form]+[verb2]= Do verb1, then verb2
薬を飲んで、寝てください。
Kusuri wo nonde, nete kudasai.
Please take medicine and then sleep.
Chaining actions with て connects them naturally — "do A, and then B". Very common in instructions.
Culture note
🏥
Healthcare in Japan — excellent but document everything
Japan's healthcare system is world-class. As a tourist you pay 100% of costs out-of-pocket but prices are surprisingly reasonable by international standards — a clinic visit might cost ¥3,000–8,000. Always get a receipt (領収書, ryōshūsho) and a breakdown of treatments (診療明細書, shinryō meisaisho) for your travel insurance claim. Major cities have hospitals with international patient services — Tokyo Medical and Surgical Clinic, St. Luke's International Hospital, and Hiroo Hospital all have strong English-speaking staff. If in doubt, your hotel concierge (コンシェルジュ) is an excellent first port of call.

Lesson 4 · At the clinic & pharmacy
Essential phrases for getting medical help in Korea
🚨
구급차 불러 주세요!
Gugeuпча bulleo juseyo!
Please call an ambulance!
구급차 (gugeuпча) = ambulance. 불러 주세요 = please call. Say clearly — 119 also works if you can't speak Korean.
🏥
가까운 병원이 어디예요?
Gakkaun byeongwoni eodiyeyo?
Where is the nearest hospital?
병원 (byeongwon) = hospital. 의원 (uiwon) = clinic. 응급실 (eunggeupssil) = emergency room. 약국 (yakguk) = pharmacy.
📋
여행자 보험이 있어요
Yeohaengja bohemi isseoyo
I have travel insurance
여행자 보험 (yeohaengja bohem) = travel insurance. Keep all receipts — 영수증 (yeongsujeung). Korean hospitals often have English-speaking international desks.
💊
이 약 있어요?
I yak isseoyo?
Do you have this medicine?
약 (yak) = medicine. 약국 (yakguk) = pharmacy. Show the packaging or name written down. Many pharmacies in tourist areas have English signage.
🌡️
〜에 알레르기가 있어요
[substance]-e allereugi-ga isseoyo
I am allergic to [substance]
페니실린 (penisil-lin) = penicillin · 견과류 (gyeongwaryu) = nuts · 유제품 (yujepum) = dairy · 해산물 (haesanmul) = seafood

할 수 있다 (hal su itda) = to be able to do. 의사 (uisa) = doctor. Major hospitals in Seoul (Severance, Asan Medical Centre) have excellent international services.

Lesson 5 · Grammar — the -(으)면 conditional
"If / when ~ then ~" — one of Korean's most useful grammar patterns

The -(으)면 ending attaches to verb or adjective stems to create "if/when" conditional sentences. It's the equivalent of English "if" clauses — and it's incredibly practical for daily life, giving advice, and expressing conditions.

The rule: Verb/adjective stem + 으면 (after a consonant) or + 면 (after a vowel or ㄹ). e.g. 먹다 → 먹으면 (if you eat), 가다 → 가면 (if you go), 아프다 → 아프면 (if it hurts).
Verb stem + (으)면 → "if/when ~"
약을 먹으면 나을 거예요.
Yageul meogeumyeon naul geoyeyo.
If you take medicine, you'll get better.
먹다 (to eat) → 먹으면 (if you eat). 낫다 (to get better) → 나을 거예요 (will get better)
아프면 병원에 가세요.
Apeumyeon byeongwone gaseyo.
If you're sick, go to the hospital.
아프다 (to be sick/hurt) → 아프면 (if sick). Ends in vowel so just add 면.
열이 있으면 쉬세요.
Yeori isseumyeon swiseyo.
If you have a fever, rest.
있다 (to exist/have) → 있으면 (if there is / if you have)
필요하면 말씀하세요.
Piryohamyeon malsseum-haseyo.
If you need anything, please let me know.
필요하다 (to need) → 필요하면 (if you need). A beautifully polite everyday expression.

More practical -(으)면 sentences:

시간이 있으면 연락해요
sigani isseumyeon yeollakaeyo
If you have time, contact me
시간이 있다 = to have time
비가 오면 택시 타세요
biga omyeon taeksi taseyo
If it rains, take a taxi
비가 오다 = rain comes (= it rains)
모르면 물어보세요
moreumyeon mureoboseyo
If you don't know, ask
모르다 = to not know
배가 고프면 드세요
baega gopeumyeon deuseyo
If you're hungry, please eat
배가 고프다 = to be hungry
Culture note
🏥
Healthcare in Korea — fast, affordable, and foreigner-friendly
Korea's national health system (국민건강보험, gungmin geongang boheom) doesn't cover tourists, but medical costs are still remarkably affordable by international standards. A clinic visit typically costs ₩15,000–50,000. Pharmacies (약국) are everywhere and pharmacists (약사, yaksa) are highly trained — they can recommend OTC treatments for common ailments without a doctor's visit. Many urban pharmacies have staff who speak basic English. One important note: Korea has a strict stance on certain medications brought into the country — ADHD medication and some sleeping pills require advance approval. Always check Korea Customs before travelling with any prescription medicine.
