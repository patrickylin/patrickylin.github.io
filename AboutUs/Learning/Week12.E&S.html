Lesson 1 · What is reported speech?
When you tell someone what someone else said — without quoting them directly

Reported speech (also called indirect speech) is how we relay what someone said, thought, asked, or told us — without using their exact words. In English, this almost always involves a change of tense (backshift), a change of pronouns, and sometimes a change of time expressions. It's essential for storytelling, gossip, news, and professional communication.

Direct speech (quoting exactly):
She said, "I am tired." · He asked, "Are you ready?"

Reported speech (relaying the message):
She said she was tired. · He asked if I was ready.

Notice: quotation marks disappear · "I" → "she" · "am" → "was" · "Are you" → "if I was"
Lesson 2 · Tense backshift — the central rule
When the reporting verb is in the past, tenses shift one step back in time

When the main reporting verb is past tense (said, told, asked), the tense in the reported clause typically shifts one step back — present becomes past, past becomes past perfect, will becomes would, etc. This "backshift" is English's way of signalling that the speech happened at an earlier time.

Direct speech → Reported speech
Present simple
"I work in Tokyo." 		→ Past simple
She said she worked in Tokyo.
Present continuous
"I am studying Spanish." 		→ Past continuous
He said he was studying Spanish.
Past simple
"I visited Kyoto." 		→ Past perfect
She said she had visited Kyoto.
Present perfect
"I have been to Spain." 		→ Past perfect
He said he had been to Spain.
Will (future)
"I will call you." 		→ Would
She said she would call me.
Can
"I can speak Korean." 		→ Could
He said he could speak Korean.
Must / Have to
"You must leave now." 		→ Had to
She said I had to leave.
When NOT to backshift: If the reported information is still true now, backshift is optional. "She said the earth is round." ✅ (still true — present fine). "She said Tokyo is the biggest city in Japan." ✅ (still true — no backshift needed). Backshift is also NOT required when the reporting verb is present: "She says she is tired." (present reporting verb — no backshift).

Changes to time expressions and pronouns:

Pronouns: I → she/he · we → they · you → I/we · my → her/his
Time: now → then · today → that day · yesterday → the day before · tomorrow → the next day / the following day
Place: here → there · this → that · these → those

"I will meet you here tomorrow."
→ She said she would meet me there the following day.
Lesson 3 · Reporting verbs — beyond "said"
Rich vocabulary for relaying speech — each verb carries a different nuance

English has dozens of reporting verbs, each encoding not just what was said but how it was said — the tone, intention, and type of speech act. Using varied reporting verbs is a hallmark of sophisticated English.

tell
tell + person + (that) clause
She told me she was leaving. · He told us to be quiet.
Always needs a person: "told me" — never "told that" alone ❌
ask
ask + (person) + if/whether / wh-word
She asked if I was ready. · He asked where I lived.
For questions — use if/whether for yes/no, wh-word for wh-questions
explain
explain + (to person) + that / how
She explained that the train was delayed. · He explained how to use it.
"Explain me" ❌ → "Explain to me" ✅ — common learner error
suggest
suggest + -ing / that + subject + (should) + base form
She suggested visiting Seville. · He suggested that we leave early.
"Suggest to go" ❌ — suggest never takes to + infinitive!
warn
warn + person + not to / that
She warned me not to be late. · He warned us that it would be cold.
Strong reporting verb — implies potential negative consequence
admit
admit + -ing / that
He admitted making a mistake. · She admitted that she was wrong.
Implies accepting something previously denied or hidden
deny
deny + -ing / that
He denied stealing the money. · She denied that she knew him.
"Deny to do" ❌ — deny always uses -ing for actions
promise / refuse / offer / agree
verb + to + infinitive
She promised to call. · He refused to answer. · She offered to help.
This group all take to + infinitive in reported speech
Lesson 4 · Reported questions & commands
Two special patterns with their own rules

Reported questions — three key changes:

1. No question mark, no auxiliary inversion:
Direct: "Where do you live?" → Reported: She asked where I lived. (not "where did I live")

2. Yes/No questions use if or whether:
Direct: "Are you coming?" → Reported: He asked if/whether I was coming.

3. Wh-questions keep the wh-word:
Direct: "What time does the film start?" → Reported: She asked what time the film started.
Direct: "Why did you leave?" → Reported: He asked why I had left.

Reported commands and requests — use to-infinitive:

Direct command: "Close the door!" → She told me to close the door.
Direct negative: "Don't be late!" → He warned me not to be late.
Direct request: "Please help me." → She asked me to help her.
Direct invitation: "Come to my party!" → She invited me to come to her party.
The suggest trap — one of the most common errors: Suggest never takes to + infinitive. "She suggested to go" ❌. The correct patterns: "She suggested going" ✅ or "She suggested that we go" ✅ or "She suggested that we should go" ✅. This is frequently tested in exams and frequently wrong in learner speech — memorise it as a fixed rule.

Culture note
📡
Reported speech in news and everyday life
Reported speech is the backbone of journalism and gossip — two of the most socially important uses of language. In English news reporting, the choice of reporting verb carries enormous editorial weight: "The minister said" is neutral. "The minister claimed" implies scepticism. "The minister admitted" implies they were reluctant to say it. "The minister insisted" implies pushback. "The minister revealed" implies it was previously hidden. Native readers process these distinctions automatically — learners often miss them entirely. In everyday conversation, reported speech powers gossip: "She told me that...", "He mentioned that...", "Apparently she said..." The word "apparently" is particularly British — it signals that the speaker is relaying something heard second-hand and cannot verify its truth. Learning reported speech is learning to navigate both journalism and social life.

Lesson 2 · Para que — subjunctive in purpose clauses
In order that someone (else) does something — always subjunctive

When expressing purpose for another person's action, Spanish uses para que + subjunctive. This is different from para + infinitive (same subject). The rule is elegant: if the subject of the purpose clause is DIFFERENT from the main verb's subject, use para que + subjunctive. If the subject is the SAME, use para + infinitive.

Structure When used Example
para + infinitive SAME subject does the purpose action Estudio para aprender. (I study in order to learn — same person does both)
para que + subjunctive DIFFERENT subject does the purpose action Te lo explico para que lo entiendas. (I explain it so that YOU understand — different subjects)
a fin de que + subjunctive More formal equivalent of para que Se publicó a fin de que todos lo supieran. (It was published so that everyone would know)
sin que + subjunctive Without (someone doing something) Entró sin que nadie lo viera. (He entered without anyone seeing him)
The same-subject / different-subject rule in full:
Same: Voy al mercado para comprar fruta. (I go to buy fruit — I go AND I buy)
Different: Voy al mercado para que tú puedas quedarte en casa. (I go so that YOU can stay home — I go, you stay)
This distinction — one infinitive form for same subject, subjunctive for different subject — runs throughout Spanish grammar and is one of its most consistent and beautiful principles.
🗣️
Te lo digo para que no cometas el mismo error.
I'm telling you so that you don't make the same mistake.
Para que + subjunctive (cometas — subjunctive of cometer). Different subjects: I tell, you act. A very natural warning or advice structure.
💡
He traído el mapa para que podáis orientaros.
I've brought the map so that you all can find your way.
He traído (I) + para que + podáis (you all — subjunctive of poder). Classic travel situation — guide brings resources for others to use.
🤫
Habla más despacio para que te entiendan.
Speak more slowly so that they understand you.
Command (habla) + para que + entiendan (subjunctive). Three different subjects: I (giving the command), you (speaking), they (understanding). Complex but perfectly natural.
Lesson 3 · Relative clauses with the subjunctive
Known vs unknown — the clause that reveals whether something exists or is desired

One of the most elegant uses of the Spanish subjunctive: when a relative clause refers to something that is KNOWN to exist, use the indicative. When the thing is UNKNOWN, DESIRED, or SEARCHED FOR (and may or may not exist), use the subjunctive. The grammar encodes existential certainty!

Indicative — known entity
Exists — specific, identified
The noun EXISTS and is known.
You're describing a specific thing or person you already have in mind.
Busco al camarero que habla inglés.
(I'm looking for the waiter who speaks English — I know there is one specific person)

Tengo un amigo que vive en Madrid.
(I have a friend who lives in Madrid — specific real friend)

Conozco un restaurante que sirve paella auténtica.
(I know a restaurant that serves authentic paella — it exists, I know it)
Subjunctive — unknown entity
Desired / hypothetical / may not exist
The noun may NOT EXIST yet.
You're searching, wishing, or describing a desired but unfound person/thing.
Busco un camarero que hable inglés.
(I'm looking for a waiter who speaks English — I don't know if one exists)

Quiero un amigo que viva cerca.
(I want a friend who lives nearby — hypothetical, not yet found)

¿Hay algún restaurante que sirva paella auténtica?
(Is there a restaurant that serves authentic paella? — unknown, seeking)
The article as a clue: The article often signals which mood to use. Definite article (el/la) → specific, known entity → indicative: "Busco al médico que habla chino." Indefinite article (un/una) or algún/ningún → unknown entity → subjunctive: "Busco un médico que hable chino." Negative sentences almost always use subjunctive: "No hay nadie que sepa la respuesta." (There is no one who knows the answer.)

Culture note
🎨
El turismo cultural — Spain's extraordinary cultural heritage
Spain has 50 UNESCO World Heritage Sites — the fourth highest number of any country in the world. From the prehistoric cave paintings of Altamira to the modernist architecture of Antoni Gaudí in Barcelona, the Moorish palaces of Granada's Alhambra to the medieval pilgrimage route of El Camino de Santiago — the country is a living museum of civilisations. The Museo del Prado in Madrid holds one of the world's greatest collections of European art: Velázquez, Goya, El Greco, and Bosch are represented by masterworks that rarely travel. Spain's approach to heritage is deeply personal — locals take extraordinary pride in their local monuments, history, and traditions. When visiting a Spanish city, asking a local "¿Qué es lo más especial de aquí?" (What is most special about this place?) will unlock pride, passion, and recommendations no guidebook could provide. The most authentic cultural experience in Spain is always the one shared by a Spaniard.

The tense backshift insight: English is unusually strict about reported speech tense backshift — mandatory when the reporting verb is past. Japanese barely changes tenses at all in reported speech, relying on the quotation particle と (to) to do the work. Korean similarly uses minimal backshift. Spanish does backshift, but the rules are somewhat more flexible than English. This means Japanese and Korean learners often forget English backshift — while Spanish learners may find English backshift more familiar, but find the mandatory nature of it somewhat stricter than their own language.
