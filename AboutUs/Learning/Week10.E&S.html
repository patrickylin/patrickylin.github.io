Lesson 1 · Prepositions — the small words that cause big problems
In, on, at, by, for, with, from, to... — no language maps them the same way

Prepositions are among the hardest parts of any language because they don't translate directly. The preposition you use in Japanese or Spanish for a given concept is often completely different from English — and even within English, the same preposition covers wildly different meanings. They must be learned as set phrases rather than rules.

The in/on/at time system: Think of a funnel — biggest to smallest. in = large periods (years, months, seasons, parts of day: in the morning). on = medium units (days, dates: on Monday, on 14th February). at = precise points (exact times, holidays as moments: at 3pm, at Christmas, at midnight). Exception: at night (not "in night") — English is not always logical!
Lesson 2 · Phrasal verbs — the heart of natural English
Verb + particle = completely new meaning — the most challenging part of English

Phrasal verbs combine a verb with a preposition or adverb particle to create a meaning that is often completely unrelated to the individual words. They are extremely common in spoken and informal written English — and largely absent in formal writing. Native speakers use them constantly; learners must simply build a collection of them over time.

Why phrasal verbs are hard — and how to approach them: "Put" means to place something. But "put off" = postpone · "put up with" = tolerate · "put on" = wear / gain weight · "put out" = extinguish · "put down" = criticise / euthanise · "put through" = connect (a call). The particle completely transforms the meaning. There is no shortcut — they must be learned in context, one by one. Group them by base verb, and always learn them with an example sentence rather than just a definition.
Phrasal verbMeaningExample

Separable vs inseparable phrasal verbs: Some phrasal verbs can be split: "Turn the music up" ✅ = "Turn up the music" ✅. But with pronouns, they MUST be split: "Turn it up" ✅ — never "Turn up it" ❌. Inseparable phrasal verbs cannot be split at all: "I came across an old friend" ✅ — never "I came an old friend across" ❌. Three-word phrasal verbs (put up with, look forward to, get away with) are always inseparable.

Culture note
💬
Phrasal verbs as a register marker — why they matter so much
One of the clearest signs that someone has reached an advanced level of English is their natural, unselfconscious use of phrasal verbs. Formal English avoids them — an academic paper says "postpone", "investigate", "tolerate", "discover". Conversational English reaches for "put off", "look into", "put up with", "find out". The same person switches between these registers constantly. Learners who only know the Latin-root formal synonyms sound educated but slightly robotic in conversation; learners who know the phrasal verbs sound genuinely natural. This is why phrasal verbs appear so frequently in English language tests — they are reliable markers of authentic fluency. The goal isn't to memorise lists, but to absorb them through exposure — TV, films, podcasts, and conversations with native speakers.

Lesson 1 · Verbos reflexivos — reflexive verbs
When the subject and object are the same person — doing it to yourself

Reflexive verbs describe actions that the subject performs on itself. They are identified by the pronoun se attached to the infinitive (levantarse, llamarse, sentirse). Spanish uses reflexive verbs far more broadly than English — many actions that English describes with ordinary verbs require reflexive constructions in Spanish.

Reflexive vs non-reflexive — the meaning shifts:
lavar = to wash (something): Lavo el coche. (I wash the car.)
lavarse = to wash oneself: Me lavo las manos. (I wash my hands.)
llamar = to call: Llamo a mi madre. (I call my mother.)
llamarse = to be called: Me llamo Patrick. (I am called Patrick.)
ir = to go: Voy al mercado. (I go to the market.)
irse = to leave / go away: Me voy — hasta mañana. (I'm leaving — see you tomorrow.)

Conjugating reflexive verbs — levantarse (to get up):

PronounReflexive pronounVerb formFull formEnglish
yo me levanto me levanto I get up
tú te levantas te levantas you get up
él/ella se levanta se levanta he/she gets up
nosotros nos levantamos nos levantamos we get up
vosotros os levantáis os levantáis you all get up
ellos/ellas se levantan se levantan they get up
Pronoun placement rules: The reflexive pronoun goes BEFORE a conjugated verb: Me levanto. But AFTER and attached to an infinitive: Quiero levantarme. Or BEFORE the conjugated auxiliary: Me quiero levantar. Both are correct when there is an auxiliary verb — the pronoun can float to either position. With a gerund (-ando/-iendo), it attaches: Estoy levantándome.
Lesson 2 · La rutina diaria — daily routine
A typical Spanish day — built almost entirely from reflexive verbs
7:00
Me despierto y me levanto.
I wake up and get up.
despertarse (to wake up) · levantarse (to get up)
7:10
Me ducho y me lavo el pelo.
I shower and wash my hair.
ducharse (to shower) · lavarse (to wash oneself)
7:30
Me visto y me peino.
I get dressed and comb my hair.
vestirse (to get dressed — stem-changing!) · peinarse (to comb hair)
7:45
Me maquillo y me pongo el abrigo.
I put on make-up and put on my coat.
maquillarse (to put on make-up) · ponerse (to put on clothing — irregular!)
8:00
Desayuno con calma — el café es sagrado.
I have breakfast calmly — coffee is sacred.
desayunar (NOT reflexive! — just eating)
14:00
Como con la familia o los compañeros.
I eat lunch with family or colleagues.
comer (not reflexive) — la comida is the main meal in Spain
22:30
Me relajo, me quito la ropa y me acuesto.
I relax, take off my clothes and go to bed.
relajarse · quitarse (to take off clothing) · acostarse (to go to bed — stem-changing!)
23:00
Me duermo enseguida. ¡Buenas noches!
I fall asleep immediately. Good night!
dormirse (to fall asleep — stem-changing: me duermo)
Stem-changing reflexive verbs to know: vestirse (e→i): me visto · te vistes · se viste · nos vestimos. acostarse (o→ue): me acuesto · te acuestas · se acuesta · nos acostamos. dormirse (o→ue): me duermo · te duermes · se duerme · nos dormimos. These stem changes happen in the same persons as all o→ue and e→i verbs — all except nosotros and vosotros.
Lesson 3 · El subjuntivo — introduction to the subjunctive mood
The most misunderstood feature of Spanish — and the most rewarding to master

The subjunctive is a grammatical mood (not a tense) that expresses subjectivity — doubt, desire, emotion, uncertainty, hypotheticals, and recommendations. It contrasts with the indicative mood (the "normal" mood you've been using) which states facts. English has traces of the subjunctive ("If I were you", "I suggest he be there") but Spanish uses it extensively and constantly.

Indicative — stating facts
Regular tenses — what IS
✅ Objective statements
📋 Facts and certainties
🔮 Definite future plans
📅 Regular past/present/future
Sé que hablas español. (I know you speak Spanish — FACT)
Es verdad que llueve mucho. (It's true it rains a lot.)
Creo que viene. (I think he's coming — fairly certain)
Subjunctive — expressing subjectivity
Subjunctive forms — what MIGHT BE / is desired
💭 Doubt and uncertainty
❤️ Desire and recommendation
😢 Emotion and reaction
❓ Negated belief / unknown
Espero que hables español. (I hope you speak Spanish — desire)
No creo que llueva. (I don't think it will rain — doubt)
Quiero que vengas. (I want you to come — desire)

Forming the present subjunctive — the "opposite vowel" trick:

Step 1: Take the yo present indicative form: hablar → hablo · comer → como · vivir → vivo
Step 2: Drop the -o: habl- · com- · viv-
Step 3: Add the OPPOSITE vowel endings (-AR verbs get -E endings; -ER/-IR get -A endings):
-AR subjunctive: hable · hables · hable · hablemos · habléis · hablen
-ER/-IR subjunctive: coma/viva · comas/vivas · coma/viva · comamos/vivamos · comáis/viváis · coman/vivan
The WEIRDO acronym — when to use the subjunctive:
Wishes: Quiero que vengas. (I want you to come.)
Emotion: Me alegra que estés aquí. (I'm glad you're here.)
Impersonal expressions: Es importante que estudies. (It's important that you study.)
Recommendations/requests: Te recomiendo que pruebes la paella. (I recommend you try the paella.)
Doubt/denial: No creo que sea verdad. (I don't think it's true.)
Ojalá (hopefully): ¡Ojalá haga buen tiempo! (Hopefully the weather will be good!)
When any of these WEIRDO triggers appears in the first clause + que + new subject → subjunctive in the second clause.

Essential subjunctive trigger phrases:

🙏
Espero que + subjuntivo
I hope that... (desire/wish)
Espero que tengas un buen día. (I hope you have a good day.) · Espero que llegues pronto. (I hope you arrive soon.) One of the most natural and useful subjunctive triggers.
😊
Me alegra que + subjuntivo
I'm glad that... (emotion)
Me alegra que estés aquí. (I'm glad you're here.) · Me alegra que te guste. (I'm glad you like it.) Emotion about another person's action → subjunctive.
⚠️
Es importante/necesario que + subjuntivo
It's important/necessary that... (impersonal expression)
Es importante que practiques cada día. (It's important that you practise every day.) Impersonal expressions (es + adjective + que) ALWAYS trigger the subjunctive — no exceptions.
🎯
Te recomiendo que + subjuntivo
I recommend that you... (recommendation)
Te recomiendo que pruebes el gazpacho. (I recommend you try the gazpacho.) · Te sugiero que llegues pronto. (I suggest you arrive early.) Recommendation verbs: recomendar, sugerir, aconsejar, pedir — all trigger subjunctive.

Culture note
🌅
La rutina española — a different rhythm of life
The Spanish daily routine is famously out of step with Northern European and East Asian schedules — and wonderfully so. The working day often splits into two blocks with a long midday break: la jornada partida (split shift) runs roughly 9am–2pm and 5pm–8pm or 9pm, with a long midday pause for la comida (the main meal). Many shops and businesses still close during this midday window, though larger cities are increasingly adopting jornada continua (continuous hours). Evenings begin late — dinner rarely before 9pm, social gatherings from 10pm onwards. Children stay up late by Northern European standards — family dinners at 10pm with children present are entirely normal. This rhythm has deep roots: the Mediterranean climate made midday rest (la siesta) practical for centuries. Learning the vocabulary of the Spanish daily routine is also learning to understand a fundamentally different relationship with time, work, and rest.

Pre vs post positions — a deep typological divide: English and Spanish are prepositional languages — function words come BEFORE the noun (at the station, en la estación). Japanese and Korean are postpositional — equivalent particles come AFTER (駅に · 역에서). This reflects a broader pattern: SOV languages (Japanese, Korean) tend to put everything after the noun; SVO languages (English, Spanish) tend to put function words before. The subjunctive insight is equally striking: Spanish treats wish, doubt, and emotion as grammatically distinct realities; English and East Asian languages encode these meanings differently — through lexical choice, particles, or verb endings rather than a dedicated mood.
