Lesson 1 · The past simple — what it expresses
English's most fundamental way of talking about completed past events

The past simple describes actions or situations that were completed in the past — with no connection to the present. It corresponds roughly to the Spanish pretérito indefinido you learned last week. The key question: when did it happen? If it's finished and in the past, past simple is almost always correct.

Past simple is used for:
✅ Completed single action: She called me yesterday.
✅ Sequence of past events: I woke up, showered, and left the house.
✅ Past habits (no longer true): He played football every Saturday. (but not now)
✅ Past states: They lived in Seoul for three years.
✅ With specific past times: We met in 2019. I saw her last week.
Lesson 2 · Regular verbs — the -ed ending
The same -ed ending for all pronouns — English's most regular feature

Regular past tense is beautifully simple: add -ed to the base form, and it stays the same for every pronoun. I worked · You worked · She worked · We worked · They worked. Unlike Spanish (which changes ending for every pronoun) or the present tense (which adds -s for he/she/it) — the past simple regular form never changes.

1. Most verbs — add -ed
Base form ends in a consonant or a vowel-pair
work→worked · play→played · visit→visited · rain→rained · open→opened
2. Silent -e — add just -d
Base form already ends in -e
love→loved · use→used · arrive→arrived · like→liked · hope→hoped
3. Consonant + y → -ied
Ends in consonant + y (same rule as -s endings)
study→studied · try→tried · carry→carried · cry→cried
4. Double the consonant + -ed
Short verb, single vowel + single consonant (stressed)
stop→stopped · plan→planned · chat→chatted · drop→dropped
-ed pronunciation — three different sounds: The -ed ending is not always pronounced "ed"! After voiced sounds: /d/ — called /kɔːld/ · lived /lɪvd/. After voiceless sounds: /t/ — walked /wɔːkt/ · cooked /kʊkt/. After t or d: /ɪd/ — wanted /ˈwɒntɪd/ · needed /ˈniːdɪd/. This is why English learners sometimes struggle to hear the past tense — the ending can be almost inaudible (/t/) in fast speech.
Lesson 3 · Irregular verbs — the 30 you must know
No -ed here — these verbs change their entire form in the past

About 200 common English verbs are irregular — they don't add -ed in the past. Instead, they change their vowel, their ending, or both (and some don't change at all). The most frequent verbs in English tend to be the most irregular — which is why they must simply be memorised.

Lesson 4 · Negatives & questions — Did and Didn't
Just like the present "do/does" — past questions use "did" for everything

In the past simple, ALL verbs (regular and irregular) use did for questions and didn't for negatives — regardless of the pronoun. And just like with does/doesn't in the present, the main verb goes back to its base form when did/didn't appears.

Statement → Negative → Question:
She went to Seoul. → She didn't go to Seoul. → Did she go to Seoul?
I ate breakfast. → I didn't eat breakfast. → Did you eat breakfast?
They worked late. → They didn't work late. → Did they work late?
He was tired. → He wasn't tired. → Was he tired? (be inverts directly — no did!)
The base form rule — critical! When did or didn't appears, the main verb MUST return to its base form — even if it was irregular! "Did she went?" ❌ → "Did she go?" ✅. "I didn't ate" ❌ → "I didn't eat" ✅. The past is carried entirely by "did" — the main verb is set free. Exception: the verb to be doesn't use did — it uses was/were for all forms and inverts directly for questions.
Lesson 5 · Storytelling in the past
A short story using past simple — notice all the irregular and regular past forms
📖 A day in Kyoto
Last spring, I visited Kyoto for the first time. I woke up early and took the shinkansen from Tokyo. When I arrived, it was raining lightly. I bought an umbrella at the station and walked to my hotel. In the afternoon, I went to Fushimi Inari and climbed through hundreds of torii gates. The view took my breath away. That evening, I ate at a small ramen restaurant near the river. The chef didn't speak English, and I didn't speak much Japanese — but we smiled and understood each other anyway. I slept wonderfully that night.
Past time connectors for storytelling: last spring/week/year · yesterday · in [year] · at first · then · after that · suddenly · finally · in the end · when + past clause · before/after + -ing. These connectors link past events naturally and make English narratives flow. Notice how "when I arrived, it was raining" combines past simple (completed arrival) and past continuous (ongoing background rain) — a pattern covered more fully in Week 7.

Culture note
📚
Why English has so many irregular verbs — and why the most common words are the most irregular
English irregular verbs are the fossils of Old English — a highly inflected Germanic language where vowel changes (called ablaut) signalled tense. "Sing / sang / sung" follows the same pattern as German "singen / sang / gesungen". Over centuries, regular -ed endings replaced most of these patterns — but the most frequently used verbs survived because they were spoken too often to be "corrected." This is a universal linguistic principle: the more a word is used, the more resistant it is to regularisation. Go/went is so common that even children who know the -ed rule say "went" automatically. This is why the irregular verbs in English are almost exactly the words you need most — they are the irreplaceable core of the language.

Lesson 2 · En el restaurante — ordering food in Spanish
Everything from arriving to paying the bill
🍽️ En el restaurante — At the restaurant
🚪 Arriving
🍴 Ordering
💬 During the meal
💳 Paying
🪑
Una mesa para dos, por favor.
/ˈuna ˈmesa ˈpaɾa ðos poɾ faˈβoɾ/
A table for two, please.
The most useful opening line. Change dos to tres, cuatro, cinco... for larger groups.
📋
¿Nos puede traer la carta, por favor?
/nos ˈpweðe tɾaˈeɾ la ˈkaɾta/
Could you bring us the menu, please?
La carta = the menu. La carta del día = today's menu. El menú del día = fixed-price lunch menu — excellent value in Spain.
🕐
Tenemos reserva a nombre de...
/teˈnemos reˈserβa a ˈnombɾe ðe/
We have a reservation under the name of...
La reserva = the reservation. Hacer una reserva = to make a reservation. Essential for popular restaurants, especially on weekends.
🍽️
¿Qué me recomienda?
/ke me rekoˈmjenda/
What do you recommend?
A brilliant opener — shows confidence and gets you the best dish. The waiter will appreciate it.
✋
Para mí, la paella, por favor. / Yo quiero...
/ˈpaɾa mi la paˈeʎa/ · /ʝo ˈkjeɾo/
For me, the paella, please. / I want...
Para mí = for me (very natural when ordering). Yo quiero is direct but correct. The more polite form: ¿Me podría traer...? (Could you bring me...?)
🌶️
¿Tiene algo sin gluten / sin mariscos?
/ˈtjene ˈalɡo sin ˈɡluten/
Do you have anything without gluten / without seafood?
Sin = without. Soy alérgico/a a... = I am allergic to... Soy vegetariano/a = I am vegetarian. Soy vegano/a = I am vegan.
🍷
¿Nos trae una botella de vino tinto, por favor?
/nos ˈtɾae ˈuna boˈteʎa/
Could you bring us a bottle of red wine, please?
Una botella de = a bottle of. Una copa de = a glass of. Una jarra de agua = a jug of water — often free in Spanish restaurants.
😋
¡Está riquísimo! / ¡Qué rico!
/esˈta riˈkisimo/ · /ke ˈriko/
It's delicious! / How delicious!
Rico/a = tasty/delicious (also means rich). Riquísimo = the superlative (-ísimo). Note: ESTAR is used — taste is a temporary state. Say this to the chef and they will love you forever.
🧂
¿Me puede traer más pan / sal / agua?
/me ˈpweðe tɾaˈeɾ mas pan/
Could you bring me more bread / salt / water?
Más = more. ¿Me puede traer...? = Could you bring me...? Polite and natural for making requests during a meal.
🫙
No me gusta(n)... / Tengo alergia a...
/no me ˈɡusta/ · /ˈteŋɡo aˈleɾxja/
I don't like... / I have an allergy to...
Gustar works backwards: No me gusta el cilantro (cilantro doesn't please me). No me gustan las aceitunas (olives don't please me) — plural noun = gustan. This gustar pattern is covered properly in Week 8.
💳
La cuenta, por favor. / ¿Nos trae la cuenta?
/la ˈkwenta poɾ faˈβoɾ/
The bill, please. / Could you bring us the bill?
La cuenta = the bill/check. Note: In Spain, the waiter will NOT bring the bill until you ask — it would be considered rude to rush you. Always request it explicitly.
🤲
¿Está incluido el servicio? / ¿Se puede pagar con tarjeta?
/esˈta inkluˈiðo el serˈβiθjo/
Is service included? / Can I pay by card?
Tipping in Spain is optional and usually modest (rounding up or 5–10%). Unlike the US, tips are a pleasant gesture not an obligation. Most restaurants now accept card (tarjeta).
👋
Todo estuvo delicioso. ¡Muchas gracias!
/ˈtoðo esˈtuβo ðeliˈθjoso/
Everything was delicious. Thank you so much!
Estuvo = past tense of estar — used here because the meal is now over (completed experience). A warm farewell that will make the restaurant staff smile.
Lesson 3 · The imperfect tense — pretérito imperfecto
The "background" past — ongoing states, habits, and descriptions

The pretérito imperfecto is the second essential Spanish past tense. Where the preterite captures completed "snap photo" moments, the imperfect describes the ongoing background — habitual past actions, states that lasted, and descriptions of how things were.

-AR imperfect: hablar (to speak)

PronounEndingFormExample
yo-abahablabaCuando era niño, hablaba mucho. (When I was a child, I used to talk a lot.)
tú-abashablabas¿Hablabas español de niño? (Did you speak Spanish as a child?)
él/ella-abahablabaElla siempre hablaba muy despacio. (She always used to speak slowly.)
nosotros-ábamoshablábamosHablábamos horas por teléfono. (We used to talk for hours on the phone.)
vosotros-abaishablabais¿Hablabais catalán en casa? (Did you all speak Catalan at home?)

-ER/-IR imperfect (same endings): comer / vivir

Pronoun-ER/-IR endingcomía (ate)vivía (lived)
yo-íacomíavivía
tú-íascomíasvivías
él/ella-íacomíavivía
nosotros-íamoscomíamosvivíamos
vosotros-íaiscomíaisvivíais

Only 3 irregular imperfect verbs — the most regular tense in Spanish! The imperfect has almost no irregular verbs. Only three break the pattern: ser → era/eras/era/éramos/erais/eran · ir → iba/ibas/iba/íbamos/ibais/iban · ver → veía/veías/veía/veíamos/veíais/veían. Every other verb — including all the verbs that were irregular in the preterite — is completely regular in the imperfect. This makes it a huge relief to learn after the preterite's many exceptions.
Lesson 4 · Preterite vs Imperfect — the essential contrast
The most important distinction in Spanish tense — together they tell the full story
Pretérito Indefinido
comí · fui · hablé
📸 Completed action (snap photo)
⏱️ Specific time / duration
🔢 Number of occurrences
📋 Sequence of events
Ayer fui al mercado.
(Yesterday I went to the market.)
Comí paella tres veces.
(I ate paella three times.)
Pretérito Imperfecto
comía · era · hablaba
🎥 Background / ongoing state
🔄 Habitual past action (used to)
🖼️ Description in the past
🕐 Time, age, feelings in the past
De niño, iba al mercado todos los sábados.
(As a child, I used to go to the market every Saturday.)
Hacía mucho calor aquel día.
(It was very hot that day.)
The classic combined sentence: "Llovía cuando llegué." — It was raining (imperfect — ongoing background) when I arrived (preterite — completed event that interrupted). This pattern — imperfect sets the scene, preterite delivers the action — is the engine of Spanish storytelling. "Mientras comíamos (imp.), sonó (pret.) el teléfono." = While we were eating, the phone rang. Two tenses, one vivid moment.

Key trigger words for each tense:

Preterite triggers: ayer · anoche · el lunes · en 2020 · hace dos años · una vez · de repente · de pronto · finalmente
Imperfect triggers: siempre · normalmente · a veces · todos los días · de niño/a · antes · generalmente · cuando era joven

Culture note
🍽️
La sobremesa — the untranslatable art of sitting after the meal
In Spain, the meal doesn't end when the food is finished. La sobremesa (literally "over the table") is the time spent lingering after eating — talking, laughing, drinking coffee or a small glass of something — sometimes for hours. It is considered rude to rush from the table. The restaurant expects you to stay. Asking for the bill immediately after dessert would signal you didn't enjoy yourself. La sobremesa is where friendships deepen, business deals are made informally, and families reconnect. There is no direct English or Japanese equivalent — the concept that the best part of a meal might be the two hours after eating is uniquely Mediterranean. La sobremesa is one of the most beautiful things about Spanish culture, and knowing the word unlocks a whole dimension of how Spanish speakers relate to food, time, and each other.

The "used to" insight: Spanish grammaticalises the past habit distinction — it has a whole dedicated tense (imperfect) for it. English handles it lexically — with the phrase "used to" or "would". Japanese and Korean rely heavily on context. This reveals something deep about each language: Spanish trusts its grammar to carry meaning, English trusts its vocabulary, and East Asian languages trust shared context. None is better — they're just beautifully different strategies.
