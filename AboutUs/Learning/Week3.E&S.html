Lesson 1 · The present simple tense
What it expresses — and one very strange rule

The present simple tense in English describes habits, routines, facts, and general truths. It is NOT used for things happening right now — that requires the present continuous (I am eating — Week 5). This distinction surprises many learners.

Present simple = routine, fact, general truth:
✅ I drink coffee every morning. (routine)
✅ Water boils at 100°C. (scientific fact)
✅ She works at a hospital. (general situation)
❌ I drink coffee right now. → Should be: I am drinking coffee. (happening NOW = continuous)

The present simple — full conjugation of "to work":

PronounAffirmativeNegativeExample
I work don't work I work in Tokyo. / I don't work on Sundays.
You work don't work You work hard. / You don't work here.
He / She / It works doesn't work She works at a school. / He doesn't work late.
We work don't work We work together. / We don't work weekends.
They work don't work They work in London. / They don't work today.
The -s rule — English's most surprising verb rule: In every tense, English verbs look the same for all pronouns — EXCEPT for he/she/it in the present simple, which adds -s or -es. "I eat · you eat · we eat · they eat" — but "he eats". This is one of the most common errors in learner English and requires deliberate practice. Spanish does this far more consistently across all pronouns, making it ironically simpler!

Lesson 3 · Questions & negatives with Do / Does
The helper verb "do" — English's question-making machine

Most English verbs cannot form questions by inverting subject and verb (that only works with "to be" and modal verbs like can, will, should). For all other verbs, English needs a "helper" — do (for I/you/we/they) or does (for he/she/it).

Statement → Question → Negative:
You speak Japanese. → Do you speak Japanese? → You don't speak Japanese.
She likes coffee. → Does she like coffee? → She doesn't like coffee.
They live in Seoul. → Do they live in Seoul? → They don't live in Seoul.
Critical rule: When you use does or doesn't, the main verb goes back to its base form — the -s disappears! "She likes coffee" → "Does she like coffee?" (not "Does she likes coffee?"). The -s moved to "does", so it can't appear on the main verb too. This is one of the most common learner errors in English.

Culture note
🗣️
The tyranny of "do" — why English questions sound so strange to other language speakers
Almost every language in the world forms questions by simply changing intonation or inverting words. English does this too — but only for "to be" and modal verbs. For all other verbs, it requires inserting a meaningless helper "do" that carries no semantic content at all. "You like coffee" → "Do you like coffee?" The word "do" adds nothing in meaning — it's a purely grammatical carrier. This "do support" (as linguists call it) is almost unique to English among the world's major languages. It emerged during the Middle English period and linguists still debate exactly why. The result: English question formation is one of the first things learners must simply memorise rather than derive from logic.

Lesson 1 · The three verb families
All Spanish verbs belong to one of three families — defined by their infinitive ending

Spanish verbs are categorised by the last two letters of their infinitive (dictionary) form. Each family has its own set of present tense endings. Learn the endings for each family and you can conjugate thousands of regular verbs immediately.

The -ar family is by far the largest — and the most productive. Any new verb borrowed into Spanish (including from English) takes the -ar ending: googlear (to google), tuitear (to tweet), formatear (to format). When in doubt about a new verb, it's almost certainly -ar.

Tener vs tener — the "I am hungry" trap: Spanish expresses many physical/emotional states with TENER (to have) rather than SER/ESTAR. "I am hungry" = Tengo hambre (I HAVE hunger). "I am thirsty" = Tengo sed. "I am cold" = Tengo frío. "I am sleepy" = Tengo sueño. "I am afraid" = Tengo miedo. "I am 30 years old" = Tengo 30 años. English speakers constantly want to say "estoy hambre" — but Spanish says "I have hunger". Learn these tener expressions as fixed phrases.

Key number patterns: 16–19 have two forms — the traditional three-word form (diez y seis) and the modern contracted form (dieciséis). From 21 upward: veintiuno (21), veintidós (22)... but treinta y uno (31), treinta y dos (32) — note the "y" returns! 100 = cien · 101 = ciento uno. The gender alert: uno becomes un before masculine nouns (un libro) and una before feminine nouns (una casa) — even in compound numbers: veintiún libros, veintiuna casas.
Culture note
⏰
Spanish time culture — la hora española
Numbers and time in Spain follow a famously relaxed schedule. Lunch (la comida) — the main meal of the day — is eaten between 2pm and 4pm. Dinner (la cena) begins no earlier than 9pm, often at 10pm or later. Shops may close for a siesta between 2pm and 5pm in smaller towns. Social plans are fluid — arriving 20–30 minutes after the agreed time is entirely normal and not considered rude. The phrase ahora mismo (right now) can mean anything from two minutes to two hours. This relaxed relationship with time is an essential cultural context for the numbers and time expressions you're learning — in Spain, time is a suggestion, not a deadline.

The fascinating spectrum: Japanese and Korean barely change the verb at all — one form works for all persons (politeness level matters more than pronoun). English changes it only once — the notorious he/she/it -s. Spanish changes it six times — every pronoun gets a unique ending. This spectrum reveals how differently languages can encode the same grammatical information. Spanish's approach means subject pronouns can often be dropped (the ending already tells you who!), while English needs its pronouns every time.
