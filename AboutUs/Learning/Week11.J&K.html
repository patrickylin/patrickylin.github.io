Lesson 1 · Where to stay in Japan
6 accommodation types — from budget to unforgettable
🏯
旅館
ryokan
Traditional Japanese inn
The quintessential Japanese experience — tatami floors, futon bedding, yukata robes, communal hot spring baths, and elaborate multi-course kaiseki dinners. Staying in a ryokan is one of Japan's greatest travel highlights.
¥15,000–80,000/night
🏨
ホテル
hoteru
Western-style hotel
From budget business hotels (ビジネスホテル, bijinesu hoteru) to luxury. Toyoko Inn, APA, and Dormy Inn are excellent value. Always immaculate — Japanese hotel standards are exceptionally high.
¥7,000–30,000/night
🛖
民宿
minshuku
Family-run guesthouse
Like a ryokan but simpler and more intimate. Often run by a family who serves home-cooked meals. Very popular in rural areas and fishing villages. Authentic and affordable.
¥6,000–12,000/night
⚡
カプセルホテル
kapuseru hoteru
Capsule hotel
A uniquely Japanese invention — compact sleeping pods stacked in rows. Modern ones are surprisingly comfortable with TV, USB charging, and privacy curtains. A memorable budget option.
¥3,000–5,000/night
🏠
ゲストハウス
gesuto hausu
Guesthouse / Hostel
Budget-friendly with shared facilities. Great for solo travellers and meeting others. Many have private tatami rooms alongside dorms. Very common in Kyoto and Tokyo.
¥2,500–5,000/night
♨️
温泉宿
onsen yado
Hot spring inn
A ryokan built around natural hot spring baths. Regions like Hakone, Beppu, and Kinosaki are famous for their onsen culture. Often the centrepiece of a Japan trip.
¥20,000–60,000/night

Lesson 3 · Check-in, check-out & room requests
Everything from arrival to departure
🏨
チェックインをお願いします
Chekkuin wo onegaishimasu
I'd like to check in, please
Simply approach the front desk and say this. The staff will ask for your name (お名前は？, onamae wa?) and passport.
📋
予約した〜と申します
Yoyaku shita [name]-to moushimasu
I have a reservation under the name [name]
予約した (yoyaku shita) = (I) made a reservation. Combine with your name and a bow — front desk staff will immediately look you up.
🌊
海が見える部屋はありますか？
Umi ga mieru heya wa arimasu ka?
Is there a room with an ocean view?
〜が見える (ga mieru) = can see ~. 山が見える (yama ga mieru) = mountain view. 庭が見える (niwa ga mieru) = garden view.
🚿
タオルをもう一枚いただけますか？
Taoru wo mō ichimai itadakemasu ka?
Could I have one more towel?
もう (mō) = another/more. いただけますか (itadakemasu ka) = polite "could I have". Use this pattern for any extra amenity.
❄️
エアコンの使い方を教えてもらえますか？
Eakon no tsukaikata wo oshiete moraemasu ka?
Could you show me how to use the air conditioning?
〜の使い方 (no tsukaikata) = how to use ~. Works for any appliance: テレビ (TV), お風呂 (bath), Wi-Fi router.
🔧
部屋に問題があります
Heya ni mondai ga arimasu
There is a problem in my room
問題 (mondai) = problem. Be specific: お湯が出ません (oyū ga demasen = no hot water), 鍵が壊れています (kagi ga kowarete imasu = the key is broken).
🧳
チェックアウトは何時ですか？
Chekkuauto wa nan-ji desu ka?
What time is check-out?
Standard check-out in Japan is 11am. Late check-out (レイトチェックアウト, reito chekkuauto) is available for a fee at most hotels.
💼
荷物を預かってもらえますか？
Nimotsu wo azukatte moraemasu ka?
Could you store my luggage?
荷物 (nimotsu) = luggage. 預かる (azukaru) = to keep/store. Most Japanese hotels offer free luggage storage before check-in and after check-out.

Lesson 4 · Ryokan etiquette — 7 rules
A ryokan stay is an experience — know the customs
👟
Remove shoes at the genkan (玄関) The entrance foyer is where you step out of outdoor shoes and into slippers provided. Never wear outdoor shoes inside — this is a strict rule.
🪴
Wear your yukata (浴衣) everywhere inside The cotton robe provided is worn for dinner, in the corridors, and to the onsen. Wrap it left over right — right over left is reserved for funerals.
♨️
Onsen etiquette — wash thoroughly before entering Always wash and rinse your body completely at the shower stations before entering the communal bath. Enter clean. No swimwear — it's nude bathing. Tattoos are still restricted at many traditional onsen.
🍱
Meals are often served in your room A ryokan breakfast and dinner (kaiseki 懐石) may be served in your tatami room by staff. Wait to be seated and let them arrange the dishes before eating.
🌙
The futon appears while you dine Staff will lay out your floor futon while you're at dinner. In the morning it's put away — a ryokan custom that feels magical the first time it happens.
Lesson 5 · Grammar — the potential form
〜られる / 〜える — "can do / am able to do"

The potential form expresses ability — what you can or cannot do. It's essential for asking about hotel services, your own abilities, and politely expressing limitations.

Group Dictionary form Potential form Example in use
Group 1
(u-verbs)		 泳ぐoyogu (swim)
書くkaku (write) 話すhanasu (speak) 		泳げるoyogeru
書けるkakeru 話せるhanaseru 		Change final う-sound to える-sound.
ぐ→げる · く→ける · す→せる · つ→てる · ぬ/む/ぶ→ねる/める/べる · る→れる 日本語が話せます。= I can speak Japanese.
Group 2
(ru-verbs)		 食べるtaberu (eat)
見るmiru (see) 		食べられるtaberareru
見られるmirareru 		Drop る, add られる.

刺身が食べられます。= I can eat sashimi.
Group 3
(irregular)		 するsuru (do)
来るkuru (come) 		できるdekiru
来られるkorareru 		Memorise these two. 予約できますか？= Can I make a reservation?
Negative potential — can't do: Just add ません to the potential form. 日本語が話せません (Nihongo ga hanasemasen = I can't speak Japanese). 食べられません (taberaremasen = I can't eat it). The potential form with が instead of を for the object is more natural in modern Japanese.

Potential form in accommodation context:

🏊
プールは使えますか？
Pūru wa tsukaemasu ka?
Can I use the pool?
使う (tsukau = to use) → 使える (tsukaeru = can use). Works for: ジム (jimu = gym), サウナ (sauna), 駐車場 (chūshajō = parking).
🌸
温泉に入れますか？
Onsen ni hairemasu ka?
Can I enter the hot spring bath?
入る (hairu = to enter) → 入れる (haireru = can enter). Essential to ask if you have a tattoo — some onsen have restrictions.
🍳
朝食は何時から食べられますか？
Chōshoku wa nan-ji kara taberaremasu ka?
From what time can I eat breakfast?
食べられる = can eat (Group 2 potential). 朝食 (chōshoku) = breakfast. 夕食 (yūshoku) = dinner.
Culture note
♨️
温泉 (onsen) — Japan's bathing culture
Japan has over 27,000 hot spring sources, making onsen one of the country's most treasured cultural institutions. The ritual of bathing together in natural mineral waters is seen as deeply restorative — physically and socially. Many Japanese families take annual onsen trips as their primary holiday. The water itself varies dramatically by region: sulphurous and milky white in Noboribetsu, crystal clear and slightly acidic in Kinosaki, rich in iron and rust-coloured in Beppu. Each has claimed health benefits and traditional customs. For visitors, an onsen stay is often described as transformative — the combination of hot water, clean air, seasonal food, and profound quiet is unlike anything else in the world.

6 accommodation types — including the uniquely Korean ones

호텔
hotel
Hotel
From budget (모텔, motel — very common and not seedy in Korea) to luxury (롯데 호텔, Lotte Hotel). Korean business hotels offer great value. Many have Korean-style communal baths.
₩50,000–300,000/night
🧖
찜질방
jjimjilbang
Korean sauna house
A 24-hour heated public bathing and rest facility. Includes hot and cold pools, steam rooms, sleeping areas, food stalls, and entertainment. Staying overnight is very affordable and completely safe.
₩10,000–15,000
🛖
한옥스테이
hanok seutei
Traditional Korean house stay
Stay in a restored hanok (한옥) — Korea's traditional wooden architecture with curved roofs and ondol (heated floor) rooms. Bukchon and Jeonju are famous hanok villages.
₩80,000–200,000/night
🏠
게스트하우스
geseuteu hauseu
Guesthouse / Hostel
Very social — great for solo travellers. Many offer free breakfast, city tours, and K-pop nights. Hongdae and Insadong areas have excellent guesthouses.
₩20,000–40,000/night
🏢
고시원
gosiwon
Study room rental (micro-accommodation)
Ultra-budget tiny private rooms originally for students studying for exams. Now popular with budget travellers for extended stays. Very basic but private and cheap.
₩300,000–500,000/month
⛺
펜션
pension
Korean pension (countryside villa)
A popular weekend getaway — private villas or cabins outside the city, often with private BBQ facilities, hot tubs, and stunning natural settings.
₩100,000–300,000/night
Lesson 2 · Check-in, check-out & room requests
Everything you need from arrival to departure in Korean
🏨
체크인 하려고요
Chekeu-in haryeogoyo
I'd like to check in
하려고요 (haryeogoyo) = I intend to / I'd like to. Very natural and casual — perfect for hotels and guesthouses alike.
📋
〜 이름으로 예약했어요
[name] ireumeuro yeyakhasseoyo
I have a reservation under the name [name]
이름으로 (ireumeuro) = under the name of. 예약했어요 (yeyakhasseoyo) = made a reservation (past tense).
🌊
조용한 방으로 주세요
Joyonghan bangeuro juseyo
Please give me a quiet room
조용하다 (joyonghada) = quiet. 높은 층 (nopseun cheung) = high floor · 창문이 큰 방 (changmuni keun bang) = room with big windows.
🛁
수건 더 가져다 주실 수 있어요?
Sugeon deo gajeoda jusil su isseoyo?
Could you bring more towels?
가져다 주다 (gajyeoda juda) = to bring (something). 더 (deo) = more. Notice -(으)ㄹ 수 있어요 here — "is it possible to ~?" — the grammar you're learning today!
🔧
방에 문제가 있어요
Bange munje-ga isseoyo
There is a problem in my room
문제 (munje) = problem. Be specific: 온수가 안 나와요 (onsuga an nawayo = no hot water), 에어컨이 안 돼요 (eeokeoni an dwaeyo = the AC doesn't work).
🧳
짐 맡아 주실 수 있어요?
Jim mata jusil su isseoyo?
Could you store my luggage?
짐 (jim) = luggage/bag. 맡다 (matda) = to keep/store. Korean hotels are generally very accommodating about luggage storage.
🕐
체크아웃은 몇 시예요?
Chekeu-auteun myeot siyeyo?
What time is check-out?
Standard check-out in Korea is noon (12pm). 늦은 체크아웃 (neujeun chekeu-aut) = late check-out — ask at the front desk, often possible for a fee.

Lesson 3 · 찜질방 (jjimjilbang) — the ultimate Korean experience
How to survive and thrive in a Korean sauna house

The jjimjilbang is one of the most uniquely Korean experiences you can have. Open 24 hours, they're used by families, couples, friends, and solo travellers alike. Here's everything you need to know.

👕
You'll be given a uniform Upon entry (입장, ipjang), pay at the counter and receive a locker key, two towels, and a cotton uniform (짐질복, jjimjilbok) — shorts and a T-shirt in a single colour. Everyone wears the same uniform in the common areas.
♨️
Bathing areas are gender-separated and nude The bath floors (목욕탕, mogyoktang) are gender-separated. Shower and wash thoroughly before entering the communal pools. No swimwear — it's nude in the bath area, clothed in the common sauna areas.
🥚
Eat the iconic jjimjilbang eggs 맥반석 계란 (maekbansok gyeran) — eggs slow-cooked in the sauna rooms overnight, slightly caramelised and sold for ₩500–1,000 each. A beloved jjimjilbang tradition. Don't leave without trying one.
😴
You can sleep overnight Many people (including families) sleep overnight on the heated ondol floor of the common room with small cushions. It's completely normal and very comfortable — a unique budget accommodation option.
🎮
Facilities beyond the bath Larger jjimjilbang have PC rooms (PC방 inside), noraebang (karaoke), food courts, massage chairs, nail salons, and children's play areas. Some are essentially full leisure complexes.
Essential jjimjilbang vocabulary: 탕 (tang) = heated pool · 냉탕 (naengtang) = cold pool · 온탕 (ontang) = hot pool · 황토방 (hwangtobang) = clay room · 소금방 (sogumbang) = salt room · 얼음방 (eoreum-bang) = ice room · 때밀이 (ttaemiri) = body scrub service (ask for this — transformative).
Lesson 4 · Grammar — -(으)ㄹ 수 있다 / 없다
"Can do / Cannot do" — Korean's ability expression

This pattern expresses what is or isn't possible — ability, permission, and potential. It's made of three parts: the verb stem + -(으)ㄹ (future modifier) + 수 (means "way/possibility") + 있다 (exists) or 없다 (doesn't exist).

The rule: Verb stem + 을 수 있다/없다 (after a consonant) or + ㄹ 수 있다/없다 (after a vowel or ㄹ). e.g. 먹다 → 먹을 수 있다 (can eat), 가다 → 갈 수 있다 (can go), 만들다 → 만들 수 있다 (can make).
수영할 수 있어요
suyeonghal su isseoyo
I can swim
수영하다 → 수영할 수 있다
수영할 수 없어요
suyeonghal su eopseoyo
I cannot swim
없다 (to not exist) = cannot
한국어를 할 수 있어요
hangugeoreul hal su isseoyo
I can speak Korean
하다 → 할 수 있다
운전할 수 없어요
unjeonhal su eopseoyo
I cannot drive
운전하다 → 운전할 수 없다
여기 앉을 수 있어요?
yeogi anjeul su isseoyo?
Can I sit here?
앉다 → 앉을 수 있다
카드로 결제할 수 있어요?
kadeuro gyeoljaehal su isseoyo?
Can I pay by card?
결제하다 → 결제할 수 있다

In accommodation contexts:

수영장+ 이용할 수 있어요?= Can I use the swimming pool?
수영장 이용할 수 있어요?
Suyeongjang iyonghal su isseoyo?
Can I use the swimming pool?
이용하다 (iyonghada) = to use/utilise. Works for: 사우나 (sauna), 조식 (josik = breakfast), 주차장 (juchajang = car park).
짐을 맡길 수 있을까요?
Jimeul matgil su isseulkkayo?
Would it be possible to leave my luggage?
-(으)ㄹ까요 = "I wonder if / would it be possible" — a softer, more polite form of the question.
여기서 와이파이 쓸 수 있어요?
Yeogiseo waipai sseul su isseoyo?
Can I use Wi-Fi here?
쓰다 (sseuda) = to use/write. → 쓸 수 있다. One of the most asked questions at any accommodation!
-(으)ㄹ 수 있다 vs -(으)면 vs -(아/어)서 — keeping them straight: -(으)ㄹ 수 있다 = "can do" (ability/possibility). -(으)면 = "if/when" (condition). -(아/어)서 = "because/so" (cause). Together these three patterns cover a huge range of natural Korean — you now know all three!
Culture note
🏠
온돌 (ondol) — Korea's underfloor heating tradition
Ondol (온돌) is one of Korea's most ingenious architectural inventions — an underfloor heating system that circulates warm air or hot water beneath stone or wood floors. Dating back over 5,000 years, it's the reason Koreans traditionally sit and sleep on the floor rather than on chairs and beds. You'll feel it in hanok guesthouses, traditional homes, and any 온돌방 (ondol room) at a hotel or jjimjilbang. The experience of sleeping on a warm floor in winter, covered by a thick blanket (이불, ibul), is deeply comforting and quintessentially Korean. Modern apartments still use electric ondol — it's one of Korea's greatest gifts to indoor living.
