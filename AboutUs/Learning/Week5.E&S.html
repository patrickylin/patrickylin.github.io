Lesson 1 · Present simple vs present continuous
The distinction that defines English tense — and confuses almost every learner

This is the heart of English tense usage. Japanese and Korean don't make this distinction at all — one verb form covers both "I eat" and "I am eating". Spanish makes a weaker version of it. English is strict: the two tenses mean fundamentally different things and cannot be swapped.

Present Simple
I eat / She eats
📅 Habit or routine
🌍 General truth or fact
📍 Permanent situation
❤️ Opinion or preference
I eat breakfast at 7am. (habit)
Fish live in water. (fact)
She works in Tokyo. (permanent)
He likes jazz. (preference)
Present Continuous
I am eating / She is eating
⏱️ Happening right now
📆 Temporary situation
🔄 Changing / developing trend
📅 Future arrangement (informal)
I am eating lunch. (right now)
She is living in Tokyo this year. (temporary)
Prices are rising. (trend)
We are meeting at 3pm. (arranged)
The classic contrast pair — "I live" vs "I am living":
"I live in Tokyo." = This is my permanent home. I live there always.
"I am living in Tokyo." = I'm here temporarily — for a project, a year abroad, while looking for somewhere else.
Same verb, same city — completely different meaning! This nuance is invisible in Japanese (東京に住んでいます covers both) and in most learner English, where this distinction is frequently overlooked.
Lesson 2 · Forming the present continuous
am / is / are + verb-ing — simple to form, important to get right
PronounTo be+ verb-ingExample
I am working I am working from home today.
You are reading You are reading this right now!
He / She / It is eating She is eating lunch at her desk.
We are learning We are learning English together.
They are travelling They are travelling through Spain.

Negative and question forms:

Negative: She is not eating / She isn't eating / She's not eating
Question: Is she eating? · Are you working? · What are you doing?
Short answer: "Are you working?" → "Yes, I am." / "No, I'm not." (never "Yes, I'm.")

-ing spelling rules — 4 patterns to know:

1. Most verbs — just add -ing
Base form ends in consonant or vowel+consonant cluster
eat→eating · read→reading · sleep→sleeping · rain→raining
2. Drop silent -e, then add -ing
Base form ends in silent -e
make→making · write→writing · come→coming · live→living
3. Double the final consonant
Short verb, single vowel + single consonant
run→running · sit→sitting · swim→swimming · stop→stopping
4. -ie verbs → change to -y
Base form ends in -ie
die→dying · lie→lying · tie→tying
The doubling rule — when exactly? Double the final consonant only when the verb has ONE syllable AND ends in a single consonant preceded by a single vowel: run (r-u-n) → running ✅. But: read (has two vowels) → reading ✅ (no doubling). Visit (two syllables, stress on first) → visiting ✅ (no doubling). Begin (two syllables, stress on second) → beginning ✅ (double!). The stress position matters for two-syllable verbs.
Lesson 3 · Stative verbs — the verbs that refuse -ing
A critical rule: some verbs almost never use the continuous form

Stative verbs describe states rather than actions — mental states, emotions, senses, possessions. In English, these verbs strongly resist the continuous form. "I am knowing the answer" is wrong. "I know the answer" is correct. This rule is uniquely important to English — Spanish, Japanese, and Korean don't have this restriction.

Mental states
know · believe · think* · understand · remember · forget · mean · seem
*think = opinion (stative) · think = consider (action)
Emotions & feelings
love · hate · like · want · need · prefer · wish · care · fear
These describe states of feeling, not ongoing actions
Senses
see · hear · smell* · taste* · feel*
*smell/taste/feel can be actions: "She is smelling the flowers"
Possession
have* · own · possess · belong
*have = possession (stative) · "having lunch" = action ✅
Existence / being
be · exist · contain · consist · include
Exceptions: "You are being rude" = temporary behaviour ✅
Measurements
weigh · measure · cost · equal
These express facts, not ongoing processes
The "think" trap — stative vs dynamic: Some verbs have BOTH a stative meaning (no -ing) and a dynamic action meaning (uses -ing). "I think this is correct." = opinion/belief (stative — no -ing). "I am thinking about the problem." = ongoing mental process (dynamic — -ing OK!). Same applies to: "I have a car" (possession — no -ing) vs "I am having lunch" (action — -ing fine). The meaning shifts between state and action.

Culture note
💬
"What are you doing?" — the continuous as social glue
The present continuous does remarkable social work in English. "I'm just calling to say hello." "We're thinking of visiting Japan." "She's doing really well." Notice how the continuous tense softens and humanises statements — it implies things are in motion, in progress, alive. Compared to the blunter present simple ("I call." "We think about visiting." "She does well."), the continuous form gives English conversation a warmer, more tentative, more human quality. Many learners over-use the present simple when native speakers would reach for continuous — and the result, while grammatically defensible, can sound unexpectedly cold or abrupt.

Lesson 1 · The Spanish past — an overview
Spanish has two main past tenses — this week we learn the first and most essential one

Spanish has several past tenses, but two are critical for beginners: the pretérito indefinido (preterite) and the pretérito imperfecto (imperfect). This week focuses on the preterite — used for completed actions with a clear endpoint in the past.

When to use pretérito indefinido:
✅ Completed past actions
Ayer comí sushi. (Yesterday I ate sushi.)
Fui a Madrid en 2022. (I went to Madrid in 2022.)
🕐 Specific time: ayer, anoche, el lunes, en 2020, hace dos años
🔢 Number of times: Lo visité tres veces. (I visited it three times.)
📋 Sequence of events: Llegué, comí, y dormí. (I arrived, ate, and slept.)
⏱️ Actions with a clear beginning and end: Estudié español durante dos horas. (I studied Spanish for two hours.)
A useful memory rule: The pretérito indefinido is the "snap photo" tense — it captures a completed moment or sequence of moments. The imperfect (coming in Week 7) is the "video camera" tense — it describes ongoing background situations. "Llovía cuando llegué" = It was raining (ongoing background — imperfect) when I arrived (completed moment — preterite).

The written accent marks on yo (-é, -í) and él/ella (-ó, -ió) distinguish past from present. Omitting them is a spelling error that can genuinely confuse meaning — take them seriously.
Lesson 3 · Irregular preterite verbs — the essential 10
These don't follow the rules — but they appear in almost every conversation

The most irregular preterites in Spanish are unfortunately the most common verbs. The good news: ser and ir share identical past forms (context always clarifies which is which!), and most of the common irregulars follow an "-uv-", "-ij-", or "-u-" stem pattern.

ser / ir

The ser / ir coincidence: Ser (to be) and ir (to go) have exactly the same preterite forms — fui, fuiste, fue, fuimos, fuisteis, fueron. This sounds alarming but almost never causes confusion in practice, because context makes the meaning obvious: "Fui al supermercado" = I went to the supermarket (ir). "Fue una sorpresa" = It was a surprise (ser). Spanish grammar's occasional coincidences are actually a gift — one set of forms to memorise for two verbs.

These words signal a completed past action — always use the preterite with them
📅

/aˈʝer/ · /aˈnotʃe/ · /anteaˈʝer/

Culture note
📖
Spain vs Latin America — two different past tenses in daily use
Here is one of the most striking regional differences in Spanish: in Spain, the pretérito perfecto (he comido = I have eaten) is used for recent past actions connected to the present — exactly like English "I have eaten." The pretérito indefinido (comí) is reserved for more distant, disconnected past events. But in most of Latin America, the pretérito indefinido (comí) is used for ALL past actions, recent or distant — the perfecto (he comido) is used much less. So a Spaniard says "Esta mañana he desayunado" (This morning I have had breakfast) while a Mexican says "Esta mañana desayuné" (This morning I had breakfast). Both are completely correct — just different regional preferences. This week's preterite indefinido is safe and understood everywhere.

The simple/continuous insight: English is unique among these four languages in strictly requiring a different form for "right now" vs "habitually". Spanish has the estar + gerund (estoy comiendo) but uses it less rigidly — simple present works fine for right now. Japanese and Korean handle both meanings with one form, relying on context (今 = now, 毎日 = every day) to clarify. English forces the distinction grammatically. This is one reason English feels unforgiving to learners — you cannot take a shortcut.
