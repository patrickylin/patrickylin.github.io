Lesson 1 · Formal & informal email writing
The complete toolkit for written English — from job applications to messages to friends

Written English requires conscious register choices at every stage — from the opening salutation to the closing sign-off. The gap between formal and informal written English is wider than in most languages, and getting it wrong can damage credibility just as surely as a grammar error.

📧 Formal email — job application, professional, unknown recipient
Opening salutation: Dear Mr/Ms [surname], · Dear Sir or Madam, (if name unknown) · To Whom It May Concern, (very formal/impersonal)
Opening line: I am writing to enquire about... · I am writing with reference to... · Further to our conversation... · I would like to express my interest in...
Body language: I would be grateful if... · Please find attached... · I would appreciate your assistance with... · It would be helpful to know... · As per our previous correspondence...
Closing: I look forward to hearing from you. · Please do not hesitate to contact me. · Thank you for your time and consideration.
Sign-off: Yours sincerely, [name] (when you know the name) · Yours faithfully, [name] (when you wrote "Dear Sir/Madam")
The sincerely/faithfully rule: if you used a name → sincerely. If you wrote "Dear Sir/Madam" → faithfully. Breaking this rule is a classic error.
💬 Informal email / message — friend, colleague you know well
Opening salutation: Hi [first name], · Hey [name], · Hope you're well — (no comma needed in very casual messages)
Opening line: Hope this finds you well! · Just wanted to drop you a line... · Quick question — · Hope you're having a good week!
Body language: I was wondering if... · Would it be possible to...? · Let me know what you think · Just to let you know... · FYI... · As I mentioned...
Closing: Let me know if you have any questions! · Looking forward to it! · Speak soon! · Thanks so much!
Sign-off: Best, / Best wishes, / Kind regards, [name] · Thanks, [name] · Cheers, [name] · [name] alone (most casual)
Contractions (I'm, I'd, we'll) are expected in informal — and increasingly acceptable in semi-formal. Contractions in a formal letter signal carelessness.
Kind regards — the neutral middle ground: "Kind regards" has become the dominant sign-off in professional English because it sits perfectly between warm and formal. It works for emails to colleagues, clients, and new contacts where "Yours sincerely" feels stiff but "Cheers" feels too casual. "Best regards" is slightly warmer. "Regards" alone is slightly cooler. "Warm regards" implies a closer relationship. Learning to calibrate sign-offs is a small but telling mark of written fluency.
Lesson 2 · The most common English writing mistakes
The errors that damage credibility — and how to fix them permanently
Its vs It's
❌ The company changed it's policy.
✅ The company changed its policy.
It's = it is / it has. Its = belonging to it (possessive). The apostrophe does NOT show possession for "it" — it shows a missing letter.
There / Their / They're
❌ Their going to there office.
✅ They're going to their office.
They're = they are. Their = belonging to them. There = that place / introductory word. Three completely different words — same sound.
Apostrophe in plurals
❌ The 1980's were amazing. Three cat's sat down.
✅ The 1980s were amazing. Three cats sat down.
Apostrophes do NOT make plurals. Never add apostrophe to make a noun plural. The grocer's apostrophe ("apple's £1") is a famous British error.
Fewer vs Less
❌ Less people attended. Less chairs.
✅ Fewer people attended. Fewer chairs.
Fewer = countable nouns (fewer books, fewer people). Less = uncountable nouns (less water, less time). Supermarkets often write "10 items or less" — technically wrong but widely accepted.
Effect vs Affect
❌ The rain effected our plans. What is the affect?
✅ The rain affected our plans. What is the effect?
Affect = verb (to affect something). Effect = noun (the effect). Memory trick: RAVEN — Remember, Affect is a Verb, Effect is a Noun.
Who vs Whom
❌ Who did you speak to? (formal writing)
✅ To whom did you speak? (formal writing)
Who = subject (who did it). Whom = object (to/for/by whom). Test: replace with he/him. "He spoke" → who. "Spoke to him" → whom. Whom is vanishing from speech but still expected in formal writing.
Sentence fragments
❌ Because I was tired. Which is interesting.
✅ I left early because I was tired. This is interesting.
A sentence must have a subject and a main verb. Fragments beginning with because/which/although/however are common — each needs a main clause attached.
Comma splice
❌ I finished the report, I sent it immediately.
✅ I finished the report and sent it. / I finished the report; I sent it immediately.
Two independent clauses cannot be joined with just a comma. Use a conjunction (and, but, so), a semicolon, or a full stop. The comma splice is very common in learner writing.
Lesson 3 · Punctuation — the road signs of written English
The marks that make meaning — and the ones that cause most confusion
.
Full stop / Period
Ends a statement. Also used in abbreviations (Mr. Dr.) in American English — British often omits these (Mr Dr).
She arrived early. The meeting began at noon.
,
Comma
Separates list items, introductory clauses, non-defining clauses. The Oxford comma (before "and" in lists) is optional but prevents ambiguity.
I bought bread, milk, and cheese. However, she disagreed.
;
Semicolon
Links two closely related independent clauses. Stronger than a comma, weaker than a full stop. Also separates complex list items.
She loved the city; he preferred the countryside.
:
Colon
Introduces a list, explanation, or quotation. What follows must relate directly to what precedes.
She had one goal: to finish the project on time.
—
Em dash
Creates a strong pause — more emphatic than a comma. Used to add information, create drama, or show an interruption. Very flexible in English.
He had one talent — the ability to listen carefully.
'
Apostrophe
Two uses only: (1) contractions — it's, don't, she's. (2) possession — John's bag, the children's toys. NEVER for plurals.
It's John's responsibility. The teachers' meeting.

Culture note
✍️
Why written English matters more than ever — and why it is increasingly rare
In an era of voice messages, emoji, and autocorrect, the ability to write clear, correct, well-structured English has become a genuine differentiator. Surveys consistently show that poor written English damages professional credibility — hiring managers report rejecting CVs for apostrophe errors, and business communication studies find that spelling and punctuation mistakes reduce perceived intelligence and trustworthiness. Yet writing standards in native English speakers are also declining — schools teach less formal writing, and informal digital communication has blurred conventions. This means that a non-native English speaker who writes clearly, correctly, and with appropriate register can actually outshine many native speakers in professional contexts. The investment in learning formal written English is not nostalgic — it is strategic. Clear writing is clear thinking made visible, and it is always impressive regardless of where the writer comes from.

Lesson 1 · El tiempo — weather vocabulary
¿Qué tiempo hace? — talking about the weather in Spanish

Spanish uses two main structures for weather: hacer (to make/do) for most conditions and haber (impersonal — hay) for precipitation and fog. A third option is using estar with adjectives. Unlike English, Spanish doesn't say "it is raining" with a subject pronoun — the verb itself is impersonal.

Hacer + weather noun: Hace calor / frío / sol / viento / buen/mal tiempo.
Haber (hay) + noun: Hay niebla / tormenta / nubes. · Hay mucho viento.
Llover/Nevar (impersonal): Llueve (it rains) · Está lloviendo (it is raining) · Nieva (it snows).
Estar + adjective: El día está nublado / despejado / soleado.

Lesson 2 · Las estaciones del año — the seasons
Spain's four seasons — and the very different climates across the country
🌸
la primavera
spring
March–May. Mild temperatures, wildflowers, rain in the north. Semana Santa (Holy Week) falls in spring. Las flores brotan (the flowers bloom). La temporada de alergia (hay fever season).
☀️
el verano
summer
June–August. Very hot in the interior (40°C+). Crowded coasts. La siesta becomes essential. Los incendios forestales (forest fires) are a serious issue. School holidays: julio y agosto.
🍂
el otoño
autumn
September–November. Harvest season. Las vendimias (grape harvests) in Rioja and other wine regions. Temperatures drop pleasantly. La temporada de setas (mushroom season) — a beloved pastime.
❄️
el invierno
winter
December–February. Mild on coasts, cold in the interior, snow in the mountains. La Navidad and Los Reyes Magos are the great winter celebrations. La temporada de esquí (ski season) in the Pyrenees and Sierra Nevada.
🌡️
¿A cuántos grados estamos hoy?
/a ˈkwantos ˈɡɾaðos esˈtamos ˈoi/
What temperature is it today? (how many degrees are we at?)
Spain uses Celsius exclusively. Estamos a treinta grados = We're at 30 degrees. La temperatura máxima/mínima = the maximum/minimum temperature. La previsión dice que... = the forecast says that...
🌦️
Parece que va a llover / a hacer calor.
/paˈɾeθe ke ˈβa a ʎoˈβeɾ/
It looks like it's going to rain / be hot.
Parecer que + ir a + infinitive = it seems like it's going to. Also: Creo que va a cambiar el tiempo (I think the weather is going to change). Se avecina tormenta = a storm is approaching (more dramatic).
Lesson 3 · Ya / Todavía — the Spanish already/still/yet/anymore
Two tiny words with enormous nuance — depending entirely on context

Ya and todavía (also aún) are among the most versatile and context-dependent words in Spanish. Their meaning shifts with tense, negation, and intonation — making them fascinating to study and occasionally confusing to master.

YA — versatile word of completion, immediacy, and resigned acceptance:
Ya + present/perfect = already: Ya he comido. (I've already eaten.)
Ya + future = soon / eventually: Ya llamará. (He'll call eventually.)
Ya no = not anymore: Ya no vivo allí. (I don't live there anymore.)
¡Ya! alone = right! / yes! / OK! / I know! — depends entirely on tone
Ya voy = I'm coming! (lit. "I go now" — response to being called)

TODAVÍA / AÚN — still, yet, still not:
Todavía + positive = still: Todavía vive aquí. (He still lives here.)
Todavía no / Aún no = not yet: Todavía no he llegado. (I haven't arrived yet.)
¿Todavía...? in question = still? / Is [thing] still happening?
Ya vs todavía contrast pairs:
¿Ya has comido? = Have you eaten yet? / Have you already eaten?
¿Todavía no has comido? = Haven't you eaten yet? (surprised — it's late!)
Ya no tengo hambre. = I'm no longer hungry. (I was, but not now.)
Todavía tengo hambre. = I'm still hungry. (I was and still am.)
The pair mirrors English already/yet vs still — but ya does even more duty than "already".

Culture note
🌞
El tiempo en España — why weather is more than small talk
In Britain, weather is the canonical small talk subject — a safe, neutral topic for strangers. In Spain, it's also discussed constantly — but with greater emotional intensity. A summer heat wave (una ola de calor) is a genuine social emergency: work schedules adjust, schools close, the elderly are monitored. The calima — a hot, dust-laden wind from the Sahara that turns the sky yellow-orange — arrives several times a year and is discussed with dramatic flair. The gota fría (cold drop — a meteorological phenomenon causing extreme rainfall) produces catastrophic flooding in Valencia and the Mediterranean coast every few years, and its name alone evokes dread. Spanish weather is extreme by Northern European standards — 45°C summers in Seville, -15°C winters in Burgos, and some of the highest sunshine hours in Europe on the Canary Islands. Understanding weather vocabulary in Spanish is also understanding one of the country's most fundamental daily realities.

The weather grammar insight: Languages treat weather very differently grammatically. English uses a dummy "it" that refers to nothing — purely a grammatical placeholder. Japanese and Korean make the weather phenomenon (rain, heat) the actual subject. Spanish uses a completely subjectless impersonal construction with hacer or the impersonal verb llueve/nieva — no subject at all. These choices reflect deep grammatical differences: English requires a subject in every sentence; Spanish does not. Spanish's impersonal weather expressions are more honest, in a way — the rain just rains, with nobody and nothing doing the raining.
