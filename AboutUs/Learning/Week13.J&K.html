Lesson 2 · Workplace & study phrases
Talking about work and study naturally in Japanese
🏢
〜の仕事をしています
[field]-no shigoto wo shite imasu
I work in [field]
仕事 (shigoto) = work/job. e.g. IT の仕事をしています = I work in IT. Use this alongside your job title for a complete answer.
🏙️
〜に勤めています
[company name]-ni tsutomete imasu
I work at [company]
勤める (tsutomeru) = to work at/be employed by. More specific than 働いています — used when naming a company.
📚
日本語を勉強しています
Nihongo wo benkyō shite imasu
I am studying Japanese
A sentence you can genuinely say with pride right now! 勉強している (benkyō shite iru) = is studying. Add 趣味で (shumi de = as a hobby) to clarify it's recreational.
🕐
今日は会議があります
Kyō wa kaigi ga arimasu
I have a meeting today
会議 (kaigi) = meeting. 打ち合わせ (uchiawase) = informal work meeting/briefing. 出張 (shutchō) = business trip.
🌙
残業しなければなりません
Zangyō shinakereba narimasen
I have to work overtime
A preview of the grammar you're about to learn! 残業 (zangyō) = overtime. This sentence is unfortunately very common in Japanese office culture.
🤝
お疲れ様です
Otsukaresama desu
Thank you for your hard work
One of the most important Japanese workplace phrases. Said to colleagues throughout and at the end of the day — literally "you must be tired." There's no direct English equivalent.
🚪
行ってきます / 行ってらっしゃい
Ittekimasu / Itterasshai
I'm heading out / Take care (response)
Said when leaving home or the office. The person staying responds with いってらっしゃい. A deeply ingrained daily ritual in Japanese life and work.
Lesson 3 · Formal email phrases
Japanese business email — the most formal register you'll encounter

Japanese business emails (ビジネスメール, bijinesu mēru) follow a strict structure. Even if you're writing to a Japanese colleague in English, knowing these phrases shows cultural respect and sophistication.

📧 ビジネスメール — Business email template
〜株式会社 田中様
[Company] Kabushiki-gaisha Tanaka-sama
To: Mr/Ms Tanaka, [Company] Co., Ltd.
お世話になっております
Osewa ni natte orimasu
"Thank you for your continued support" — mandatory opening for business emails to people you've contacted before
〜の件でご連絡いたしました
[topic]-no ken de go-renraku itashimashita
I am writing regarding [topic]
ご確認のほど、よろしくお願い申し上げます
Go-kakunin no hodo, yoroshiku onegai moushiagemasu
I would be grateful if you could review this / Please check this at your convenience
ご不明な点がございましたら、お気軽にお問い合わせください
Go-fumei na ten ga gozaimashitara, okigaru ni otoiawase kudasai
If you have any questions, please feel free to contact us
どうぞよろしくお願いいたします
Dōzo yoroshiku onegai itashimasu
Closing salutation — "I look forward to your continued cooperation"
Key email vocabulary: 様 (sama) = honorific for individuals in emails (never さん) · 株式会社 (kabushiki-gaisha) = Co., Ltd. · 件 (ken) = matter/subject · ご連絡 (go-renraku) = contact · ご確認 (go-kakunin) = confirmation/review · お問い合わせ (otoiawase) = enquiry.
Lesson 4 · Grammar — 〜なければならない
"Must / have to do" — expressing obligation in Japanese

This pattern expresses obligation or necessity — things you must do or have to do. It's made of the verb's negative conditional form + ならない (must not fail to). It's essential for talking about work deadlines, rules, and duties.

Formation: Take the ない-form (negative) of any verb, change ない → なければ, then add ならない or なりません (polite). e.g. 行く → 行かない → 行かなければならない = must go. The casual short form 〜なきゃ (nakya) is very common in conversation.
Dictionary 〜なければならない form Meaning & example
行くiku (to go) 行かなければなりませんikanakereba narimasen Must go会議に行かなければなりません。= I must go to the meeting.
勉強するbenkyō suru (to study) 勉強しなければなりませんbenkyō shinakereba narimasen Must studyもっと勉強しなければなりません。= I must study more.
食べるtaberu (to eat) 食べなければなりませんtabenakereba narimasen Must eat野菜を食べなければなりません。= I must eat vegetables.
提出するteishutsu suru (to submit) 提出しなければなりませんteishutsu shinakereba narimasen Must submit今日中に提出しなければなりません。= I must submit it by today.
帰るkaeru (to return home) 帰らなければなりませんkaeranakereba narimasen Must go homeもう帰らなければなりません。= I have to go home now.
Casual shortcut — 〜なきゃ (nakya): In daily conversation, the full form is often shortened. 行かなければならない → 行かなきゃ (ikanakya = gotta go). 勉強しなければ → 勉強しなきゃ (benkyō shinakya = gotta study). This is what you'll hear from friends and colleagues constantly — know it when you hear it!

Culture note
🏢
働き方改革 (hataraki-kata kaikaku) — Japan's work style reform
Japan has long been famous — and sometimes infamous — for its extreme work culture. The concept of 過労死 (karōshi = death from overwork) entered global consciousness as a genuine social crisis. In recent years, the government launched 働き方改革 (Work Style Reform) to cap overtime hours, mandate paid leave use, and promote remote work. The culture is genuinely changing — especially in tech and creative industries. Words like ワークライフバランス (work-life balance), テレワーク (telework), and 副業 (fukugyō = side job) are increasingly common. If you work with Japanese colleagues, being aware of this ongoing shift shows cultural awareness far beyond basic language ability.

Lesson 2 · Work & study phrases in Korean
Talking about your work and studies naturally
💼
〜에서 일하고 있어요
[company/place]-eseo ilhago isseoyo
I work at [company/place]
에서 (eseo) = at (a place). 일하다 (ilhada) = to work. e.g. 학교에서 일하고 있어요 = I work at a school.
📚
한국어를 공부하고 있어요
Hangugeoreul gongbuhago isseoyo
I am studying Korean
A sentence you can say with genuine pride! 공부하고 있어요 = is studying (ongoing). Add 취미로 (chwimiro = as a hobby) if studying recreationally.
🌙
오늘 야근해야 해요
Oneul yageunhaeya haeyo
I have to work overtime today
야근하다 (yageunhada) = to work overtime/at night. A preview of the grammar pattern you're about to learn! Very relatable in Korea.
🤝
수고하셨습니다
Sugohasheosseumnida
You've worked hard / Well done (formal)
The Korean equivalent of お疲れ様です. Said to colleagues at end of day or after completing a task. 수고했어요 (informal) or 수고해 (casual). Use freely — it's universally appreciated.
📋
마감이 언제예요?
Magami eonjeyeyo?
When is the deadline?
마감 (magam) = deadline/closing. 언제 (eonje) = when. 마감이 촉박해요 (magami chokbakaeyo) = the deadline is tight.
🎯
열심히 하겠습니다
Yeolsimhi hagesseumnida
I will do my best / I'll work hard
열심히 (yeolsimhi) = diligently/hard. One of the most respected phrases in Korean work culture — showing commitment and effort is deeply valued.
Lesson 3 · K-pop & K-drama vocabulary
Korea's global cultural exports — the vocabulary behind the phenomenon

K-pop and K-dramas have introduced millions of people worldwide to Korean culture and language. Knowing the vocabulary behind these scenes helps you connect with Korean people and understand references in everyday conversation.

🎤
아이돌
aidol
Idol (K-pop artist)
A K-pop idol undergoes years of training (연습생, yeonseupssaeng = trainee) before debut. Groups like BTS (방탄소년단), BLACKPINK, and aespa have global fanbases in the hundreds of millions.
💃
안무
anmu
Choreography / Dance moves
K-pop choreography (안무) is an art form — performed with military precision. 칼군무 (kalgungmu = knife-sharp group dancing) describes perfectly synchronised group movement.
👑
팬덤
paendeom
Fandom
K-pop fandoms have names — BTS fans are 아미 (ARMY), BLACKPINK fans are 블링크 (BLINK). Fandom loyalty and participation (응원, eungwon = cheering) are central to K-pop culture.
🎵
컴백
keombaek
Comeback (new release)
In K-pop, 컴백 (comeback) means an artist's return with new music — even if they never went away! It's the equivalent of a "new album release." Accompanied by promotion on music shows (음악방송, eumagbangsong).
📺
드라마
deurama
K-drama (Korean TV drama)
Korean dramas typically run 16 episodes, airing twice weekly. Genres include 로맨스 (romance), 스릴러 (thriller), 사극 (sageuk = historical drama). Globally distributed on Netflix, Viki, and Wavve.
😭
설레다
seolleda
Heart fluttering / excited nervousness
A uniquely Korean emotional concept — the excited, nervous, heart-fluttering feeling of a crush or romantic moment. Extremely common in dramas. There's no single English word for it.
💔
짝사랑
jjaksarang
One-sided love / unrequited love
짝 (jjak) = one side, 사랑 (sarang) = love. One of the most common K-drama storylines. 첫사랑 (cheot-sarang) = first love — another cornerstone of Korean romantic vocabulary.
🌟
주인공
juingong
Main character / protagonist
주인공 신드롬 (juingong sindeurom = main character syndrome) — the K-drama trope of the lead having extraordinary luck. 조연 (joyeon) = supporting character. 악역 (aknyeok) = villain.
K-pop vocabulary you'll hear constantly: 데뷔 (debut) · 솔로 (solo) · 그룹 (group) · 뮤직비디오 (music video) / 엠비 (MV) · 음원차트 (music chart) · 1위 (1st place) · 콘서트 (concert) · 사인회 (fan signing event) · 직캠 (fancam — individual focus video). Learning these unlocks a huge amount of Korean content online.
Lesson 4 · Grammar — -(아/어)야 하다
"Must / have to do" — expressing necessity and duty in Korean

-(아/어)야 하다 expresses obligation, necessity, or duty. It's the Korean equivalent of "must" or "have to" — and it's one of the most useful patterns in daily Korean conversation, from work deadlines to personal responsibilities.

The rule: Take the 아/어-form of the verb (same as -(아/어)서 from Week 10!) and add 야 하다. Bright vowels (ㅏ/ㅗ) → 아야 하다. Dark vowels → 어야 하다. 하다 verbs → 해야 하다. e.g. 가다 → 가야 해요 (must go), 먹다 → 먹어야 해요 (must eat), 공부하다 → 공부해야 해요 (must study).
가야 해요
gaya haeyo
Must go / Have to go
가다 → 가 + 야 하다
공부해야 해요
gongbuhaeya haeyo
Must study
공부하다 → 공부해 + 야 하다
먹어야 해요
meogeoya haeyo
Must eat
먹다 → 먹어 + 야 하다
일어나야 해요
ireona ya haeyo
Must get up
일어나다 → 일어나 + 야 하다
안 가도 돼요
an gado dwaeyo
Don't have to go / It's OK not to go
안 + verb + 도 돼요 = don't have to. Different from "must not"!
가면 안 돼요
gamyeon an dwaeyo
Must not go / Cannot go
-(으)면 안 되다 = must not / prohibited. Critical distinction!
Three important distinctions: 가야 해요 = "must go" (obligation). 안 가도 돼요 = "don't have to go" (no obligation). 가면 안 돼요 = "must not go" (prohibition). Getting these mixed up can cause real confusion — memorise all three!

-(아/어)야 하다 in work and daily life:

오늘 마감이라서 일찍 일어나야 해요.
Oneul magamiraso iljjik ireonaya haeyo.
Because today is the deadline, I have to get up early.
마감이라서 (because it's deadline) + 일찍 (early) + 일어나야 해요 (must get up). Uses -(아/어)서 from Week 10!
한국어를 더 열심히 공부해야 해요.
Hangugeoreul deo yeolsimhi gongbuhaeya haeyo.
I must study Korean harder.
더 열심히 = harder/more diligently. A sentence you can genuinely say — and mean!
드라마 보기 전에 숙제를 해야 해요.
Deurama bogi jeone sukjereul haeya haeyo.
I must do my homework before watching dramas.
보기 전에 (before watching) + 숙제 (homework) + 해야 해요 (must do). 전에 = before — a new useful connector!

Culture note
🌏
한류 (Hallyu) — the Korean Wave
한류 (Hallyu = Korean Wave) refers to the global spread of Korean popular culture — music, dramas, films, food, beauty, and language — that began in the 1990s and exploded globally in the 2010s. BTS broke Western chart records. Parasite won the Academy Award for Best Picture. Squid Game became Netflix's most-watched series. Approximately 100 million people worldwide are now estimated to be learning Korean — many motivated entirely by love of Korean pop culture. This makes Korean one of the fastest-growing language learning communities in the world. You're part of this wave. The connection between pop culture enthusiasm and language acquisition is no accident — emotional investment in a culture creates the deepest, most durable learning.
