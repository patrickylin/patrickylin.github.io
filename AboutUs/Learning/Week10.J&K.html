Lesson 1 · Self-introduction (自己紹介)
How to introduce yourself naturally in Japanese

A Japanese self-introduction follows a fairly standard script. Knowing the structure means you can slot in your own details with confidence.

🙇
はじめまして
Hajimemashite
How do you do / Nice to meet you (first time)
Always said when meeting someone for the first time. Literally means "for the first time". A light bow accompanies this.
👤
〜と申します
[name]-to moushimasu
My name is [name] (formal)
と申します is the humble/formal way to give your name. Casual version: 〜といいます (to iimasu) or simply 〜です.
🌍
〜から来ました
[country/city]-kara kimashita
I came from [place]
から (kara) = from. アメリカ (Amerika), イギリス (Igirisu = UK), オーストラリア (Ōsutoraria), カナダ (Kanada).
💼
〜をしています
[job/activity]-wo shite imasu
I work as / I do [job]
エンジニア (enjinia), 教師 (kyōshi = teacher), 学生 (gakusei = student), 医者 (isha = doctor), 会社員 (kaishain = office worker).
🙏
よろしくお願いします
Yoroshiku onegaishimasu
I look forward to your acquaintance / Please treat me well
Said at the end of every self-introduction — there's no direct English equivalent. It's a warm expression of goodwill and the hope for a good relationship. Always bow here.
Full introduction template: はじめまして。[name]と申します。[country]から来ました。[job]をしています。よろしくお願いします。— Practice slotting in your own name, country, and job. This 5-line introduction will serve you in almost every formal first meeting in Japan.

Lesson 3 · Expressing opinions
How to agree, disagree, and share your views politely
そうですね
sō desu ne
That's right / I agree
The most common agreement response in Japanese.
なるほど
naruhodo
I see / I understand (that makes sense)
Shows you're listening and processing. Very natural.
〜と思います
[statement]-to omoimasu
I think that ~ (soft opinion)
e.g. いいと思います = I think it's good.
〜かもしれません
kamoshiremasen
It might be ~ / Perhaps ~
Softens any statement — very Japanese!
ちょっと違うと思います
chotto chigau to omoimasu
I think it's a little different (polite disagreement)
Never say a flat "no" — always soften it.
本当ですか？
hontō desu ka?
Really? / Is that true?
Shows engagement. Equivalent of "oh really?"
すごいですね！
sugoi desu ne!
That's amazing / Wow!
One of the most useful reactive phrases.
難しいですね
muzukashii desu ne
That's difficult, isn't it
A safe response when something is complex or uncertain.

Lesson 5 · Keigo (敬語) — polite speech basics
Japan's speech level system — the most important thing to understand about Japanese social language

Japanese has three main speech levels. You don't need to master all of them — but you need to understand why the same verb has completely different forms, and know which level to use in which situation.

Level Name Example — "to eat" When to use
Formal 丁寧語
teineigo		 食べます tabemasu With strangers, in shops, with customers, in service settings. This is the level you've been learning throughout this course — it ends in ます/です.
Honorific 尊敬語
sonkeigo		 召し上がります meshiagarimasu When talking about what someone senior to you (a boss, customer, elder) does — you elevate their actions. Used heavily in business and formal service.
Casual 普通体
futsūtai		 食べる taberu With close friends, family, people your own age. The dictionary form you've seen in grammar charts. Only use this once you have a clear close friendship.
As a beginner, always use 丁寧語 (teineigo — the ます/です form). Japanese people expect foreigners to speak formally and will never be offended by it. Using casual speech (plain form) with someone you've just met can feel rude. When in doubt: add ます and です to everything. You've been doing this all course — keep it up!

Keigo in action — 3 common scenarios:

🏢
〜さんはいらっしゃいますか？
[name]-san wa irasshaimasu ka?
Is [name] available? (on the phone / at the door)
いらっしゃる is the honorific form of いる (to be/exist). Used when asking about someone senior. Very common in business and formal settings.
🎁
どうぞ、お召し上がりください
Dōzo, omeshiagari kudasai
Please help yourself / Please eat
Said when offering food to a guest. お召し上がりください is the full honorific "please eat". You'll hear this in restaurants and at formal gatherings.
📞
少々お待ちいただけますか？
Shōshō omachi itadakemasu ka?
Could you please wait a moment?
The formal version of 待ってください. You'll hear this in shops, hotels, and on the phone. 少々 (shōshō) = a little while.

Culture note
💼
名刺 (meishi) — the art of the business card exchange
In Japan, exchanging business cards (名刺交換, meishi kōkan) is a social ritual as important as a handshake in the West. Cards are presented and received with both hands, with a slight bow. The card is then studied carefully — never write on it, fold it, or put it in your back pocket in front of the giver. Place it respectfully on the table in front of you during a meeting. If you don't have a card, apologise gracefully: 申し訳ありませんが、名刺を切らしております (moushiwake arimasen ga, meishi wo kirashite orimasu = I apologise, I've run out of business cards). This attention to objects as extensions of the person is a key expression of Japanese respect culture.

Lesson 1 · Self-introduction (자기소개)
Introduce yourself naturally in Korean
🙌
처음 뵙겠습니다
Cheoeum boepgesseumnida
How do you do / Nice to meet you (formal, first time)
The formal equivalent of はじめまして. 처음 = first time. 뵙다 = humble form of "to see/meet". Use with seniors or in formal settings.
👤
저는 〜라고 합니다
Jeoneun [name]-rago hamnida
My name is ~ (formal)
라고 하다 = to be called. Casual version: 저는 〜예요 / 〜이에요. 저 (jeo) = formal "I", 나 (na) = casual "I".
🌍
저는 〜에서 왔어요
Jeoneun [place]-eseo wasseoyo
I am from [place]
에서 (eseo) = from (a place). 미국 (miguk) = USA · 영국 (yeongguk) = UK · 호주 (hoju) = Australia · 캐나다 (kaenada) = Canada.
💼
저는 〜을/를 하고 있어요
Jeoneun [job]-eul/reul hago isseoyo
I work as / I do [job]
엔지니어 (enjinieo = engineer) · 선생님 (seonsaengnim = teacher) · 학생 (haksaeng = student) · 의사 (uisa = doctor) · 회사원 (hoesawon = office worker).
😊
잘 부탁드립니다
Jal butakdeurimnida
I look forward to working with you / Please take care of me
The Korean equivalent of よろしくお願いします — said at the end of introductions to express goodwill. Slightly more formal: 잘 부탁드립니다. Casual: 잘 부탁해요.
Full introduction template: 처음 뵙겠습니다. 저는 [name]라고 합니다. [country]에서 왔어요. [job]을/를 하고 있어요. 잘 부탁드립니다. — Exactly mirrors the Japanese structure, making it easy to remember both!

Lesson 3 · Weekend plans & small talk
Natural Korean conversation starters
📅
주말에 뭐 해요?
Jumare mwo haeyo?
What are you doing this weekend?
주말 (jumal) = weekend. 뭐 (mwo) = what. The most natural small talk opener in Korean — expect to be asked this constantly.
🤷
아직 모르겠어요
Ajik moreugesseoyo
I don't know yet / I'm not sure yet
아직 = yet/still. A natural, relaxed response when you haven't made plans.
😄
같이 가요!
Gachi gayo!
Let's go together!
같이 (gachi) = together. One of the warmest invitations in Korean — Koreans are very group-oriented and love including people.
🎉
재미있었어요!
Jaemiisseosseoyo!
It was fun / That was great!
재미있다 (jaemiitda) = to be fun/interesting. Past tense: 재미있었어요. The go-to response after any positive experience.
🥺
다음에 또 만나요
Daeume tto mannayo
Let's meet again next time
다음에 = next time · 또 = again · 만나다 = to meet. A warm, natural goodbye after a social outing.
💬
한국어 잘 하시네요!
Hangugeo jal hasineyo!
Your Korean is great!
You'll receive this often — respond modestly: 아니에요, 아직 많이 부족해요 (anievo, ajik mani bujokaeyo = No, I'm still lacking a lot).
Lesson 4 · Giving and receiving compliments
8 Korean compliments — and how to respond modestly
멋있어요!
meositsseoyo!
You're cool / awesome!
For people, style, actions. Very common among younger Koreans.
귀여워요!
gwiyeowoyo!
So cute!
For people, animals, objects — widely used and appreciated.
예뻐요!
yeppeoyo!
Beautiful / Pretty!
For people and things. 잘생겼어요 (jalsaenggyeosseoyo) = handsome (for men).
정말 맛있어요!
jeongmal masisseoyo!
It's really delicious!
정말 = really/truly. Adding 정말 intensifies any compliment.
대단해요!
daedanhaeyo!
That's amazing / impressive!
For impressive achievements or skills. 대단하다 = to be great/impressive.
잘 어울려요!
jal eoullyeoyo!
It suits you well!
어울리다 = to suit/match. Say this about someone's outfit, hairstyle, or item they've just bought.
Responding to compliments Korean-style: Koreans typically deflect compliments modestly. When complimented, say 아니에요 (anievo = no, not at all), 과찬이세요 (gwa-chaniseyo = you're too kind), or 별말씀을요 (byeolmalsseumeullyo = not at all). Accepting a compliment directly can seem arrogant — modest deflection is the culturally warm response.
Lesson 5 · Grammar — -(아/어)서 connector
"Because ~ / So ~" — the cause-and-effect connector that makes speech natural

The -(아/어)서 ending connects two clauses to express cause and effect or sequential actions. It's one of the most natural-sounding connectors in Korean — and once you start using it, your sentences immediately sound more fluent.

The rule: Verb/adjective stem + 아서 (bright vowels ㅏ/ㅗ) or + 어서 (all other vowels). e.g. 좋다 → 좋아서 (because it's good), 먹다 → 먹어서 (because I ate), 하다 → 해서 (because I do/did). Note: -(아/어)서 cannot be used with tense markers — the tense shows in the final verb only.

Using -(아/어)서 for cause and effect:

Verb stem + 아서/어서 → "because ~" / "so ~"
피곤해서 일찍 잤어요.
Pigonhaeseo iljjik jasseoyo.
Because I was tired, I went to bed early.
피곤하다 (tired) → 피곤해서. The cause always comes first.
배가 고파서 밥을 먹었어요.
Baega gopaseo babeul meogeosseoyo.
I was hungry so I ate rice.
고프다 (hungry) → 고파서 (bright vowel ㅗ, so 아서).
한국어가 재미있어서 매일 공부해요.
Hangugeoga jaemiisseoseo maeil gongbuhaeyo.
Because Korean is fun, I study every day.
재미있다 (fun) → 재미있어서. A sentence you can genuinely say by now!

Using -(아/어)서 for sequential actions:

카페에 가서 커피를 마셨어요.
Kape-e gaseo keopireul masyeosseoyo.
I went to a café and (then) drank coffee.
가다 (to go) → 가서 (going, and then~). The two actions are connected in sequence.
친구를 만나서 같이 밥 먹었어요.
Chingureul mannaseo gachi bap meogeosseoyo.
I met a friend and we ate together.
만나다 (to meet) → 만나서 (after meeting). Natural storytelling in Korean.
-(아/어)서 vs -(으)면 — don't mix them up: -(으)면 = "if/when" (conditional, future possibility). -(아/어)서 = "because/so" (cause already happened or is a fact). e.g. 비가 오면 택시 타세요 (If it rains, take a taxi) vs 비가 와서 택시를 탔어요 (Because it rained, I took a taxi). Context and tense will guide you.

Culture note
🍺
눈치 (nunchi) — Korea's unspoken social intelligence
눈치 (nunchi) is one of the most important concepts in Korean social life. Literally "eye measure", it refers to the ability to read the room — to sense what others are feeling or needing without them having to say it directly. Someone with 눈치 knows when to refill someone's glass, when to change the topic, when a gathering is winding down. 눈치가 없다 (nunchi-ga eopda = to have no nunchi) is a social failing. 눈치가 빠르다 (nunchi-ga ppareuda = to have quick nunchi) is a high compliment. As a visitor, demonstrating basic nunchi — pouring for others, noticing when someone is tired, saying goodbye at the right moment — will earn you enormous respect from Korean hosts.
