Lesson 1 · The four conditionals — from fact to impossible regret
If-clauses that span reality, possibility, hypothesis, and the unchangeable past

English conditionals express relationships between conditions and results. They fall into four main types, each with a specific structure and meaning — ranging from universal truths to impossible past regrets. Understanding which conditional to use is one of the clearest markers of English proficiency.

Zero conditional
Universal truths & general facts
If + present simple → present simple
Used for things that are always true — scientific facts, habits, and cause-and-effect that is certain every time.
If you heat water to 100°C, it boils.
If I don't sleep enough, I feel terrible.
If it rains, the streets get wet.
"When" can replace "if" in zero conditionals — the result is inevitable. "When you heat water..." is equally correct.
First conditional
Real & possible future conditions
If + present simple → will + base form
Used for situations that are genuinely possible or likely. The speaker believes the condition could realistically happen.
If it rains tomorrow, I will take an umbrella.
If you study hard, you will pass the exam.
If she calls, tell her I'm busy.
The if-clause uses present simple even though it refers to the future — never "If it will rain". The main clause uses will (or another modal: can, may, should).
Second conditional
Hypothetical present or unlikely future
If + past simple → would + base form
Used for imaginary, unlikely, or impossible present situations — and dreams. The speaker does NOT expect the condition to happen.
If I had more time, I would learn three languages.
If she lived in Tokyo, she would visit us more.
If I were you, I would accept the offer.
"If I were you" uses WERE for all persons in formal English (the subjunctive survives here). "If I was you" is common in casual speech. The result clause always uses would/could/might — never will.
Third conditional
Impossible past — regrets & what-ifs
If + past perfect → would have + past participle
Used for things that DIDN'T happen in the past. The condition is impossible to fulfil — history cannot be changed. Used for regrets and alternate histories.
If I had studied harder, I would have passed.
If she hadn't missed the train, she would have arrived on time.
If we had known, we would have helped.
This is the "regret tense" — it always refers to something that is now impossible to change. Could have / might have can replace would have for different nuances.
Mixed conditional
Past condition → present result (or vice versa)
If + past perfect → would + base form
When the condition and result belong to DIFFERENT times — typically a past condition with a present consequence, or a present state that would have changed a past result.
If I had studied medicine, I would be a doctor now.
(Past condition → present result)
If I were more organised, I would have finished on time.
(Present state → past result)
Mixed conditionals cross time boundaries — a mark of sophisticated English. The key is logical consistency: which time does each clause refer to?
The most common errors:
❌ "If it will rain..." → ✅ "If it rains..." (never will in the if-clause of first conditional)
❌ "If I would have money..." → ✅ "If I had money..." (never would in the if-clause)
❌ "If I had more time, I will..." → ✅ "If I had more time, I would..." (second conditional: would, not will)
The if-clause NEVER takes will or would — these appear only in the result clause.
Lesson 2 · Wish & If only — expressing regrets and desires
The grammar of longing — wishing things were different

Wish and if only follow the same tense patterns as conditionals — but express feelings about present dissatisfaction, past regrets, and desires about the future. "If only" is more emphatic and emotional than "wish".

Wish about present / future
wish / if only + past simple
Dissatisfaction with the present situation — wishing things were different NOW. Uses past simple (or were for all persons with "to be").
I wish I spoke more languages.
I wish I were taller. (not "was" in formal English)
If only I lived closer to the city.
She wishes she had more free time.
Wish about the past
wish / if only + past perfect
Regret about something that DID or DIDN'T happen in the past. Uses past perfect — one step further back than the wish about the present.
I wish I had studied harder at school.
If only we hadn't missed the train!
She wishes she had travelled more when she was young.
If only I had known about this sooner!
Wish + would — for annoying habits or refusing to change: There is a third wish pattern: wish + would + base form, used when you want someone (or something) to change their behaviour — often with irritation. "I wish you would stop making that noise!" · "I wish it would stop raining." · "I wish she would reply to my messages." This is NOT used for your own behaviour — "I wish I would study more" ❌ → "I wish I studied more" ✅.

Culture note
💭
Conditionals as a window into cultural attitudes
The way a culture uses conditionals reveals something about its relationship with possibility, regret, and the future. English's four-conditional system is unusually fine-grained — it distinguishes sharply between what is possible, what is hypothetical, and what is past and impossible. This grammatical precision reflects something broader: Anglo-American culture's emphasis on scenario planning, risk assessment, and counterfactual thinking ("what if we had done X instead?"). The third conditional — the grammar of regret and alternate histories — is particularly rich in literature, philosophy, and everyday introspection. Japanese, by contrast, tends to express hypotheticals with softer hedging language rather than grammatical mood shifts, reflecting a cultural preference for indirectness about uncertainty. Studying conditionals across languages reveals not just grammar, but how different peoples understand the relationship between actions and their consequences.

Lesson 2 · Ser vs Estar — advanced and subtle uses
Beyond permanent vs temporary — the cases that surprise even advanced learners

You learned the fundamental ser (permanent/identity) vs estar (temporary/state/location) distinction in Week 2. But many adjectives change meaning depending on which verb accompanies them — and some situations break the permanent/temporary rule entirely.

Adjectives that CHANGE MEANING with ser vs estar:

Adjective With SER → characteristic With ESTAR → state
aburrido es aburrido = he is a boring person está aburrido = he is bored (right now)
listo es listo = he is clever/smart está listo = he is ready
malo es malo = he is bad (wicked / of poor quality) está malo = he is ill / sick
bueno es bueno = he is good (virtuous, good quality) está bueno = it tastes good / (colloquial) he is attractive
muerto es un hombre muerto = it's a dead man (fact) está muerto = he is dead (state — always estar!)
vivo es muy vivo = he is very sharp/shrewd está vivo = he is alive (state)
rico es rico = he is rich (wealthy) está rico = it's delicious (food/drink)
seguro es seguro = it is safe (inherently safe — e.g. a car model) está seguro = he is certain / sure (feeling)
The "dead" rule — estar breaks the permanent/temporary logic: Death is permanent — yet Spanish says está muerto (estar). Why? Because estar describes the STATE of being dead, not an inherent quality. Spanish treats death as a condition of the body — just like illness. "El soldado caído es un hombre muerto" (The fallen soldier is a dead man — ser, defining a category) vs "El soldado está muerto" (The soldier is dead — estar, describing his current state). This is one of Spanish's deepest and most revealing distinctions.
The estar + adjective surprise — describing unexpected qualities: Normally sers describes inherent qualities. But usar estar with adjectives that usually go with ser emphasises surprise or a notable deviation from the norm: "¡Estás muy guapa hoy!" (You look really beautiful today! — normally not said/implied this way). "Este café está muy caro hoy." (This coffee is very expensive today — more expensive than expected). Estar with a "ser adjective" carries a surprise or temporary implication.
Lesson 3 · El se impersonal y pasivo — impersonal and passive se
Spain's elegant way of avoiding the passive — and speaking impersonally

Spanish uses se in several powerful constructions that avoid naming a specific subject. Where English uses the passive voice (is spoken, is sold) or impersonal "you/one/they" (you can park here, one must...), Spanish uses se + verb. It is one of the most frequently used constructions in everyday Spanish.

Impersonal se — "one / you / people"
se + 3rd person singular verb
Used when no specific subject is named — general, universal statements about what people do.
Se trabaja mucho en España. (People work a lot in Spain.)
Aquí se come muy bien. (You eat very well here.)
Se vive bien con ese sueldo. (One lives well on that salary.)
¿Cómo se dice "hello" en español? (How do you say "hello" in Spanish?)
Passive se — "it is done / are done"
se + verb (agrees with noun subject)
Used when the agent is unknown or irrelevant — verb agrees in number with the noun (not always singular!).
Se habla español aquí. (Spanish is spoken here.)
Se venden pisos. (Flats are sold / for sale. — plural verb!)
Se necesitan camareros. (Waiters needed. — plural!)
Se abre a las 9. (It opens at 9 / Opens at 9.)
Se + poder — "can be done"
se puede / se pueden + infinitive
Expressing what is allowed, possible or permitted — impersonally and politely.
¿Se puede aparcar aquí? (Can one park here? / Is parking allowed?)
No se puede fumar. (Smoking is not permitted.)
Aquí se pueden sacar fotos. (Photos can be taken here.)
¿Se puede pagar con tarjeta? (Can one pay by card?)
Se on signs & notices
se + verb (infinitive or conjugated)
Extremely common on Spanish signs, notices, advertisements and menus.
Se alquila. (For rent.)
Se vende. (For sale.)
Se busca. (Wanted.)
Se prohibe el paso. (No entry. / Access prohibited.)
Se ruega silencio. (Silence is requested. — formal)
The number agreement trap: In the passive se construction, the verb must agree in number with the noun that follows. Many learners forget this and use singular for everything. "Se habla español" ✅ (Spanish is spoken — singular). But "Se hablan tres idiomas" ✅ (Three languages are spoken — plural!). The test: ask what the subject is. If "languages" is the subject → plural verb. If there's no clear noun subject → singular. "Se vende casa" (House for sale) vs "Se venden pisos" (Flats for sale).

Culture note
💼
La cultura laboral española — working life in Spain
Spain's working culture has its own rhythm. The jornada partida (split working day) traditionally divides work into a morning block and an afternoon block with a long midday break — though the jornada continua (continuous day, finishing by 3pm) is increasingly common, especially in Madrid and among younger workers. Spanish work culture values relationships and informal communication — decisions are often made over coffee or lunch rather than in formal meetings. The concept of enchufe (lit. "plug") refers to connections or nepotism — knowing the right people. Labour protections in Spain are strong: the Estatuto de los Trabajadores (Workers' Statute) guarantees extensive rights including minimum holiday, severance pay, and restrictions on dismissal. The ERE (Expediente de Regulación de Empleo — collective redundancy procedure) is headline news whenever a major company invokes it. Understanding these concepts is essential for anyone navigating the Spanish professional world.

The "to be" complexity spectrum: English uses one verb for everything — "is" covers identity, location, state, and existence. Japanese uses three different verbs depending on whether the subject is a living creature or an inanimate object. Spanish splits into two (ser/estar) based on whether the quality is inherent or temporary/locational. Korean uses two forms. This reveals something profound: English speakers perceive "being" as a single unified concept, while Japanese speakers grammatically encode the animate/inanimate distinction, and Spanish speakers grammatically encode the permanent/temporary distinction. Three languages — three fundamentally different ontologies embedded in grammar.
