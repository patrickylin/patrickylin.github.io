Lesson 1 · Word order — Subject · Verb · Object
English is an SVO language — the order of words carries the meaning

English word order is strict. Unlike Japanese and Korean (which are SOV — Subject Object Verb), English places the verb immediately after the subject, and the object after the verb. Changing this order usually produces nonsense or a completely different meaning.

Subject
The cat
→
Verb
drinks
→
Object
milk
✅ The cat drinks milk. — Correct SVO order.
❌ Milk drinks the cat. — Wrong — now the milk is doing the drinking!
❌ The cat milk drinks. — Wrong — this is SOV (Japanese/Korean pattern) — not English!
The big difference from Japanese & Korean: In Japanese you say 猫はミルクを飲む — literally "cat milk drinks" (SOV). In Korean: 고양이가 우유를 마셔요 — also "cat milk drinks". English insists on the verb between subject and object. This single difference takes time to internalise for East Asian language speakers — but once it clicks, it feels completely natural.

Word order and questions — English inverts subject and verb:

Statement: She is a teacher. (Subject → Verb → Object)
Question: Is she a teacher? (Verb → Subject → Object — inverted!)
Statement: You speak English.
Question: Do you speak English? (Add "do" as a helper verb for most verbs)
Lesson 2 · Articles — a, an, the
The hardest concept for Japanese & Korean speakers — languages with no articles at all

Japanese and Korean have no equivalent of "a", "an", or "the". These tiny words are among the most frequent in English — and among the most difficult to master, because native speakers use them instinctively based on a complex set of rules about definiteness, countability, and shared knowledge.

Article Name When to use Examples
a Indefinite
(before consonant sound)		 First mention · one of many · any member of a group · before consonant sounds a cat · a book · a university (/juː/ starts with consonant sound!)
an Indefinite
(before vowel sound)		 Same as "a" — but used before vowel sounds only an apple · an hour (/aʊ/ — H is silent!) · an umbrella
the Definite Specific/known item · previously mentioned · unique things · superlatives the cat (we know which one) · the Sun · the best restaurant
— Zero article Plural generalisations · uncountable nouns · proper nouns · abstract ideas Cats are animals. · Water is life. · Tokyo is big. · Love is kind.
The a/an trap: It's the sound that matters, not the spelling. "An hour" — because H is silent and "hour" starts with the vowel sound /aʊ/. "A university" — because despite starting with U, it's pronounced /juː/ which starts with the consonant sound /j/. Always ask: what sound does the next word START with?
The definite article "the" — the hardest rule: Use "the" when both the speaker and listener know which specific thing is meant. "I saw a dog. The dog was big." — First mention: "a dog". Second mention: "the dog" (now we both know which dog). This shared-knowledge concept is entirely absent from Japanese and Korean — making it one of the most common article errors in learner English.
Lesson 3 · The verb "to be" — am / is / are
English's most essential verb — and its most irregular one

The verb "to be" is the most common verb in English — and the most irregular. It has three present tense forms (am, is, are) that must be memorised. It's used to describe identity, state, location, and much more.

Pronoun "To be" form Contracted Example
I am I'm I am a student. / I'm happy.
You are You're You are a teacher. / You're welcome.
He / She / It is He's / She's / It's She is from Spain. / It's cold today.
We are We're We are friends. / We're ready.
They are They're They are students. / They're here.
Negatives and questions with "to be":
Negative: add "not" after the verb → I am not tired. / She is not here. → Contracted: I'm not · She isn't · They aren't
Question: invert subject and verb → Are you a student? · Is she here? · Am I right?
"To be" is the only verb in English that doesn't need "do/does" to make a question — it inverts directly.

Greetings, introductions & essential social phrases

Hello / Hi / Hey
Formal → Informal
Greetings — from formal to very casual
Hello = neutral/polite · Hi = casual · Hey = very casual or to get attention. Time-of-day greetings: Good morning · Good afternoon · Good evening.
😊
How are you?
/haʊ ɑː juː/
Standard greeting question (not always a real question!)
In English, "How are you?" is often a greeting ritual — the expected answer is "Fine, thanks" or "Good, thanks" — not a detailed health report! This surprises many learners.
🙋
My name is [name]. / I'm [name].
Formal / Casual
Introducing yourself
"My name is Patrick" = formal. "I'm Patrick" = natural and casual. "Call me Patrick" = very relaxed. Notice: the verb "to be" appears in every version!
🌍
I'm from [country/city].
/aɪm frɒm/
Saying where you're from
Compare with Japanese: 〜から来ました (kara kimashita) · Spanish: Soy de [place]. English uses "from" as a preposition. No particle system like Japanese/Korean!
🤝
Nice to meet you.
/naɪs tə miːt juː/
Meeting someone for the first time
Full form: "It's nice to meet you." The "It's" is often dropped in speech. Response: "Nice to meet you too." Compare Japanese よろしくお願いします — English is far less ceremonial.
🙏
Thank you. / Thanks. / Cheers.
Formal → Casual → British
Expressing gratitude
Thank you = polite · Thanks = casual · Cheers = British casual. Response options: "You're welcome" · "No problem" · "Of course" · "Don't mention it" · "My pleasure".
🚪
Goodbye / Bye / See you later.
Formal → Casual
Farewells
Also: Take care · Have a good one · Catch you later · Talk soon. English farewells are relaxed compared to the ceremonial さようなら (sayōnara) or the warm 또 만나요 (tto mannayo).

Culture note
🫖
English politeness — indirect and understated
English politeness operates very differently from Japanese or Korean politeness systems. Where Japanese has explicit grammatical levels of formality (keigo), English relies on indirectness, softening language, and understatement. "Could you possibly help me?" is more polite than "Help me." "I was wondering if you might have a moment" is more polite still. British English is particularly famous for extreme understatement — "not bad" often means "excellent", "a bit difficult" can mean "completely impossible", and "I might pop round" is a firm commitment. Learning to read this indirectness is as important as learning the grammar itself.

Lesson 1 · Spanish word order — SVO with flexibility
Spanish is SVO like English — but with much more flexibility

Like English, Spanish is fundamentally SVO (Subject Verb Object). But Spanish has considerably more word order freedom because its verb endings show who is doing what — making the subject often redundant.

Subject
El gato
→
Verb
bebe
→
Object
leche
✅ El gato bebe leche. — The cat drinks milk. (SVO — most common)
✅ Bebe leche el gato. — Also correct — Spanish verbs carry the subject information!
✅ Bebo café. — I drink coffee. (Subject pronoun "yo" dropped — the verb ending -o tells you it's "I")
Subject pronouns are often dropped in Spanish! Because verb endings encode who is doing the action, Spanish speakers routinely omit subject pronouns: "Hablo español" = "I speak Spanish" — no "yo" needed. This is called pro-drop and it's a feature English doesn't have — you must always say "I speak", never just "Speak Spanish."
Lesson 2 · Noun gender — every noun is masculine or feminine
El / La — the single most important concept to understand about Spanish nouns

Every noun in Spanish has a grammatical gender — masculine or feminine. This isn't about biology (though it often correlates) — it's a grammatical category that affects the article, adjective, and pronoun that accompany every noun. Mastering gender is the foundation of Spanish grammar.

Masculine
el / un
Most nouns ending in -o are masculine. Also: male beings, days of the week, months, languages, mountains, rivers, oceans.
el libro (the book)
el gato (the cat)
el hombre (the man)
un coche (a car)
el lunes (Monday)
el español (Spanish language)
Feminine
la / una
Most nouns ending in -a are feminine. Also: female beings, letters of the alphabet, islands, countries ending in -a.
la casa (the house)
la gata (the female cat)
la mujer (the woman)
una mesa (a table)
la ciudad (the city)
España (Spain — feminine)
Exceptions and tricks: Not every -o word is masculine or every -a word feminine. El mapa (map) = masculine despite ending in -a. La mano (hand) = feminine despite ending in -o. Words of Greek origin ending in -ma are masculine: el problema, el programa, el tema, el idioma. When in doubt, learn the article alongside every new noun — always learn "el libro", not just "libro".

The four Spanish articles — a complete picture:

Definite (the)Indefinite (a/an)
Masculine singular el — el libro (the book) un — un libro (a book)
Feminine singular la — la casa (the house) una — una casa (a house)
Masculine plural los — los libros (the books) unos — unos libros (some books)
Feminine plural las — las casas (the houses) unas — unas casas (some houses)

Lesson 3 · Ser vs Estar — two verbs that both mean "to be"
The most famous challenge in Spanish — and one of the most fascinating

Where English has one verb "to be", Spanish has two: ser and estar. The choice between them changes the meaning dramatically. Learning when to use each is one of the central skills of Spanish grammar — and one that native English speakers find genuinely surprising.

SER
Permanent · Essential · Identity
👤 Identity: Soy Patrick. (I am Patrick.)
🌍 Origin: Soy de Japón. (I am from Japan.)
💼 Occupation: Eres médico. (You are a doctor.)
🕐 Time: Son las tres. (It is three o'clock.)
🎨 Inherent traits: El cielo es azul. (The sky is blue.)
📦 Material: La mesa es de madera. (The table is made of wood.)
Yo soy · Tú eres · Él/Ella es
Nosotros somos · Ellos son
ESTAR
Temporary · State · Location
📍 Location: Estoy en Tokio. (I am in Tokyo.)
😊 Temporary state: Estás cansado. (You are tired.)
🤒 Health: Ella está enferma. (She is sick.)
🏃 Ongoing action: Estoy comiendo. (I am eating.)
💭 Mood/feeling: Estamos felices. (We are happy.)
🔄 Changeable quality: El café está frío. (The coffee is cold.)
Yo estoy · Tú estás · Él/Ella está
Nosotros estamos · Ellos están
The classic ser/estar contrast: "Él es aburrido" (ser) = He is a boring person — a character trait. "Él está aburrido" (estar) = He is bored right now — a temporary state. Same person, same adjective, completely different meaning! The ser/estar distinction encodes whether a quality is seen as permanent or temporary — a deeply philosophical difference baked into the grammar.
Lesson 4 · Spanish greetings & introductions
Your first Spanish conversations — using ser, estar, and gender together
🌞
Buenos días / Buenas tardes / Buenas noches
/ˈbwenos ˈdias/ · /ˈbwenas ˈtaɾðes/ · /ˈbwenas ˈnotʃes/
Good morning / Good afternoon / Good evening
Notice: días (days) is masculine → buenos. But tardes (afternoons) and noches (nights) are feminine → buenas. Gender agreement in action from your very first phrases!
😊
¿Cómo estás? / ¿Cómo está usted?
Casual / Formal
How are you?
Uses ESTAR — because your current state/health is temporary! Response: Muy bien, gracias (Very well, thanks) · Bien (Good) · Más o menos (So-so) · Regular (Not great).
🙋
Me llamo [nombre]. / Soy [nombre].
/me ˈʎamo/ · /soi/
My name is [name]. / I am [name].
Me llamo = literally "I call myself" (a reflexive verb — Week 5!). Soy uses SER — because your name is a permanent identity. ¿Cómo te llamas? = What is your name? (casual)
🌍
Soy de [país/ciudad].
/soi ðe/
I am from [country/city].
Uses SER — origin is considered permanent. Soy de Japón · Soy de España · Soy de México. ¿De dónde eres? = Where are you from? (casual)
🤝
Mucho gusto. / Encantado/a.
/ˈmutʃo ˈɡusto/ · /enkanˈtaðo/
Nice to meet you. / Delighted to meet you.
Encantado = masculine speaker · Encantada = feminine speaker. Your first example of adjective gender agreement with the speaker! Response: Igualmente (Likewise) or El gusto es mío (The pleasure is mine).
📍
Estoy en [lugar].
/esˈtoi en/
I am in/at [place].
Uses ESTAR — location is always temporary (you could move!). Estoy en Tokio · Estoy en casa · Estoy en el aeropuerto. Compare: SER for origin, ESTAR for current location.
Culture note
💃
Tú vs Usted — formality in Spanish
Spanish has two ways to say "you" — tú (informal, singular) and usted (formal, singular). This distinction carries enormous social weight. Using tú with someone who expects usted can feel disrespectful; using usted with a close friend can feel cold and distancing. In Spain, the shift from usted to tú happens quickly — even in professional settings. In Latin America, usted is used much more broadly, even between friends in some countries (particularly Colombia). A safe rule: use usted with anyone older, more senior, or whom you've just met in a formal context, and follow their lead. Also note: ustedes (you all) is used across all registers in Latin America, while Spain uses vosotros for informal plural.

The great East-West divide: Japanese and Korean are SOV — the verb comes at the end. English and Spanish are SVO — the verb comes in the middle. This is one of the most fundamental typological differences in world languages. Interestingly, about 45% of the world's languages are SOV, and about 42% are SVO — so both patterns are roughly equally common globally!
