Lesson 1 · The present perfect — connecting past to present
Have/has + past participle — the tense that bridges then and now

The present perfect describes past events that have a connection to the present moment — either because the result is still relevant, because the time is not finished, or because the exact time is unknown or unimportant. It's formed with have/has + past participle.

Present Perfect
have/has + past participle
🔗 Past action, present result
⏳ Unfinished time period
🌍 Life experience (ever/never)
🆕 Very recent action (just)
❓ No specific time given
I have lost my keys. (result: I can't get in)
She has lived here for ten years. (still now)
Have you ever eaten sushi?
I've just finished the report.
Past Simple
verb-ed / irregular past
✅ Completed, no present link
📅 Specific time mentioned
🔚 Finished time period
📋 Sequence of events
I lost my keys yesterday. (specific time)
She lived in Seoul in 2019. (finished period)
Did you eat sushi last night?
I finished the report at 5pm.
The single most important rule: If a specific past time is mentioned (yesterday, last week, in 2019, at 6pm) — use past simple. The present perfect never goes with specific finished times. "I have seen that film yesterday." ❌ → "I saw that film yesterday." ✅ The word "yesterday" kills the present perfect every time.
Lesson 2 · Forming the present perfect
Have / has + past participle — and irregular past participles to memorise
PronounAuxiliaryContractedNegativeExample
I / You / We / They have 've haven't I've visited Japan twice.

Past participles — regular and key irregulars:

Regular (same as past simple): work→worked · play→played · visit→visited
Irregular — same as past simple: buy→bought · think→thought · bring→brought
Irregular — different from past simple:
go→went→gone · eat→ate→eaten · see→saw→seen · write→wrote→written
take→took→taken · speak→spoke→spoken · give→gave→given · do→did→done
be→was/were→been · know→knew→known · swim→swam→swum · fly→flew→flown
Been vs gone: "She has been to Japan." = She went and has come back. "She has gone to Japan." = She went and is still there — she's not here now. This been/gone distinction is unique to the present perfect and causes frequent confusion.
Lesson 3 · The present perfect's key adverbs
Ever · Never · Already · Yet · Just · For · Since — each triggers the perfect
ever
In questions — at any time in life. Always with present perfect.
Have you ever eaten durian?
never
Not at any time — negative experience.
I've never been to Brazil.
already
Sooner than expected — positive statement.
I've already seen that film.
yet
Up to now — questions and negatives only.
Have you eaten yet? · I haven't eaten yet.
just
Very recently — moments ago.
She's just arrived at the station.
for / since
Duration: for + period · since + start point.
I've lived here for five years / since 2019.
Already vs yet — position matters: Already goes between have and the participle, or at the end (positive): "I've already eaten." / "I've eaten already." Yet goes only at the end of questions and negatives: "Have you eaten yet?" / "I haven't eaten yet." Never: "I've yet eaten" ❌ or "Have you already eaten?" (implies surprise) — subtle but real.

Culture note
🇺🇸
British vs American present perfect — a genuine divide
British English uses the present perfect consistently for recent actions with present relevance: "I've just eaten." "Have you done your homework yet?" American English often uses the past simple in the same situations: "I just ate." "Did you do your homework yet?" Both are grammatically correct within their own variety — but mixing them can sound odd. "Did you eat yet?" is natural in New York; it sounds unusual in London. This is one of the clearest grammatical differences between the two major English varieties, and a useful reminder that "correct English" often depends on which English you're using. As a teacher of English, it's worth choosing a consistent standard and sticking to it — while acknowledging the other variety exists.

Lesson 2 · En el médico — at the doctor's
Describing symptoms, getting help, and the Spanish healthcare system
🚨
¡Llame a una ambulancia! / Necesito ayuda.
/ˈʎame a ˈuna ambuˈlanθja/
Call an ambulance! / I need help.
Emergency number in Spain: 112 (European standard) · 061 (medical emergencies). ¡Es una emergencia! = It's an emergency! ¡Auxilio! = Help! These are the phrases you hope never to need but must know.
🏥
Necesito ver a un médico. / ¿Dónde está el centro de salud?
/neθeˈsito ˈβeɾ a un ˈmeðiko/
I need to see a doctor. / Where is the health centre?
El centro de salud = GP/health centre. El hospital = hospital. Urgencias = A&E / Emergency Department. La farmacia = pharmacy. El médico de cabecera = GP (family doctor).
🤒
Me duele(n)... / Tengo dolor de...
/me ˈdwele/ · /ˈteŋɡo doˈloɾ ðe/
I have pain in... / I have a ... ache
Me duele + singular: Me duele la cabeza (headache). Me duelen + plural: Me duelen los pies (my feet hurt). Tengo dolor de muelas = toothache. Tengo fiebre = I have a fever.
💊
¿Puede darme algo para...? / ¿Tiene analgésicos?
/ˈpweðe ˈðaɾme ˈalɡo ˈpaɾa/
Can you give me something for...? / Do you have painkillers?
Un analgésico = painkiller. Un antiinflamatorio = anti-inflammatory. El ibuprofeno / el paracetamol = ibuprofen / paracetamol. La receta = prescription. Sin receta = over the counter (OTC).
🌡️
Soy alérgico/a a... / Tomo medicación para...
/soi aˈleɾxiko a/
I am allergic to... / I take medication for...
Soy alérgico/a a la penicilina (I am allergic to penicillin). Tomo medicación para la tensión (I take medication for blood pressure). Essential information to communicate clearly at any medical appointment.
📋
¿Desde cuándo le duele? / ¿Tiene seguro médico?
/ˈdesðe ˈkwando le ˈdwele/
How long has it been hurting? / Do you have health insurance?
Questions a Spanish doctor will ask you. Desde hace tres días (for three days). La tarjeta sanitaria = health card. El seguro médico privado = private health insurance. EU citizens can use the EHIC card in Spain.
Lesson 3 · The conditional tense — el condicional
"Would" in Spanish — hypotheticals, polite requests, and dreams

The Spanish conditional expresses what would happen — hypothetical situations, polite requests, and wishes. It uses the same irregular stems as the future tense, but different endings. The good news: regular verbs add the endings directly to the infinitive, just like the future!

Same irregular stems as the future! tener→tendr- (tendría) · salir→saldr- (saldría) · venir→vendr- (vendría) · poder→podr- (podría) · hacer→har- (haría) · decir→dir- (diría) · querer→querr- (querría). Once you know the future irregulars (Week 7), the conditional stems are free — same stems, different endings (-ía instead of -é/-ás/-á etc.).

Si + imperfect subjunctive →

The conditional as a politeness tool — its most practical use:

🎩
¿Podría ayudarme? / ¿Me traería la carta?
/poˈðɾia/ · /me tɾaˈeɾia/
Could you help me? / Could you bring me the menu?
Podría (conditional of poder) = could — far more polite than puede (present). This is exactly like English: "Can you help?" vs "Could you help?" — the conditional adds courtesy distance. Use it in restaurants, hotels, and formal contexts.
💭
Quisiera... / Me gustaría...
/kiˈsjeɾa/ · /me ɡustaˈɾia/
I would like... (polite)
Quisiera (conditional of querer) = I would like — the most elegant way to make a request. Me gustaría + infinitive = I would like to [do something]. Both are considerably more polite than quiero (I want) — the Spanish equivalent of "I'd like" vs "I want".
🌟
¿Qué harías si...? / Yo que tú...
/ke aˈɾias si/ · /ʝo ke ˈtu/
What would you do if...? / If I were you...
¿Qué harías si tuvieras un millón de euros? = What would you do if you had a million euros? Yo que tú, descansaría = If I were you, I would rest. Yo en tu lugar... = In your place... These are wonderful conversation starters.

Culture note
🏥
La sanidad española — Spain's healthcare system
Spain has one of the world's best public healthcare systems — consistently ranked in the top ten globally. The Sistema Nacional de Salud (SNS) provides universal free healthcare to all residents and EU citizens with an EHIC card. The first port of call for non-emergencies is the centro de salud (health centre), where your médico de cabecera (GP) can refer you to specialists. Pharmacies (farmacias — marked with a green cross) are remarkably accessible: Spanish pharmacists are highly trained and can recommend treatments for minor ailments without a prescription. They are the first stop for colds, minor injuries, and common complaints — a deeply embedded cultural habit. The word farmacia is cognate with "pharmacy" in English and "farmacia" in Italian — you'll spot the green cross instantly across Spain and Latin America.

The perfect tense insight: Japanese and Korean have no dedicated present perfect tense — they use their past tense and rely on context to show present relevance. English and Spanish both have dedicated perfect tenses (have + participle / haber + participio) — but use them differently: British English uses it for any recent past with present relevance, while in Latin America Spanish speakers often prefer the preterite. The grammar is similar but the usage habits diverge across cultures.
